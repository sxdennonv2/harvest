Excel to a High Standard · Lesson 0001

The $ that anchors your formula

Tied to your mission: turning a SQL extract into a clean summary means dragging one formula across hundreds of rows. Get references right and it just works. Get them wrong and the numbers quietly drift — the most common silent error in analyst workbooks.

Practice workbook · open this first

Open the course workbook and click the References tab — the products, case costs and a rate cell in F1 are set up and waiting. The Landed cost column is left empty on purpose: that's your task.

The one idea

A cell reference like B2 is not really an address — it's a direction. When you copy a formula, Excel keeps the direction, not the address. Copy =B2 one cell to the right and it becomes =C2; copy it one row down and it becomes =B3. That "moving with you" behaviour is a relative reference, and it's the default.

A dollar sign $ pins a part of the reference so it stops moving. That's an absolute reference. You can pin the column, the row, or both:

You typeColumnRowName
B2movesmovesrelative
$B$2lockedlockedabsolute
$B2lockedmovesmixed
B$2moveslockedmixed

Why it matters to a buyer

You have a table of case costs in column B, and a single exchange-rate cell in F1. You want landed cost per line:

=B2*$F$1

The $F$1 is pinned, so when you fill the formula down the whole column, every row still multiplies by that one rate cell. Without the dollars, =B2*F1 becomes =B3*F2 on the next row — and F2 is empty, so your landed cost silently collapses to zero or nonsense.

The bad habit this undoes Self-taught users often retype the rate into every row, or fix the drift by hand after filling. Both are slow and error-prone. One $F$1 and a single fill-down replaces all of it — and updates instantly when the rate changes.

The keyboard trick: F4

You don't type the dollar signs by hand. Click into a reference in the formula bar (or just after typing it) and press F4 to cycle the lock states:

B2 → F4 → $B$2 → F4 → B$2 → F4 → $B2 → F4 → back to B2.

Mental shortcut: the $ sits in front of whatever it locks. $B2 = "lock the column B, let the row roam". B$2 = "let the column roam, lock row 2".

Try it for real

Open a workbook. Put any number in F1, a short column of numbers in B2:B6, and in C2 type =B2*, click F1, press F4, press Enter, then fill C2 down to C6. Change F1 and watch the whole column recalculate. That muscle memory is the goal.

The point of the ladder The task never changed — landed cost from a case cost and one rate. What changed is how much AI did. And at every rung the thing that keeps you safe is identical: you can read the formula and know whether the rate is locked. That is why the craft comes first.
