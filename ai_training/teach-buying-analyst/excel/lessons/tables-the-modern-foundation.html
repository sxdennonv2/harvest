Excel to a High Standard · Lesson 0002

Tables: the modern foundation

Tied to your mission: you paste a SQL extract and need to summarise it fast — and the extract is a different length every time. An Excel Table is the single feature that makes that robust: it grows by itself, fills formulas down for you, and feeds pivots and charts that expand automatically.

Practice workbook · open this first

Open the course workbook and click the Tables tab — a plain block of products and case costs with a rate cell named Rate is waiting. It is deliberately not a Table yet: turning it into one is your task.

The one idea

A plain block of cells is just that — Excel doesn't know where it starts or ends. Press Ctrl+T and that block becomes a Table: a named, self-describing object with a header row, banded rows, filter arrows, and — crucially — the ability to grow. Add a row at the bottom and the Table absorbs it; every formula and the chart built on it update with no effort from you.

Tables also change how formulas read. Instead of =B2*C2 you write =[@[Case cost]]*[@Rate] — a structured reference that names the columns. It's self-documenting, and Excel fills it down the whole column the instant you press Enter.

Why it matters to a buyer

Your extract is 480 rows today and 512 next week. With a loose range you'd hardcode B2:B480 and silently miss 32 rows next time. As a Table, the range is the data — no fixed boundary to get wrong. Add a landed-cost column once and it fills all rows; paste next week's longer extract and everything just extends.

The bad habit this undoes Dragging formulas down by hand, re-selecting B2:B480 ranges that break when the data changes length, and reading cryptic =B2*C2 formulas months later. A Table makes the range self-adjusting and the formula self-explaining.

How to do it

Click anywhere in your data → press Ctrl+T → confirm My table has headers. On the Table Design tab, give it a real name (e.g. Products). Type a formula in a new column using a structured reference and watch it fill down. Turn on Table Design → Total Row for instant sums and averages.

Builds on References: inside a Table you rarely need $ for columns — the structured reference handles the row-by-row part. You still lock a single outside cell (like a rate) with $F$1 or, better, give it a name.

Try it for real

Type a few products and case costs, click inside, press Ctrl+T. Name it Products on the Table Design tab. Add a Landed cost column with a structured reference to your rate cell — it fills down instantly. Turn on the Total Row. Now add a new product on the row below the Table and watch it swallow the row and extend every formula.
