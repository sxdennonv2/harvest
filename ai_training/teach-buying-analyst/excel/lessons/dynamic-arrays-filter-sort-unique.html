Excel to a High Standard · Lesson 0013

Dynamic arrays: FILTER, SORT & UNIQUE

Tied to your mission: a PivotTable summarises, but sometimes you want a live list — the distinct categories, or just the Snacks lines, refreshing themselves as the data changes. UNIQUE, SORT and FILTER are formulas that return a whole range at once — they "spill" — so one formula builds a list that used to need a pivot or a manual copy.

Practice workbook · open this first

Open the course workbook and click the Dynamic arrays tab — a product list with categories and revenue sits ready for a spilled list of unique categories and a filtered view of one of them.

The one idea

A dynamic array formula returns many results from a single cell and spills them into the cells below and right — you type it once, in the top-left cell, and Excel fills the rest. UNIQUE strips duplicates: =UNIQUE(A2:A15). Wrap it in SORT for an ordered list: =SORT(UNIQUE(A2:A15)).

FILTER returns only the rows that meet a test: =FILTER(B2:C15, A2:A15="Snacks") spills every Snacks product and its revenue. If a spill is blocked by something in the way, you'll see #SPILL! — clear the cells below and it fills. Point another formula at the top cell with a # — =COUNTA(E2#) — to count the whole spilled range however long it grows.

Why it matters to a buyer

A spilled SORT(UNIQUE(...)) is a category list that never goes stale — add a new category to the data and it appears, in order, with no maintenance. A FILTER is a live "just show me the Snacks" view without touching the source. These are the building blocks of a self-updating summary sheet.

The bad habit this undoes Copying a column, using Remove Duplicates, then sorting — a one-off snapshot that's wrong the next time the data changes. And filtering the source in place to "just look at Snacks", which hides the other rows from everyone else. Spill a formula instead and the list maintains itself.

How to do it

On the Dynamic arrays tab, in E2 type =SORT(UNIQUE(A2:A15)) and press Enter — a clean, ordered list of categories spills down from that one cell. In G2 type =FILTER(B2:C15, A2:A15="Snacks") — every Snacks product and its revenue spills into G and H. Don't fill these down; the single formula does it all. Change a category in the data and watch both lists update.

Builds on tables: point these formulas at a Table's columns instead of A2:A15 and the spill grows and shrinks with the table automatically — the structured range from the tables lesson doing the range-management for you.
