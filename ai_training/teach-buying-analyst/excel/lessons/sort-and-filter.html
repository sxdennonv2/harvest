Excel to a High Standard · Lesson 0003

Sort & Filter: order and isolate

Tied to your mission: a pasted extract lands as hundreds of rows in no useful order. Before you summarise anything you need to see it — the biggest lines first, or just one supplier at a time. Sort orders the list; Filter hides everything that isn't relevant right now. Together they turn a wall of rows into an answerable question.

Practice workbook · open this first

Open the course workbook and click the Sort & Filter tab — the flat sales list is waiting, deliberately in raw entry order so you can put it in the order that answers a question.

The one idea

Turn on the filter arrows once — click any cell in the list and press Ctrl+Shift+L (or Data ▸ Filter). Every header grows a dropdown. From there you sort (order the whole list by one column) or filter (tick only the values you want to keep in view). Filtering never deletes a thing — it hides rows, and clearing the filter brings them all back.

Sorting can go several levels deep: Data ▸ Sort lets you order by Category first, then by Revenue within each category. That's the difference between "sorted" and "sorted the way the question needs".

Why it matters to a buyer

"Which ten lines make most of my revenue?" and "show me only Coastal Co" are questions you ask every week. Sort answers the first in one click; filter answers the second without you deleting rows you'll need again tomorrow. It's the fastest way to interrogate an extract before you build anything on top of it.

The bad habit this undoes Deleting the rows you don't currently care about to "tidy" the view — then having to re-paste the whole extract when you need them back. Filtering hides without destroying; the data stays whole.

How to do it

On the Sort & Filter tab, click any cell in the list and press Ctrl+Shift+L. Open the Revenue (AUD) dropdown and choose Sort Largest to Smallest — the top lines rise to the top. Now open the Category dropdown, untick Select All, tick only Snacks, and the list collapses to Snacks alone. Clear it with the funnel-with-a-cross icon on the Data tab.

Builds on Tables: if you first made this a Table (a previous lesson), the filter arrows are already there — sorting and filtering are simply part of what a Table gives you for free.
