Excel to a High Standard · Lesson 0012

PivotTables: summarise in seconds

Tied to your mission: you have a long extract of sales lines and your director wants the numbers by category, then by supplier, then by month — now. A PivotTable answers all three in a few drags, and re-answers them the instant the question changes. No formulas to write, nothing to break.

Practice workbook · open this first

Open the course workbook and click the PivotTable tab — a flat list of sales lines (month, category, supplier, product, cases, revenue) is set up and waiting. Summarising it is your task.

The one idea

A PivotTable is a drag-to-summarise engine. You hand it a flat list — one row per transaction — and it groups, totals and averages any way you like, just by dragging field names into four boxes: Rows, Columns, Values and Filters. Change your mind and you drag again; the answer rebuilds in an instant.

Put Category in Rows and Revenue (AUD) in Values and you have revenue per category. Drop Supplier under Category and it breaks each category down by supplier. That is a report you would otherwise build with a wall of SUMIFS — done in three drags.

Why it matters to a buyer

Your extract is a transaction list, not a summary. The questions that land on your desk are always aggregations: which category is growing, which supplier we lean on most, how each month compares. A PivotTable turns one flat list into all of those views without touching a formula — and because it sits on a Table (see Tables: the modern foundation), a longer extract next week just needs a Refresh.

The bad habit this undoes Hand-filtering the list, copying visible rows out, and typing =SUM() under each group — then redoing all of it when the data changes or the question shifts from category to supplier. A PivotTable makes the regrouping free and the totals self-updating.

How to do it

Click any cell in the data → Insert → PivotTable → confirm the range (or, better, that it's a Table) and place it on a new sheet. In the field list, drag Category to Rows and both Cases and Revenue (AUD) to Values. Now drag Supplier under Category in Rows for the breakdown, or Month to Columns to compare across months.

Builds on Tables: if your source is a real Table, a longer extract next week is one PivotTable Analyze → Refresh away — the pivot already knows the data can grow.

Try it for real

On the PivotTable tab, click inside the data and press Insert → PivotTable. Drag Category to Rows, then Cases and Revenue (AUD) to Values. Read off revenue per category. Now drag Supplier beneath Category, and Month to Columns. Watch three different reports appear from the same list, with no formula typed.
