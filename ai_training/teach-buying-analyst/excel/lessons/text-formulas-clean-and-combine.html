Excel to a High Standard · Lesson 0005

Text formulas: clean & combine

Tied to your mission: half of every supplier extract lands as messy text — a name with stray spaces, a code buried in a longer string, a label you need to stitch together. Cleaning it by hand is slow and it drifts. TRIM, PROPER, LEFT and the join operator & turn that chore into a formula you fill down once and trust.

Practice workbook · open this first

Open the course workbook and click the Text formulas tab — a raw extract with untidy product names and supplier codes sits ready to be cleaned and combined into a neat label.

The one idea

Text functions read a string and hand back a tidier one, without you retyping anything. TRIM strips the stray spaces: =TRIM(A2). PROPER fixes the casing: =PROPER("corn puffs") gives Corn Puffs (UPPER and LOWER do the blunt versions). LEFT, RIGHT and MID slice out part of a string: =LEFT(B2, 2) takes the first two characters.

The ampersand & joins strings together: =D2 & " (" & E2 & ")" builds Corn Puffs (HL). Newer builds add TEXTSPLIT, TEXTBEFORE and SUBSTITUTE for splitting on a delimiter and swapping characters — reach for those when a code is wrapped in punctuation.

Why it matters to a buyer

Clean text is what lets everything else line up: a SUMIFS only totals correctly when "Snacks" and "Snacks " (with a trailing space) aren't treated as two categories, and an XLOOKUP only matches when the key is spelt the same on both sides. Cleaning at the source, with a formula, means the fix survives the next refresh instead of being retyped every month.

The bad habit this undoes Hand-editing product names cell by cell to fix casing and spaces — invisible trailing spaces stay behind, break your lookups, and come back the moment a new extract lands. Clean it with a formula that reruns itself.

How to do it

On the Text formulas tab, in D2 type =PROPER(TRIM(A2)) and fill down — every name comes back trimmed and correctly cased. In E2 type =LEFT(B2, 2) to pull the two-letter supplier prefix out of the code. In F2 type =D2 & " (" & E2 & ")" to stitch the clean name and prefix into one label. Fill down and you have a tidy column built entirely from the messy originals.

Builds on references: these formulas point at the raw columns with relative references, so filling down walks each row onto its own source cell — exactly the behaviour the references lesson set up.
