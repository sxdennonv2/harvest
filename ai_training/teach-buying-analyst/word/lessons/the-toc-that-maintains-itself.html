Word to a High Standard · Lesson 0004

The table of contents that maintains itself

Tied to your mission: a GDM submission gets read by people who jump straight to the section they care about, and the table of contents is how they get there. A wrong page number in front of a director doesn't read as a typo — it reads as a document nobody proofed. This lesson makes sure the page numbers in your contents page are never something you typed, so they're never something you forgot to fix.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario B — The fighting page numbers: the same range-review submission, whose table of contents was typed by hand in March and has been quietly lying ever since.

The one idea

Never type a table of contents. A real one is a field — a query over your document's heading styles, generated once and refreshed on demand. It is never edited directly, the same way you'd never hand-edit a formula's calculated result instead of the formula. Builds on The heading hierarchy: the field has nothing to query if your headings aren't real Heading 1/2 styles — a typed-bold line is invisible to it.

Typing a contents page by hand creates a second copy of the document's structure, sitting apart from the actual structure the headings describe — and two copies of anything diverge. That's exactly why the March table of contents lies: page 9 became page 11 sometime in April, the body moved on, and the typed list never heard about it. The discipline that fixes this for good: a wrong entry is never a contents-page problem, it's a body problem wearing a contents-page symptom. Fix the heading, then refresh the field — never edit the rendered entry.

Why it matters to a buyer

A range-review submission goes through drafts across weeks — sections get added, a subsection gets promoted, an annex grows by four pages. Every one of those edits shifts page numbers below it. A director or GDM reader opening your contents page to "Supplier scorecards, page 9" and finding section 4 instead doesn't conclude the document moved — they conclude you didn't check your own work before it left your outbox. A field-based contents page absorbs every one of those edits automatically; a typed one accumulates a lie for every edit since the day you typed it.

The bad habit this undoes Typing a contents page from what you can see in the document right now, then hand-adjusting the page numbers whenever something "obviously" moved — which only catches the moves you happened to notice. Its subtler cousin is just as costly: having a real, field-generated table of contents, then clicking into its text and retyping a wrong-sounding entry by hand. That edit isn't wrong for long — it's wrong the moment someone next presses F9, because the field regenerates from the headings and silently erases the hand-typed "fix", taking it with no warning and no memory that you ever made it.

How to do it

Open Scenario B's repaired document (the one already carrying real Heading 1/2 styles from the heading-hierarchy lesson). First, get rid of the lie: click into the March table of contents and delete it entirely — select the whole block and press Delete, not just the numbers.

Now build the real one. Put your cursor where the contents page belongs, open the References tab, and choose Table of Contents → an Automatic Table style. Word walks every paragraph carrying a Heading style, reads its outline level and current page, and lays out the field in front of you — you did not type a single page number.

Prove it's alive before you trust it. Scroll into the body, add a new Heading 2 paragraph — anything, even a placeholder title — a page or two above an existing entry. Click back into the table of contents (it greys to show it's a field), right-click, and choose Update Field → Update entire table (or select it and press F9). Watch your new heading appear with the correct page number, and every entry below it renumber without you touching one of them.

Now run the source-fix drill, because this is the habit that actually sticks. Say an entry reads "Supplier Scorcards" — a misworded heading, not a contents-page typo. Do not click into the table of contents and correct the spelling there. Find that heading in the body, fix the word on the heading itself, then select the table of contents and F9 again. The correction appears in the field because you fixed it at the only place a fix is allowed to live.

Try it for real

On Scenario B's document: delete the typed March table of contents, insert a real one from References → Table of Contents, then add one new heading anywhere in the body and update the field. Self-check: every entry's page number must match the page the heading actually falls on when you scroll to it — not "close enough", an exact match, because that's the whole point of the field querying instead of you typing.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — a table of contents that reflects the document's real heading structure, not a remembered one — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: every fix must land in the body or the field settings, never in the rendered contents-page text.

Rung 2 · Aldi:GPT — it diffs your two lists, it can't touch either one

Aldi:GPT can't see your document, so hand it both lists as text: what your typed table of contents currently says, and what your heading styles actually are. It can spot the divergence; it cannot fix a single character of your file.

Type this prompt into Aldi:GPT

Check: the signature failure at this rung is Aldi:GPT prescribing an edit to the table-of-contents text — "change that entry's page number to 11". That's the wrong-copy instinct again, just handed to an AI. Every fix it names must point at the body (a heading's wording) or at the field settings (levels included, style applied) — never at the rendered entries themselves.

Rung 3 · M365 Copilot — the honest boundary: this is a native Word feature

Inserting and updating a table of contents is not a Copilot capability at all — it's the References tab, native to Word, and has been for decades. Copilot's actual move here is summarising what the document's structure currently looks like, or drafting a section you then apply a heading style to yourself.

Type this prompt into Copilot (in Word)

Check: any structural claim Copilot makes about the document — "this document has six main sections", "the annex starts at Heading 1" — verify against the nav pane yourself, not against the old typed table of contents. Copilot summarises text; it doesn't know your typed TOC was ever wrong, so it can repeat the same lie back to you if you feed it that instead of the real structure.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — it reports divergence, it never rewrites the field

An agent with file access can open the .docx, walk the heading tree, read the table-of-contents field's own stored entries, and report where they disagree — but it must treat the rendered table of contents as read-only output, never a place to write a fix.

Type this prompt into GitHub Copilot

Check: run it against the March-TOC specimen first — it must report the lies (the missing entries, the page drift, the reworded heading). Then run it against your regenerated document — it must report zero divergence. If it ever proposes rewriting a TOC entry directly instead of pointing at the body or the field settings, the script has broken the one rule that matters here and needs fixing before you trust its reports.

The point of the ladder From "diff two pasted lists" to "reconcile the file's own structures" — the deliverable never changes: a table of contents that is a live query, not a typed guess. What protects you at every rung is the same rule — fixes land in the body or the field settings, never in the rendered entries — because that rule is what a field is. Learn the References-tab craft first, so an AI proposing "just edit that entry" reads to you as the bad habit it is, not a shortcut.
