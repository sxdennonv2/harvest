PowerPoint to a High Standard · Lesson 0010

Export & share: PDF, handouts, the deck that travels

Tied to your mission: the survey's PPT complaints weren't only about decks looking right in the room — they were about decks that arrive broken somewhere else. This lesson is the last mile: rehearsing well (the previous lesson) gets the room ready; this one gets the file ready for everyone who isn't in the room, or isn't looking at it live. A deck that works perfectly on your laptop and falls apart on someone else's hasn't finished the job.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario E — The 10am presentation — your range review, 15 minutes in the GDM room, then the deck has to go to the attendees and to two people who weren't there, with the correct last mile given underneath so you can mark your own export set.

The one idea

The deck that travels is not the deck you edit. Your working .pptx carries live links back to the workbook, your fonts (which may not exist on the next machine), and your speaker notes — the cues, the "push back if they mention price" asides written for you alone. The audience needs an export, chosen per audience: a PDF when fidelity matters more than editability — fonts can't break, notes can't leak, nothing can be overtyped; notes pages for the people who weren't in the room and need the cues, not just the slides; the .pptx itself only when the receiver genuinely has to edit it — and then only with fonts embedded and links severed or shipped alongside.

The pre-flight question, every time, before a file leaves your outbox: what happens to this file on a machine that isn't yours?

Why it matters to a buyer

Your range review goes to people in three different relationships to the room: the attendees who were there and just want a copy, the two absentees who need the cues you spoke aloud but never typed onto the slide, and — this cycle — an external supplier who should see the numbers and nothing else. One file cannot correctly serve all three. Sending the working .pptx to everyone is the fast option and the wrong one: the external supplier opens your speaker notes along with the slides, the absentee's machine substitutes a font and the layout you rehearsed no longer matches what they read, and a linked chart — perfect on your laptop, tied to a workbook the receiver doesn't have — either shows stale numbers or throws an update-links prompt nobody asked for.

The bad habit this undoes Emailing the working .pptx to everyone because it's the file you already have open. The external supplier gets your speaker notes — including the line that says push back if they mention price — sitting one click away in Normal view. The absentee gets broken fonts and a slide that reflows differently from the one you presented. And the linked chart, which updated beautifully in the room because your laptop had the workbook open, dies quietly on a machine that has never seen it — showing whatever numbers happened to be baked in at last save, with no error to tell anyone it's stale.

How to do it

Build Scenario E's export set — three files, three audiences, one deck.

1. PDF for the room and the externals. File > Export > Create PDF/XPS Document. This is the fidelity export: fonts are outlined into the file so they can't break on another machine, nothing in it can be overtyped, and — critically for this cycle — speaker notes do not travel with a standard slides-only PDF export. Name it so the receiver can file it on sight: RangeReview_Q3_ForRoom.pdf, not final_v3_FINAL.pptx.

2. Notes pages for the two absentees. Still under Export > Create PDF/XPS, open Options and set Publish what: Notes Pages before exporting. This prints each slide with its speaker notes beneath it — the cues you rehearsed aloud, now readable by someone who wasn't in the room. Export this as a second, separately named file: RangeReview_Q3_NotesForAbsentees.pdf. Never hand this version to the external supplier — the notes are exactly what they must not see.

3. Check the notes before either export leaves. Open the Notes pane (or Notes Page view) and read every slide's notes end to end. This is the same quotability discipline as send-mechanics in Outlook crossing into decks: a note written for your eyes only — "push back if they mention price", a candid aside about a supplier — must not leave the building attached to a slide. Delete or rewrite anything that fails this test before you export the notes-pages PDF, and confirm the room/externals PDF genuinely carries none of it.

4. Verify the linked chart froze correctly. Open the exported room/externals PDF and find the slide carrying the linked revenue chart (the one built with paste-link, not a screenshot — see Excel to slide: paste-link, never screenshot). Confirm it shows the numbers as they stood when you exported — a PDF is a snapshot, so once it's out, it can never go stale in front of the reader the way a live link can; it's simply frozen at today's values. That's the whole point of choosing PDF for people who don't have your workbook.

5. Only ship the .pptx itself if someone must edit it — and if you do, use File > Options > Save > Embed fonts in the file first, and either update the linked chart to a static picture or send the workbook alongside it, because a receiver without your workbook and without embedded fonts inherits both failure modes at once.

Try it for real

Take a real deck of your own that's due to go to a mixed audience — some attendees, some absentees, maybe an external party — and build its export set: a fidelity PDF for the room and any externals, a notes-pages PDF for the absentees only, both separately and sensibly named. Self-check: read every slide's notes before either export and confirm nothing in them would embarrass you if the wrong person opened it; open the fidelity PDF and confirm any linked chart shows today's numbers, frozen; and confirm you did not attach the working .pptx to anyone who didn't need to edit it. If you can name which file went to which audience and why, you've passed.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — build the right export for each audience in Scenario E — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: every row of the plan must name the specific failure it prevents — notes leak, font break, or dead link — and one export does not fit every audience.

Rung 2 · Aldi:GPT — it prescribes the matrix, you build the files

Aldi:GPT can't see your deck, so it can only reason from the audiences you describe; it hands back an export matrix and the pre-flight checks, and you go and build each file yourself.

Type this prompt into Aldi:GPT

Check: the signature failure at this rung is one-export-fits-all — an answer that just says "send them the PDF" for every row without distinguishing the audiences. A good answer names a different export (or the same export with a different notes setting) per audience, and every row states the specific failure it prevents: notes leak, font break, or dead link. If any row is missing its named failure, the answer hasn't actually reasoned about the audience — it's guessed.

Rung 3 · Microsoft 365 Copilot — it drafts the email and reads your notes, it doesn't export

Copilot's honest last-mile move here is narrow: it can summarise the deck for the covering email, and it can read your speaker notes back to you so you can judge what must not leave — but the actual export buttons remain native PowerPoint. Don't attribute Export-to-PDF to Copilot; it doesn't do the exporting.

Type this prompt into Copilot (in PowerPoint, with the deck open)

Check: Copilot reads what's already there — it can flag a note that looks leakable and draft the email around the deck, but it cannot export the PDF, cannot switch the Publish setting to Notes Pages, and cannot embed fonts; those remain File > Export actions you perform yourself in native PowerPoint. If a flagged note turns out to be exactly the "push back if they mention price" line, that confirms the read was useful — it does not mean Copilot did the stripping. You still open the exported PDF afterwards and confirm the note is actually gone.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — it builds the export pack script, it can't verify its own output

An agent with file access can build a script that emits the whole export pack from the .pptx — but the manifest it writes is only a claim until you open the files yourself and check it's true.

Type this prompt into GitHub Copilot

Check: the manifest must be truthful, and truth is verified by opening the files, not by reading the script's claims. Open the _ForRoom.pdf and confirm the notes pane content is genuinely absent — not just hidden from the default view. Open the _NotesForAbsentees.pdf and confirm the notes actually printed. An export pack whose manifest claims it stripped the notes when they're still readable in the PDF is the naked-send bug from the Outlook attachments lesson at deck scale — a file that looks finished and isn't, and you won't find out until the wrong reader opens it.

The point of the ladder From "AI prescribes the matrix" to "AI builds the export pack by script" — the deliverable never changes: the right export for each audience, and a stated reason for every choice. What protects you at every rung is opening the actual output file and checking it against the claim, never the claim alone. Craft the export by hand first, so you know what "notes actually stripped" looks like before you trust a script's manifest to say so.
