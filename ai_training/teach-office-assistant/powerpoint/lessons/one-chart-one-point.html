PowerPoint to a High Standard · Lesson 0006

One chart, one point

Tied to your mission: professional polish on everything that goes out under the department's name doesn't stop at alignment and templates — it reaches the charts themselves. A workbook chart and a slide chart look like the same object, but they aren't doing the same job. This lesson is the discipline that keeps a chart from undoing everything the rest of the deck got right: a tidy, on-template slide with a chart nobody can read in the three seconds they'll actually give it.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario B — The screenshot deck — the monthly review deck's linked revenue chart, carrying a second series and a legend, waiting to be stripped to the one comparison it should be making.

The one idea

A workbook chart and a slide chart are not the same object wearing different clothes. In the workbook, a chart explores — many series, full axes, gridlines everywhere, built for you to sit with it at arm's length and dig. On a slide, a chart gets three seconds and exactly one job: make ONE comparison land, with everything else stripped away — extra series gone, gridlines light, the legend deleted if there's only one series to name, axis labels kept only where the eye actually needs them.

The test is the title-cover test: cover the slide's title with your hand. If a stranger can still say, in three seconds, which bar or line matters and versus what, the chart has earned its place on the slide. If the chart needs studying to answer that, it isn't a slide chart yet — it belongs in the appendix workbook, where exploring is exactly what it's for.

Why it matters to an office assistant

The monthly category review runs on the same chart twice: once in the workbook, where the numbers were built and checked against last month, all six series visible so nothing surprises anyone later; and once on the slide, in front of a director who is reading forty other things that hour. Those two audiences don't need the same chart. One needs everything. The other needs one number to beat another number, instantly — and getting that handoff right, every cycle, is exactly the kind of consistency the department's decks and training material need from you.

The decks that get read fastest aren't the ones with the most complete charts — they're the ones whose slide chart makes its point before the presenter has finished the first sentence of commentary. A director who has to lean in and squint at a legend has already stopped listening to what's being said, and that reflects on whoever built the deck.

The bad habit this undoes Lifting the working chart straight out of the workbook and pasting it onto the slide because it was already made — six series, two axes, a legend like a phone book, gridlines dense enough to read as a texture. That's the analyst's instrument landing on the slide, not the analyst's finding. Every series you didn't strip is a series the audience now has to rule out themselves before they can find the one that matters.

How to do it

Open Scenario B's linked revenue chart on the slide. It currently carries two series — revenue by category for this month, and a second series (last year's same-month figures) riding along as "context." The one comparison this slide needs to make is revenue by category, this month, full stop — so the first move is deciding what survives: one series, not two. Click the second series and delete it. If the comparison you actually need is this-month-versus-last-year, that's a different, legitimate one-comparison chart — but it still means picking one, not keeping both because both exist.

With one series left, the legend is now telling the audience "this is revenue" on a chart that has no other series it could be — delete it; a single-series chart names itself in the title. Next, lighten the gridlines: select them and either reduce their weight or set them to a pale grey, enough to help a reader align a bar to its label, not enough to read as a grid. Check the axis labels — keep only what the eye needs to place the value (the category names, one revenue scale), and remove any label that duplicates what the title or a data label already says.

Finally, resize the chart to dominate the slide — this is the one thing on it that matters, so it should read that way at a glance, not compete for space with a caption or a logo. Now run the cover-the-title test: put your hand over the slide's title and look only at the chart. If you can say, in three seconds, "snacks is highest, beverages is lowest, this month," it has passed. If you have to trace an axis or hunt a legend to answer that, go back and strip more.

Try it for real

Take a chart from one of your own working workbooks — the one with the most series, the one you'd hesitate to put on a slide as-is — and rebuild it as a slide chart. Name the ONE comparison it must make before you touch anything. Then strip: every series that isn't part of that comparison, gone; gridlines lightened; the legend deleted if only one series remains; axis labels cut to the ones the eye needs. Self-check: cover the slide's title with your hand and see if you (or, better, someone who's never seen the data) can say the one comparison in three seconds. If you can't, something you kept still needs to go.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — turn a workbook chart into a slide chart that makes exactly one comparison — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: cover the title and count what survives; anything that isn't part of the one comparison is a candidate for the appendix workbook, not the slide.

Rung 3 · M365 Copilot — it can build a slide chart from data, it won't know your one point unless you say it

Copilot in PowerPoint can generate a chart onto a slide from data you give it, but it doesn't know on its own that a slide gets one comparison and a workbook gets many — left to its own judgment it defaults toward "showing everything."

Copilot loves company — if the data file has other columns available (last year, forecast, category targets), it may add a second series unprompted because more data "looks more complete." More than the one comparison you asked for means re-prompt and restate the single-series constraint explicitly; don't hand-fix it by deleting a series Copilot keeps re-adding on the next regeneration.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — it can enforce the rule by code, but only if you make it refuse ambiguity

An agent with file access can build a python-pptx chart generator that takes a data file plus one named comparison per slide and emits stripped charts by rule (no legend when there's a single series, gridlines encoded pale by default) — it cannot judge what the "right" comparison is; you still name it.

Type this prompt into GitHub Copilot

Check: feed it a deliberately ambiguous two-comparison request for one slide (e.g. "category revenue this month and versus last year, one slide"). It must refuse or split into two slides — never stack both comparisons onto one chart with two series and a legend. The rule "one chart, one point" has to live in the generator itself, not in a reviewer catching it afterwards; if the script accepts the ambiguous input and produces a two-series chart anyway, the generator is wrong, send it back.

The point of the ladder From "AI lists what to strip" to "AI refuses to build an ambiguous chart at all" — the deliverable never changes: one slide chart, one comparison, everything else gone. What protects you at every rung is the cover-the-title test, not the tool. Learn to strip a chart by hand first, so you can tell a genuine single point from an AI's (or your own) instinct to keep "useful context" that the slide never asked for.
