Excel to a High Standard · Lesson 0009

SUMIFS: totals by criteria

Tied to your mission: a PivotTable (see PivotTables: summarise in seconds) is perfect for exploring, but sometimes you need a live number in a fixed cell — "total revenue for Snacks" sitting in a report that updates itself. SUMIFS is that number: a total that only adds the rows meeting your conditions, and recalculates the instant the data changes.

Practice workbook · open this first

Open the course workbook and click the SUMIFS tab — the flat sales list is on the left and a small summary block (categories down column H) is waiting for its totals.

The one idea

SUMIFS adds up a column, but only the rows that pass one or more tests. You read it as: sum this range, where that range equals this. The shape is =SUMIFS(sum_range, criteria_range, criteria) — and you can bolt on more criteria_range, criteria pairs to narrow it further (category and month, say).

To total Snacks revenue: =SUMIFS($F$2:$F$19, $B$2:$B$19, "Snacks"). Point the criteria at a cell instead of typing the word — …, H2) — and one formula fills down the whole summary.

Why it matters to an office assistant

Much of what you produce goes out under the department's name — trackers, summaries, the figures that back a piece of correspondence — and they need fixed lines that are right every time, not just the day you built them. SUMIFS gives you those lines as living formulas: refresh the extract and the totals move with it. It is the difference between a summary you have to remember to update and one that keeps itself correct.

The bad habit this undoes Filtering the list to one category, reading the status-bar sum, and typing that number into your report as a hard value — then repeating it for every category, every week, and never noticing when one goes stale. SUMIFS makes the total a formula that can't fall behind the data.

How to do it

On the SUMIFS tab, in I2 type =SUMIFS($E$2:$E$19, $B$2:$B$19, H2) for total cases, and in J2 type =SUMIFS($F$2:$F$19, $B$2:$B$19, H2) for total revenue. Fill both down to row 5. The $ signs lock the list so every row still points at the full data (see References) — only H2 moves.

Builds on References: those dollar signs are the absolute references you met there, doing exactly the job they were made for — anchoring the range while the criterion walks down.

Try it for real

Fill the summary on the SUMIFS tab, then sanity-check it: the four category revenue totals should add up to a plain =SUM(F2:F19) of the whole list. If they don't, a criterion is misspelt or a range is short. Then try a second block: add $A$2:$A$19, "Feb" to see Snacks revenue for one month.
