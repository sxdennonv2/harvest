Excel to a High Standard · Lesson 0007

Logical functions & IFERROR

Tied to your mission: a report shouldn't just show numbers, it should judge them — flag the lines under target, and never blow up with a #DIV/0! when a divisor is blank. IF makes a cell decide; IFERROR makes it fail gracefully. These are the glue between a raw formula and a report that goes out under the department's name looking clean.

Practice workbook · open this first

Open the course workbook and click the Logical & IFERROR tab — a product list with revenue, cases and a target sits ready for a verdict column and a safe per-case calculation.

The one idea

IF reads as if this is true, do the first thing, otherwise the second: =IF(D2>=E2, "On target", "Below"). Need several bands? IFS tests conditions in order: =IFS(D2>=E2,"On target", D2>=E2*0.8,"Close", TRUE,"Below"). Combine tests with AND / OR: =IF(AND(D2>=E2, B2="Snacks"), "Star", "").

IFERROR wraps anything that might error and supplies a clean fallback: =IFERROR(F2/C2, 0) gives revenue-per-case, but returns 0 instead of #DIV/0! when cases are blank.

Why it matters to an office assistant

A tracker that reads "Below" beside the weak lines is doing the triage for whoever reads it next — you don't have to explain which rows need attention, the sheet already says so. And a workbook that goes out under the department's name with a stray #DIV/0! loses trust instantly — one visible error and the reader starts doubting every other number on the page. IF adds judgement; IFERROR protects the finish.

The bad habit this undoes Eyeballing a column to decide which lines are under target, and sending out a tracker with a raw error cell because one row had no cases. Let the formula judge, and let IFERROR keep the page clean.

How to do it

On the Logical & IFERROR tab, in F2 type =IF(D2>=E2, "On target", "Below") and fill down — each line judges itself against its target. In G2 type =IFERROR(C2/B2, 0) for revenue per case that stays clean when a divisor is missing. Fill down. Row 7 has blank cases on purpose — watch IFERROR catch it.

Builds on references: if the target lived in one fixed cell instead of a column, you'd lock it with $ — the absolute reference from an earlier lesson, holding the goalpost still as the formula fills down.
