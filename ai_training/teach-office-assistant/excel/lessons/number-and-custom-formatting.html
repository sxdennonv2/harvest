Excel to a High Standard · Lesson 0014

Number & custom formatting

Tied to your mission: the maths can be perfect and the report still unreadable — 3480 where a reader wants $3,480, 0.31 where they want 31%. Formatting changes how a number looks, never its value, so you make the page legible without touching the underlying figure a single formula depends on.

Practice workbook · open this first

Open the course workbook and click the Number formatting tab — raw revenue, margin and date columns are waiting, all correct in value and all ugly on the page.

The one idea

A cell holds a value and wears a format. Select cells and press Ctrl+1 to open Format Cells: choose Currency for $3,480, Percentage for 31%, or Number with a thousands separator. The stored value is unchanged — a SUMIFS on the column still adds the true figures.

Custom formats go further with a code: #,##0 (whole thousands), $#,##0.00 (money), 0.0% (one-decimal percent), or #,##0;[Red](#,##0) to show negatives red and in brackets.

Why it matters to an office assistant

Much of what you produce goes out under the department's name — a training tracker, a minutes summary, a simple budget — and readers judge it by shape before they judge it by content: aligned money, consistent decimals, percentages that look like percentages. Formatting is what turns a column of raw numbers into something that scans in seconds instead of stumbling. And because it never alters the value, you get all the polish with none of the risk to a formula someone else in the department is relying on.

The bad habit this undoes Typing a dollar sign or a "%" as text — "$3,480" — so the cell is no longer a number and every total breaks. Format the number; never disguise it as text.

How to do it

On the Number formatting tab, select the revenue column and press Ctrl+1 ▸ Currency, 0 decimals. Select the margin column ▸ Percentage, 1 decimal. Select the date column ▸ Date in your preferred style. For the variance column, apply a custom code #,##0;[Red](#,##0) so losses show red and bracketed. Nothing you typed changed — only how it reads.

Builds on the whole course: every sheet you've built — SUMIFS totals, pivots, charts — reads better the moment its numbers are formatted. This is the polish the other lessons assume.
