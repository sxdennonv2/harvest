Excel to a High Standard · Lesson 0011

Date & time functions

Tied to your mission: your work runs on dates — a task logged, a training deadline, a month to report against, an age to chase. A date in Excel is really just a number, which is exactly why you can add a lead time to it, pull its month out, or find its month-end. TODAY, EOMONTH, TEXT and plain arithmetic turn a column of dates into the calendar logic a report needs.

Practice workbook · open this first

Open the course workbook and click the Dates tab — a list of orders with dates and lead times sits ready for a due date, a month label, a month-end and an age in days.

The one idea

A date is a serial number under the surface, so date maths is just arithmetic: =B2+C2 adds a lead time in days to an order date to get a due date, and =TODAY()-B2 gives an age in days. TODAY() returns today's date and re-reads every time the file opens.

To pull parts out, YEAR, MONTH and DAY return numbers, while TEXT makes a label: =TEXT(B2, "mmm") gives Jan, =TEXT(B2, "yyyy-mm") gives 2026-01 — perfect for grouping. EOMONTH(B2, 0) returns the last day of that month (use 1 for next month-end); WEEKDAY and NETWORKDAYS handle day-of-week and working-day counts.

Why it matters to an office assistant

Meeting minutes, training records, correspondence logs — most of what crosses your desk carries a date that has to turn into a deadline, a status, or a month heading someone can file it under. A TEXT(date, "yyyy-mm") column is what makes a tracker group tidily by month instead of you scrolling and squinting for where January ends. Due dates and ages, computed not typed, are what let a task list flag what's overdue today without you touching it every morning. Get the date logic right once and every record that goes out under the department's name stays trustworthy on its own.

The bad habit this undoes Typing due dates by hand from a mental "plus two weeks", and grouping a report by month by sorting and eyeballing where the month changes. Both drift and both break on the next refresh — let the date arithmetic and a month key do it.

How to do it

On the Dates tab, in D2 type =B2+C2 to add the lead days to the order date for a due date. In E2 type =TEXT(B2, "mmm") for a short month label. In F2 type =EOMONTH(B2, 0) for that order's month-end. In G2 type =TODAY()-B2 for the age in days. Fill all four down. If a due date shows as a plain number, format the column as a date — the value is right, only its display needs setting.

Builds on formatting: when arithmetic returns a serial number, a date number format turns it back into a readable date — the same "format changes the display, not the value" idea from the number-formatting lesson.
