Word to a High Standard · Lesson 0002

The heading hierarchy: one outline, three payoffs

Tied to your mission: minutes, procedures and templates that go out under the department's name need a nav pane you can trust, a TOC that generates itself, and sections you can drag into a better order without breaking anything underneath them. All three come from the same place — the heading levels — and all three lie the moment those levels stop matching the document's real logic. Builds on Styles, not formatting: that lesson got you applying Heading 1/2 as real styles instead of bold text; this one is about choosing which level each heading actually gets.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario A — The hand-formatted submission, already rescued from the previous lesson: real Heading 1/2/Body styles are applied, the nav pane now shows something. What it shows is still wrong — the levels were assigned by how the text used to look, not by what the document actually says.

The one idea

Heading levels are an outline, not a font ladder. Heading 1, Heading 2, Heading 3 exist to encode the document's logical tree — this is a top-level section, this is a subsection of it, this is a sub-subsection of that — and nothing else. Three tools read that tree for free, without you building anything extra: the nav pane walks it and jumps you anywhere in it, the table of contents generates itself from it, and outline-drag in the nav pane moves a whole section — heading and everything nested under it — in one action.

The moment you pick a level because "this deserves to be bigger" or "this looks important", instead of because of where it sits in the tree, you've told a lie that all three tools now repeat on your behalf. Skip a level — Heading 1 straight to Heading 3 — and the nav pane shows a gap nobody can explain. Promote a subsection to Heading 1 for visual weight and the TOC publishes it as a top-level section it isn't. The tools aren't wrong; the levels fed to them were.

Why it matters to an office assistant

A set of department minutes or a procedures document routinely runs long, with sections nested two or three deep — meeting overview, then per-agenda-item detail, then per-action-point notes inside each item. Nobody who picks it up reads it start to finish; a manager opens the nav pane, jumps to the one agenda item they were asked about, and expects everything under that heading — including the note you added at the last minute — to be right there, in order. If the levels are honest, that jump takes two seconds. If a subsection was promoted to look important, it shows up in the nav pane as a sibling of the section it actually belongs inside, and the reader jumps to the wrong place, or worse, skims the TOC and never finds it at all — and because your name is on the document, the confusion lands back on you.

The bad habit this undoes Choosing a heading level by how it should look rather than where it sits in the document's logic — Heading 1 because "it needs to stand out", a jump straight from Heading 1 to Heading 3 because Heading 2 "felt like too much", a document where the visual hierarchy on the page and the logical hierarchy underneath it quietly disagree. Nobody notices until the TOC is generated and it publishes the disagreement for everyone to see.

How to do it

On Scenario A's rescued submission, open the navigation pane (View → Navigation Pane, or Ctrl+F) and read the tree it currently shows against what the document actually argues. The submission's real logic is: each numbered category is a top-level section, each supplier discussed inside a category is a subsection of it, and there are no sub-subsections in this draft. So the rule is Heading 1 for sections, Heading 2 for subsections, no skips.

Two level-lies are sitting in the draft from the hand-formatting era. First: one supplier's sub-heading was made Heading 1 during the styles rescue because in the old bold-14pt version it was sized to match the section headings above it — click into it and re-apply Heading 2 from the Styles gallery, the same select-and-click move from the last lesson. Second: section 4's sub-headings were left as Heading 3 with no Heading 2 in between — because section 4 has no subsection layer in this draft, promote them to Heading 2 so the tree doesn't show a level with nothing above it.

Now collect the payoffs. In the nav pane, click through the tree top to bottom — it should read as a plain-English contents list of the submission, sections then their suppliers, nothing orphaned and nothing skipped. Then drag one supplier subsection to a new position under its category in the nav pane and watch the body text move with it — heading and every paragraph nested beneath it travels as one block, because the outline-drag moves the logical unit, not just a line of text.

Check yourself

Answer these before moving on — immediate feedback below each.

1. A subsection was made Heading 1 because it needed to look bigger on the page. What has actually gone wrong?

2. What does dragging a heading in the navigation pane actually move?

3. A document jumps from Heading 1 straight to Heading 3 with no Heading 2 in between. What will the nav pane show?

Try it for real

Take a real multi-section document of yours — or the rescued Scenario A submission if you don't have one handy — and list every heading with its level, in order, reading straight down the nav pane. For each one, ask: does this level match where the heading sits in the document's actual argument, not how big it looks? Fix any skip (a level with nothing above it) and any promotion-for-size you find. Self-check: the nav pane should read as an honest table of contents for the document before you insert any actual TOC field — if you'd be embarrassed to hand a reader that nav-pane list as your contents page, a level is still lying.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — get the heading levels to match the document's real logical tree — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: the nav pane must read as an honest outline of the document before any TOC field exists.

Rung 2 · Aldi:GPT — it audits the list you paste, not your document

Aldi:GPT can't open your file, so it only ever sees the heading list you paste in; it can flag skips, promotions-for-size, and orphan levels in that list, but it cannot see whether a heading's actual content still belongs where you've put it.

Type this prompt into Aldi:GPT

Check: the signature failure at this rung is the model overreaching into restructuring the content — proposing that two sections be merged, or reordered, or that a heading be reworded. Those are editorial calls the outline audit doesn't own. A good answer sticks strictly to level problems: skips, size-promotions, orphan levels. If it starts rewriting your section structure, ignore that part and keep only the level flags.

Rung 3 · M365 Copilot — it drafts the new heading, it doesn't guard the tree

Copilot in Word can draft a new section directly into your document at your cursor, but it inherits the surrounding context imperfectly — it doesn't reliably check what level the new heading should be relative to the whole tree, only to the paragraph right where you asked it to write.

Type this prompt into Copilot (in Word)

Check: the moment the new section lands, click into its heading and read the Styles gallery — what level did it actually arrive at? A new Heading 1 dropped inside what should be a subsection breaks the tree silently; nothing on screen shouts about it until you open the nav pane and see it sitting as a sibling of the whole category instead of a child of the supplier section. Fix the level before you move on, every time, not just when it looks wrong.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — it audits the file, it doesn't rewrite your structure

An agent with file access can open the actual .docx, walk every heading in the XML, and report the tree with flags and the TOC it implies — far more reliable than a pasted list, because it can't miss a heading you forgot to copy.

Type this prompt into GitHub Copilot

Check: run it on the submission before your fixes — it should raise exactly the two flags you already found by hand (the size-promoted Heading 1, the orphaned Heading 3 in section 4). Run it again after your fixes — a clean tree, matching the document's stated logic, zero flags. An auditor that also starts flagging stylistic choices — a heading it thinks is worded oddly, or "too short" — has stopped auditing structure and started opining; that output is not the deliverable.

The point of the ladder From "audit the list I paste" to "audit the file itself" — the deliverable never changes: a heading tree where every level matches the document's real logic, with no skips and no promotions-for-size. What protects you at every rung is the same check — read the nav pane as the document's honest contents list — not the tool. Learn to spot a level-lie by eye first, so you can tell a real structural fix from a model quietly rewriting your document's argument.
