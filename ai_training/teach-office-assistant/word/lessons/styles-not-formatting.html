Word to a High Standard · Lesson 0001

Styles, not formatting: fix it once

Tied to your mission: a GDM submission gets assembled, reviewed, revised, and reviewed again — and every one of those passes depends on Word knowing what each paragraph is, not just what it currently looks like. This lesson is the floor the rest of this course stands on: sections, the TOC, the nav pane, and templates all read the same signal — real styles. Skip this and every later lesson is fighting a document that doesn't know its own shape.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario A — The hand-formatted submission — the Q3 range-review submission, 14 pages, formatted entirely by hand, with the correct rescue described underneath so you can check your own work.

The one idea

A style names what a piece of text is — Heading 1, Heading 2, Body, Caption. Direct formatting only paints what it looks like today: bold, 14pt, a colour picked from the swatch. The two feel identical on screen for about five minutes, and then they diverge completely, because only one of them is connected to anything.

A style-formatted document obeys: change the Heading 2 style's colour once, and every Heading 2 on fourteen pages follows, instantly, without you touching a single one of them by hand. A hand-formatted document is fourteen pages of individual decisions that were each correct in isolation and drift the moment anything changes — a "heading" typed at 14pt on page 2 and 13.5pt on page 9, because two different people eyeballed it two different days. The tell that separates the two: open the navigation pane. Does it know your document exists?

Why it matters to an office assistant

Much of what you produce goes out under the department's name — letters, minutes, training material, procedures — and almost none of it is written once and left alone. A set of minutes goes back for a correction; a procedure gets revised when the process changes; a submission gets passed round for comment before it's final. If the headings are real styles, every one of those passes is quick: the nav pane lets you or a colleague jump straight to the section that needs work, the TOC updates itself, and an instruction like "make all the section headings the department's house colour" is a thirty-second style edit. If the headings are hand-formatted bold-and-size, that same instruction means opening every page and hoping you catch every impostor — and a missed one is exactly the kind of thing that turns up in a document going out under your department's name.

The bad habit this undoes Selecting a line of Normal text and making it bold and a bigger size to "make it a heading." It looks like a heading. It is not one — it's invisible to the navigation pane, invisible to the table of contents, invisible to every tool downstream that walks a document by its styles rather than its pixels. The habit feels harmless because the page looks right today; it fails silently, later, the first time anyone needs the document to know its own structure.

How to do it

Open Scenario A. Its "headings" are Normal text made bold 14pt (sometimes 13.5pt, because a second hand joined in later) by selection; its sub-headings are bold+italic everywhere except section 4, where someone underlined instead; and one ordinary paragraph of body text was accidentally made bold 14pt too, so it now impersonates a heading it never meant to be. Rescue it in this order.

First, the real headings: click into each hand-bolded "heading" line — you don't need to select the whole line, just place the cursor in it — and click Heading 1 or Heading 2 in the Styles gallery on the Home tab, matching the level to where it sits in the document's outline. Do this by clicking the built-in style, never by retyping the text. Do the same for every sub-heading, including section 4's underlined ones — underline is not a style, it's paint, and Heading 2 replaces it the moment you click.

Second, find the impostor paragraph — the one line of body text that got bold-14pt by accident. Click into it and apply Body (or Normal, whichever this document's Body style is named). Applying the style clears the direct formatting sitting on top of it; you are demoting it by style, not by manually stripping bold and resetting the font size one property at a time.

Third, prove the fix travels: right-click Heading 2 in the Styles gallery, choose Modify, and change its colour once, in the style definition itself — not by selecting each heading and repainting it. Click OK. Every Heading 2 on all fourteen pages changes colour at once, because they were never individually coloured to begin with.

Now open the navigation pane (View → Navigation Pane). Before the rescue it showed nothing — an outline view that was a wall of Normal. After it, every real heading appears in the pane in order, nested correctly, and clicking one jumps straight to it. That walk is the proof the rescue worked, not a visual impression that it looks tidier.

Try it for real

Take a document of your own that has at least a few hand-formatted "headings" — bold-and-bigger text standing in for real structure. Apply real Heading 1/2 and Body styles by select-and-click the way you just did in Scenario A, then pick one heading level and change its style's colour or font once, in the style definition. Self-check: open the navigation pane. Every heading you styled should appear there, correctly nested, and the one style change you made should have landed on every instance of that level without you touching any of them individually. If even one heading is missing from the pane, it's still direct formatting masquerading as a heading — go back and style it.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — turn a hand-formatted document's headings and body text into real styles — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: open the navigation pane and confirm every heading is really there, and confirm a single style edit reaches every instance of that level.

Check: the signature failure at this rung is prescribing brand new custom styles for jobs the built-ins already own — Word already has Heading 1 and Heading 2; a mapping that invents "MyHeading" or "SectionTitle" multiplies the problem instead of fixing it. A good answer maps everything onto Word's existing built-in styles and tells you to strip the bold/italic/underline once the style is applied, not before.

Rung 3 · M365 Copilot in Word — it drafts into a styled document, it doesn't apply styles for you

Be honest about the boundary: applying styles is native Word — the Styles gallery, a click, done — not a Copilot capability. Copilot's move is drafting or rewriting text; what matters is whether what it writes lands inside your styled structure or arrives as its own island of direct formatting.

If the style box doesn't say what you expect, apply the correct style yourself before you move on; Copilot rewrote the words, it didn't guarantee the structure.

The point of the ladder From "AI proposes the mapping" to "AI builds the styled document from scratch" — the deliverable never changes: real styles doing the work, zero direct formatting impersonating them. What protects you at every rung is the same check — open the navigation pane, change one style, watch the whole document follow. Learn to do it by hand first, so you can tell a real style from a convincing fake at every rung above it.
