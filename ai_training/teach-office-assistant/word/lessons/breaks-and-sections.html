Breaks & sections: why your page numbering fights you

Tied to your mission: a GDM submission has a title page that shouldn't be numbered, a landscape table that must not flip the rest of the document sideways, and an annex that restarts its own numbering. None of that is optional formatting — it's structure, and structure is exactly what this course exists to make obey. Get the section map right once and the document stops fighting you every time it grows a line.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario B — The fighting page numbers: the title page shows "Page 1", the annex restarts numbering randomly, a landscape table once flipped the whole document sideways, and the "correct structure" underneath tells you exactly what the repaired section map must look like.

The one idea

Pages fight you because paragraphs are doing a section's job. Word has a hierarchy of separators, and each owns different powers. A page break (Ctrl+Enter) does exactly one thing: it moves what follows to the next page. A section break does much more — it owns page numbering, orientation, headers/footers and margins for everything after it, until the next section break says otherwise. Enter-Enter-Enter is not a break at all; it's empty paragraphs cosplaying as one, and it shatters the moment the text above it grows by a single line.

Every numbering restart, every landscape table, every unnumbered title page is a section decision — made deliberately, with a section break placed on purpose, or suffered accidentally, when one arrives pasted in from another document and nobody notices until the numbering downstream is wrong.

Why it matters to an office assistant

A GDM submission lands on your desk to assemble under deadline, stitched together from sections other people drafted, a supplier table someone else built, and a cover page nobody wants numbered "Page 1". You're the one who owns the file going out under the department's name, so if page numbering and orientation are being held together by manual pushes and blank paragraphs, every last-minute edit — the director tightening the exec summary, a colleague adding a sentence to the annex — risks shunting the whole back half of the document out of shape the night before it's due, with your name on the version that went wrong. A submission built on real section breaks survives that edit without you touching it again.

The bad habit this undoes The Enter-cascade — hitting Enter repeatedly to "push" the next heading onto a new page — which looks fine until one paragraph above it grows by a line and everything below drifts. And the accidental section break: pasted in from another document, it silently flips page numbering or orientation for the rest of the file, and the usual "fix" is more Enters piled on top, which papers over the symptom and leaves the actual accidental break sitting there for the next edit to trip over.

How to do it

First, turn on formatting marks (Ctrl+Shift+8, or the ¶ button on the Home tab). You cannot fix what you can't see, and every diagnosis in Scenario B depends on seeing paragraph marks, page-break marks and section-break marks as distinct symbols rather than guessing from where text happens to land.

Fix the title page first. Its "Page 1" is wrong because there's no section boundary between it and the body — put the cursor at the very start of the second page's content, go to Layout > Breaks > Section Breaks > Next Page, then in the body section's footer untick "Link to Previous" before setting its page numbering to restart at 1. The title page's own section stays un-numbered by decision, not by hiding the field.

Next, the annex. Scenario B says it restarts numbering "randomly" — find the section break just before it with formatting marks on (it shows as a double dotted line labelled "Section Break"). If it's a genuine Next Page section break, keep it, but make the restart deliberate: Insert > Page Number > Format Page Numbers > Start at 1, set consciously in that section's footer, not left as an accident that happens to look intentional.

Then the landscape table. It needs its own section pair: a Next Page (or Continuous, if it shouldn't start a fresh page) section break immediately before it and another immediately after, with only the pages inside that pair set to Landscape via Layout > Orientation. Everything outside that pair must stay Portrait untouched.

Last, hunt every Enter-cascade. With formatting marks on, three or more consecutive paragraph marks (¶¶¶) with nothing between them is never a break, it's a stand-in for one. Delete the run of empty paragraphs and replace the job it was doing with the right tool: Ctrl+Enter for a plain page break where nothing but the page needs to change, or a section break where numbering, orientation or the footer must change too.

The test that proves you're done: place the cursor on page 2, wherever it falls, and type one new paragraph. Nothing downstream should shatter — no numbering jump, no page flipping orientation, no annex losing its restart. If anything moves that shouldn't, a paragraph somewhere is still doing a section's job.

Try it for real

On Scenario B's specimen, repair all three problems: unnumber the title page by section decision, make the annex's restart-at-1 deliberate, and confine the landscape table to its own section pair — replacing every Enter-cascade you find along the way with the right break. Self-check: with formatting marks on, place the cursor on page 2 and type one new paragraph. If any numbering jumps, any orientation flips, or the annex's restart moves, a paragraph somewhere is still doing a section's job — find it and fix it before you call the document done.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — a correct section map for a document with a title page, a numbered body, a restarting annex and a landscape table — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: insert a paragraph on page 2, and nothing downstream shatters.

Rung 2 · Aldi:GPT — it prescribes the section map, you place every break

Aldi:GPT can't see your document, so describe its numbering and orientation requirements in full; it prescribes where each break goes and which kind, and you place every one of them yourself.

Type this prompt into Aldi:GPT

Check: the signature failure at this rung is over-sectioning — a section break proposed where a plain page break would have done the job with nothing to maintain. Read the prescription back and confirm every section break it names comes with a stated reason: the specific property (numbering, orientation, or footer) that changes at that boundary. A section break with no named property attached is one break too many.

Rung 3 · Microsoft 365 Copilot — honest boundary: breaks live on the Layout tab, not in Copilot

Inserting or changing a page break, a section break, orientation or page-number formatting is native Word functionality on the Layout tab — Copilot does not have a button for any of it. Where Copilot helps is drafting or summarising text near a section boundary; the boundary itself, you still build by hand.

Type this prompt into Copilot (in Word)

Check: after Copilot inserts anything near a section boundary, turn on formatting marks and look at exactly where the new text landed. Confirm it sits inside the section you intended, on the correct side of the break — a drafting tool has no concept of "section" and can drop text on the wrong side of a boundary without either of you noticing until the numbering is wrong again.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — a python-docx section auditor, not a fixer

An agent with file access can read the document's XML and report on its sections directly — far more reliably than eyeballing formatting marks — but it edits nothing in your live file; you apply any fix yourself in Word.

Type this prompt into GitHub Copilot

Check: run it against Scenario B's broken specimen first — the report must name the accidental section break (the one causing the random annex restart) and flag every Enter-cascade with its location; if either is missing, the auditor isn't reading closely enough. Then run it against your repaired version: the report should come back as a clean section map, one section per genuine property change, and zero suspected cascades.

The point of the ladder From "AI prescribes the map" to "AI audits the file for you" — the deliverable never changes: a section map where every break earns its place. What protects you at every rung is the same test, insert a paragraph on page 2 and watch nothing shatter, not the tool. Learn to read formatting marks by hand first, so you can tell a justified section break from an over-sectioned one and a real Enter-cascade from an audit tool's false positive.
