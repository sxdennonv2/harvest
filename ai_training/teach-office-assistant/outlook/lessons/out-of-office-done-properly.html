Outlook to a High Standard · Lesson 0013

Out-of-office done properly

Tied to your mission: the department's correspondence doesn't pause because you're on leave, and neither does the training material a colleague needs before Monday's session. An out-of-office isn't a politeness reflex — it's the one message that has to do real work in your absence. Get it right and the mailbox routes itself for a week; get it wrong and you come back to two hundred mails that all assumed someone was watching.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and read Scenario H — The absence brief. It gives you the exact dates, the two coverers and their topics, and the escalation route — everything a correct out-of-office needs and nothing it should invent.

The one idea

An out-of-office is routing, not apology. Its job is to let the sender self-route without you: exact dates including the day you're back, who covers what by name, and nothing that requires you to be reachable. Write it so a stranger reading it never has to guess or wait.

That job splits into two different messages, because the routing information a colleague needs is not the routing information a supplier needs. The internal version routes by topic with names — "DC questions to Alex, supplier commercials to Priya." The external version gives dates and one contact, nothing else — no org chart, no second name, no director mentioned. Both are set to expire the moment you're back, so the message can't go on lying about where you are after you've returned. The one line it must never contain is "I will be checking emails occasionally" — that sentence quietly cancels the coverage you just described and puts you half on the leave you were meant to be fully on.

Why it matters to an office assistant

You're the one people route around, so an out-of-office that fails to route is felt across the whole department. A colleague chasing a template or a set of minutes doesn't know or care that you're out Monday to Friday — what they need, the moment your autoreply lands in their inbox, is a name they can email today that will actually answer. If your out-of-office is an apology with no routing, that colleague either waits for you (and the training material slips) or guesses who else to try (and probably guesses wrong, or worse, escalates to the director directly because nobody named was covering). A routing-first out-of-office is what protects the department's day-to-day while you're not the one holding it together — Priya gets the commercial question the moment it's sent, not five days later when you're back and the deadline has already passed.

The bad habit this undoes The apology autoreply — "Sorry for any inconvenience, I'll respond on my return" — routes nobody to anybody. It tells the sender only that they must wait, so everything piles up in your inbox regardless of urgency, and you come back to two hundred mails that all needed a human days ago. Its worse cousin is the "checking emails occasionally" variant: it sounds considerate, but it's a promise that cancels the leave it just announced. The moment you write that line, the sender assumes you're reachable, the coverage you arranged goes unused, and you're now working your holiday in fragments instead of actually being off it.

How to do it

Build both versions from Scenario H's brief: out Monday 20 – Friday 24 July, back Monday 27; Alex Moreau covers DC/logistics questions; Priya Shah covers supplier commercials; anything genuinely urgent escalates to your director. Nothing in either message should say more, or less, than that.

Write the internal version first. This one can carry names and direct routes, because the reader already knows who Alex and Priya are:

Out of the office Monday 20 – Friday 24 July, back Monday 27 July. For DC or logistics questions, contact Alex Moreau. For supplier commercial questions, contact Priya Shah. Anything genuinely urgent, escalate to [director's name]. I will not be checking email during this time.

Write the external version second — and make it shorter, not longer. A supplier or outside contact doesn't need your org chart; they need dates and one door to knock on:

Out of the office Monday 20 – Friday 24 July, back Monday 27 July. For anything requiring attention before then, please contact Priya Shah. I will not be checking email during this time.

Notice what the external version drops: Alex's name, the DC/logistics split, and the director escalation — all internal structure a supplier has no use for and no business seeing. One contact is enough; a supplier chasing a price confirmation doesn't need to know your team has two coverers and a director behind them, they need to know who answers when they write.

Now the mechanics. In Outlook, go to File → Automatic Replies (Out of Office). Turn on Send automatic replies, then tick Only send during this time range and set it to Monday 20 July through Monday 27 July — this is what makes the message start and expire itself, so it can never lie about your absence after you're back. Paste the internal version into the Inside My Organization tab, and the external version into the Outside My Organization tab (with "Auto-reply to people outside my organization" turned on, restricted to your contacts or opened up as your organisation's policy requires). Click OK.

Check it against the pack's box: dates exact including the 27th · who covers what, by name, in the internal version only · no "checking occasionally" anywhere · two versions, the external one shorter and contact-only · expiry set so it turns itself off.

Try it for real

Draft the internal and external out-of-office pair for your own next absence, right now — real dates, your real back-on day, whoever actually covers your topics while you're out. Save both as Outlook drafts, or turn them into Quick Parts you can insert straight into Automatic Replies next time (see Signatures & Quick Parts if you haven't built your Quick Parts library yet). Check: hand each version to a stranger's read — the internal one should tell them exactly who to contact for what, by name; the external one should tell them exactly one contact and nothing about your team's structure. Confirm neither version makes a promise you won't actually keep (no "checking occasionally," no invented response time).

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — produce the internal/external out-of-office pair from the absence brief — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: does each version route correctly to the people the brief actually named, does the external version stay free of internal structure, and does neither version promise something you won't keep?

Rung 2 · Aldi:GPT — it drafts both versions from the brief, you verify the facts and the leak

Aldi:GPT can't see your calendar or your coverage arrangement, so it only knows what you paste. It drafts both versions from the brief; you verify every fact survived the trip and that nothing crossed from internal to external.

Check: the signature failure at this rung has two flavours. First, the leak — the external version naming both Alex and Priya, or mentioning the director, which hands a supplier your org chart when all they need is one contact. Second, the invented promise — a line like "I will respond by Tuesday the 28th" that the brief never stated. Facts in, facts out: the external version should end up shorter than the internal one, not longer. If it's longer, something leaked.

Rung 3 · M365 Copilot — it drafts and refines the text in Outlook, you still flip the switch

Be honest about the boundary: Copilot in Outlook can draft and refine the wording for you in context, but the Automatic Replies toggle, the time range, and which text goes in the Inside/Outside tab are settings you set — Copilot doesn't turn anything on for you.

Type this prompt into Copilot (in Outlook)

Check: two things to verify. First, the dates must still read the 27th, not "next week" or "soon" — a refined draft that loses the exact date has lost the one thing that lets it expire honestly. Second, watch for the classic slip: pasting the internal version into the external Automatic Replies box by mistake, which ships Alex and Priya's names and remits to every supplier who writes in. Open both tabs and confirm the right text is in each before you click OK.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — it builds the absence kit, not the live autoreply

GitHub Copilot is an agent with file access but no connection to your live mailbox — it cannot open Automatic Replies or flip the toggle for you. What it can do, from a plain-text coverage-definition file, is build the whole absence kit around it: both out-of-office texts, a coverage matrix, and the pre-leave checklist that makes sure the kit actually gets used.

Type this prompt into GitHub Copilot

Check: everything in the kit must trace back to the definition file. A kit that invents a coverer for a topic you didn't assign has just created a promise someone else now has to keep without knowing it. And the checklist must include setting the expiry — a kit that builds a perfect autoreply but skips "set the time range" has automated the exact lie this lesson exists to prevent.

The point of the ladder From "AI drafts both versions" to "AI builds the whole absence kit" — the deliverable is the same two-version, self-expiring out-of-office at every rung, and none of these tools can reach into Automatic Replies and turn it on for you. What protects you is the same check throughout: does every name and date trace to the brief, does the external version stay free of internal structure, and does the expiry stop the message from lying once you're back. Craft first, so you can supervise.
