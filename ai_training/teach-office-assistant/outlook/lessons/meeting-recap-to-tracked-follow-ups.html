Outlook to a High Standard · Lesson 0010

From meeting to tracked follow-ups

Tied to your mission: turning meeting notes into minutes and tracked actions people actually do is right there in your success criteria — but a boundary is worth stating plainly: writing the minutes document — the prose, the structure, what goes in the "decisions" section versus the "actions" section — is the communication course's job; a sibling lesson there covers exactly that. This lesson is earlier and narrower than that. It's the Outlook plumbing: once the notes exist, where do their outputs have to live so they actually fire, instead of sitting typed-up and ignored. Zero wording advice below — only where things go.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario G — The meeting output — raw notes from a real promo meeting, with the correct capture given underneath so you can mark your own work.

The one idea

A meeting isn't over until its outputs live where they'll fire. Notes are a holding pen, not a destination — the capture pass is the five-minute move, right after the meeting ends, that empties the pen into instruments that act without you remembering them: your own actions become flagged items or tasks with a date attached, other people's actions become follow-ups you can chase (a flagged copy of a sent email works fine), anything parked gets written straight onto the next meeting's invite rather than into a notebook, and decisions travel immediately to whoever's sheet they change.

None of that is writing. The notes can be a single scruffy paragraph, exactly like Scenario G's — what matters is that every line in them gets sorted into one of those four instruments before you close the window. A note that stays a note, however tidily typed, has zero chance of firing on its own.

Why it matters to an office assistant

Promo meetings generate exactly this kind of output every time: a volume you personally have to confirm back to a supplier by a fixed morning, a delivery-day change that has to reach someone else's capacity sheet today, a proof another team owes you by Tuesday, a booking a colleague owes by Monday, and one item that's explicitly not for now. None of those five things is safe inside a notes file. As the person who keeps the department running, you're often the one running this meeting on someone else's behalf — the Friday confirmation only happens if something on your side is dated and visible Friday morning; Dana's Tuesday proof only gets chased if something exists whose job is to remind you Dana owes it. An office assistant who runs the capture pass turns one meeting into five things that happen on their own schedule and reflects well on the department; one who just types up notes turns one meeting into a document nobody opens again until the surprise lands on the department's desk.

The bad habit this undoes The notebook graveyard: faithful, well-organised notes, typed up neatly and filed away in OneNote or a notes folder, and never consulted again. Three weeks later the parked organic-samples item ambushes everyone at the next meeting as if it had never been raised, and Dana's Tuesday proof never got chased because nothing was ever built whose job was to chase it — the notes recorded that it was owed, but recording isn't the same as tracking. The meeting doesn't fail loudly. It fails by being perfectly, silently remembered in a place nobody looks.

How to do it

Run the capture pass on Scenario G's raw notes line by line, sorting each output into its instrument rather than leaving it in prose. Two lines are your own actions: "I send Sam the confirmation Friday AM" and "I need to tell Alex today". Both become dated commitments you'll actually see — a flagged email or a task with a due date of Friday morning for the first, today for the second (a flag on the Popcorn line in your existing reply draft on the supplier thread does the "tell Alex" job, since it's already slated to go out today). Neither should still be sitting as a sentence in a notes file once you're done; each now lives somewhere that will surface it on its own on the day it matters.

Two lines are other people's actions: "Dana getting us the catalogue proof Tuesday" and "Alex to book the DC slots for the Wednesday pattern by Monday". You don't own the doing, but you own the chasing, so each becomes a follow-up you can find later — flag your own message that asked for it, or start a short reply on the thread now and flag that as a Tuesday/Monday follow-up. The point isn't to trust memory to notice Wednesday that Tuesday came and went with no proof; it's to have built, in advance, the thing that will make you notice.

One line is parked, not actioned: "organic samples timing — next month's meeting, don't lose it." The instrument for a parked item is never a notebook — it's the agenda of the meeting it's parked to. Add it as a line on next month's invite (or a draft placeholder invite) right now, while you still remember the context. A parked item that lives only in this meeting's notes has already been lost; it just hasn't surprised anyone yet.

The remaining three lines are decisions, and a decision's instrument is the person whose sheet it changes — sent now, not filed for later. "Confirmed 110/wk rice crackers" and "Popcorn is out of the promo" both change Alex's sheet, so they go to him today, in the same message that carries the Popcorn line (that's why "tell Alex today" and "Popcorn out" are the same email, not two). "Wednesday deliveries agreed" is already settled and needs no further routing unless someone downstream of Alex also plans around it — check who else's sheet the delivery-day change touches before you call this one closed.

Try it for real

Take the notes from your own last real meeting — whatever scruffy form they're currently in — and run the same capture pass on them. Sort every line into one of the four instruments: your own actions as flagged items or tasks with dates, other people's actions as follow-ups you've built to chase them, anything parked written onto the next meeting's invite, and every decision sent today to whoever's sheet it changes. Self-check when you're done: for every single output, can you say — without reopening the notes — where it now lives and what will make it fire on the right day? If you had to go back and reread the notes to answer that for even one item, the capture pass isn't finished; that item is still sitting in the notebook graveyard.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — turn raw meeting notes into outputs that live in their firing instruments — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: every action carries an owner and a date, or it's flagged as ownerless rather than quietly assigned one, and the parked item never gets promoted into an action.

Rung 2 · Aldi:GPT — it extracts the action table, you police the parked item

Aldi:GPT can't see your mailbox or your task list, so paste the raw notes and it extracts the action table — what, owner, date, instrument. The failure it can't see coming is the parked item quietly promoted into an action, since "next month's meeting" reads, out of context, a lot like a deadline.

Check: the signature failure at this rung is the parked organic-samples item landing in the table as a dated action ("next month's meeting" read as a deadline) — parked means parked, it belongs in the next-invite column, not the my-flag or chase column. The second failure to watch for is an ownerless action getting an invented owner: nothing in these notes says who owns confirming Wednesday deliveries further downstream, so if the table assigns that to someone, the tool guessed. An ownerless line means the meeting itself left it unresolved, and the table should say that, not paper over it.

Rung 3 · Microsoft 365 Copilot — it drafts the recap, you audit it against your notes

Copilot can recap a Teams/Outlook meeting and draft the follow-up email straight from the thread or recording — but it's summarising a conversation, and conversations blur categories in ways your own notes, taken at the time, don't.

Check: audit the recap line by line against your own notes — your notes are the source of truth here, the recap is a draft of them, not a replacement. The classic failure at this rung is the recap smoothing the parked organic-samples item into a decision ("samples will run next month") when nothing in the meeting decided that, or quietly dropping a deadline from an action (Dana's proof becomes "Dana will send the catalogue proof" with Tuesday gone). If a date or the parked/decided distinction didn't survive the recap, the recap is wrong, not your notes.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — it builds the capture kit, it can't manufacture owners

An agent with file access can turn a notes file into the whole capture kit in one pass — but it only has what's in the file, so anything the meeting itself left unresolved has to stay unresolved in the output too.

Check: every action in the output must carry an owner and a date, or be explicitly flagged "ownerless — raise it" — a kit that guesses an owner to fill the column is worse than one that admits the gap. Confirm the parked item appears only in next-meeting.md and nowhere in my-actions.md or chase-schedule.md: a kit that turns a parked item into a task with a date has manufactured work nobody in the room agreed to.

The point of the ladder From "AI sorts the notes you paste in" to "AI builds the whole capture kit from a file" — the deliverable never changes: every output sitting in the instrument that will actually fire it, with the parked item parked and no owner invented where the meeting left a gap. Learn to run the capture pass by hand first, so you can catch an AI smoothing a parked item into a decision or quietly filling an ownerless line — the two ways this task fails without ever raising an alarm.
