Word to a High Standard · Lesson 0007

Track changes without fear

Tied to your mission: submissions and papers for GDM and senior audiences run through review rounds before they leave your desk, and every one of those rounds arrives back as a document full of coloured strikethroughs, balloons, and edits sitting on top of someone else's draft. Reviewing long documents fast — tracked changes, comments that direct rework — is exactly what this course exists to make routine, and that only holds if you can open a reviewed document without flinching. The fear isn't the tool; it's reading the markup in the wrong view and adjudicating changes you haven't actually looked at.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario D — The reviewed draft — the submission back from your director (tracked changes: a tightened exec summary, a volume figure changed 110 → 100, a deleted paragraph) and from Merch (five comments), with the correct review pass described underneath so you can mark your own work.

The one idea

Track changes is a conversation, not graffiti on your document — but it only reads that way if you're looking at it through the right view. Simple Markup shows you the document roughly as it would read if every change were accepted — use it to sanity-check the shape of the whole thing. All Markup shows you every insertion, deletion, and comment balloon individually — use it to adjudicate, change by change. Confusing the two jobs is where the fear comes from: read in All Markup and the document looks unreadable forever; decide in Simple Markup and you're accepting things you never actually saw.

Once you're in the right view for the right job, the discipline is simple: accept or reject per change, and treat different kinds of change differently. Prose tightening is taste — accept it freely once you've read it and it holds up. A change to a fact — a figure, a date, a quantity — is not yours to wave through on taste; it's the proof-pass's territory, and it gets verified against the source before any button gets pressed. And the document doesn't leave review until the tracked history leaves with it: Accept All and Reject All are not review, they're the absence of it.

Why it matters to a manager

A submission for GDM or a senior audience is signed off with your name on the review, and its numbers — volumes, prices, dates someone downstream will act on — are the whole point of the document. When a tracked edit changes a volume figure from 110 to 100, that single character is worth more scrutiny than every comma fixed in the same pass, and it looks identical to a comma fix in the review pane if you're not slowing down for it. A manager who treats every tracked change as equally low-stakes will, sooner or later, sign off a figure nobody actually checked — because "someone edited it" is not the same as "someone verified it," and the tracked-changes pane doesn't tell you which one happened. That verification is your job, not the pane's, and it's exactly the fast, confident review this course is meant to give you: read the markup once, adjudicate it properly, and hand the document onward knowing what actually changed underneath it.

The bad habit this undoes Clicking Accept All to make the red disappear — which silently accepts the unverified 110 → 100 fact change along with every comma fix in the same sweep, because Accept All cannot tell the difference between them and neither can you once you've clicked it. Its mirror-image habit is just as costly: fear-freeze, reviewing permanently in All Markup because switching feels like losing your place, until the document is a wall of colour nobody can actually read and nothing gets adjudicated at all.

How to do it

Open Scenario D's reviewed submission and run the director's pass in two clean stages, each in the view built for it.

First, read once in Simple Markup (Review tab → Display for Review dropdown). This shows you the document approximately as it will read once every change lands — the tightened exec summary reads as tightened prose, the deleted paragraph is simply absent. Your only job in this pass is orientation: what changed, roughly, and does the resulting shape make sense. Don't accept or reject anything yet — Simple Markup is for reading, not deciding.

Second, switch to All Markup and adjudicate change by change, working through the reviewing pane (Review → Reviewing Pane) so nothing is hiding off-screen. The exec-summary tightening is prose — read each edit on its merits and accept or reject it individually as you agree or don't; this is taste, and taste moves fast. When you reach the volume change, 110 → 100, stop. Do not accept or reject it yet — hold it. This is exactly the figure a proof pass exists for: the money sweep inside a document, the one number worth checking against the source before a button gets pressed on it, the same way a 60-second proof pass checks every digit in an email before it sends. Go find where 110 came from — the original brief, the source spreadsheet, whatever this course's family treats as ground truth for that number — and only accept or reject once you know which figure is actually correct. If the source says 100, accept it. If the source still says 110, reject the edit and flag it back to whoever made it; either way, the decision follows the source, not the pane.

The deleted paragraph gets the same treatment as the volume change, minus the source lookup: read what's gone, decide whether it deserved to go, and accept or reject on that judgement — not on the reflex that someone else's deletion must be right, and not on nostalgia for a paragraph you liked. A deletion is a proposal, not a fact; judge it, don't mourn it.

Work Merch's five comments the same change-by-change way: answer the two questions, resolve the two style quibbles once you've actioned or dismissed them, and treat "check this with Supply Chain" as what it is — not a comment to close, but a real follow-up to send, with the comment left open until that reply comes back.

Finish by proving the document is actually clean. Open the reviewing pane and confirm it shows zero remaining changes and zero unresolved comments — not a quick glance at the document body, the pane's own count. Then run File → Info → Check for Issues → Inspect Document and confirm it reports no tracked changes or comments left in the file. A document that leaves review with hidden markup still riding along — visible the moment whoever receives it next opens it in All Markup themselves — is a seam-leak of documents: the review looked finished from where you were sitting, and wasn't.

Try it for real

Take a document of your own with live tracked changes and comments on it — or ask a colleague for one if nothing's currently in your queue. Read it once in Simple Markup for orientation, then switch to All Markup and adjudicate every change individually: prose on its merits, any change to a number, date, or fact held and checked against its source before you touch accept or reject. Self-check when you're done: open the reviewing pane and confirm it reads zero changes and zero open comments, then run Inspect Document and confirm it agrees. If either one still shows something, the review isn't finished — it only looked that way.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — adjudicate a reviewed document's tracked changes and comments — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: every fact-class change gets verified against its source before it's accepted, and the document leaves with zero tracked history riding along.

Rung 2 · Aldi:GPT — it triages the list, it never verifies a fact

Aldi:GPT can't see your document, so it can only work from a change list you paste as text; it can sort that list into taste, fact, and structure, but it has no access to your source data, so it can flag a fact change for verification — it can never confirm one is correct.

Check: the signature failure at this rung is the model adjudicating the fact itself, not just classifying it — it may correctly flag the 110 → 100 and 14 March → 21 March changes as FACT-class, but it must never declare either figure "correct" or "probably fine." It has no source to check against; classification is the whole deliverable here, verification never is. If it says anything like "100 looks right," that's the model overstepping, not a finding — go verify against the source yourself regardless of what it says.

Rung 3 · M365 Copilot in Word — it summarises, the review pane keeps the count

Copilot in Word can read the open document and summarise what changed, which saves you re-reading everything from scratch — but a summary is prose, and prose smooths over exactly the details a review depends on. The reviewing pane's own count of changes and comments is the fact-check on the summary, not the other way round.

Type this prompt into Copilot (in Word)

Check: read the summary against the reviewing pane's own count, not against your memory of skimming the document. The narrative-smoothing failure at this rung is a summary that says "minor edits throughout" while the pane shows a deleted paragraph and a changed volume figure sitting right there — counts beat vibes. If the summary's tone undersells what the pane counts, trust the pane and go verify every fact-class change yourself before touching accept/reject.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — it audits and reports, it never accepts a change

An agent with file access can script a revision auditor over the .docx that lists every tracked change by author and kind, and flags anything touching digits or figures as needing human verification — but report-only is the whole point: the auditor must never touch accept or reject itself.

Type this prompt into GitHub Copilot

Check: run it against Scenario D's spec and confirm the volume change (110 → 100) lands in the FACT-CHECK NEEDED list — if it doesn't catch a plain digit change, the digit-detection is broken and the auditor isn't safe to trust. Then check the script itself, not just its output: it must contain no call that accepts, rejects, or otherwise touches the tracked changes. An auditor that quietly resolves anything on your behalf has failed the one job it had.

The point of the ladder From "AI triages a pasted list" to "AI audits and reports on the file itself" — the deliverable never changes: every fact-class change verified against its source before a human accepts or rejects it, and the document leaves review with zero tracked history riding along. What protects you at every rung is that same check, not the tool. Learn to run the two-view discipline by hand first, so you can tell a real verification from a model's confident guess at every rung above it.
