Excel to a High Standard · Lesson 0015

Conditional formatting: make numbers signal

Tied to your mission: a column of margins is just numbers until something makes the weak ones shout. Conditional formatting colours cells by their own value, so the low-margin lines light up red and the strong ones go green — your eye finds the problem before you've read a single figure.

Practice workbook · open this first

Open the course workbook and click the Conditional format tab — a list of products with a plain Margin % column is waiting. Right now it's a wall of numbers; your job is to make it signal.

The one idea

Conditional formatting applies a format — a fill, a colour, an icon — only when a cell meets a rule. Two flavours cover most reporting work: a colour scale paints a whole range as a heat map (low = red, high = green), and a highlight rule flags cells that cross a line you set (margin under 20%, say). The format is live — change the number and the colour follows.

Why it matters to a manager

You skim; you don't audit. A range that turns weak margins red is read in a second and trusted, because the colour is the number — exactly the fast-read judgement a GDM submission needs to survive first contact with a senior audience. It also protects you when you're reviewing someone else's workbook: an outlier you'd miss scanning a long column can't hide once the rule paints it, so you spend your limited review time where the data tells you to look, not row by row.

The bad habit this undoes Manually shading cells yellow and red by hand after eyeballing the column — then having to redo all of it when the numbers change, and quietly getting it wrong when a value crosses the line and you forget to recolour. A rule does the judging every time the data moves; hand-shading freezes a snapshot that goes stale — exactly the kind of silent error you're meant to be able to spot when reviewing someone's work.

How to do it

On the Conditional format tab, select B2:B11. Go to Home → Conditional Formatting → Colour Scales and pick the green-white-red scale for an instant heat map. Then, with the range still selected, Home → Conditional Formatting → Highlight Cells Rules → Less Than, type 0.20, and choose a red fill. Low margins now carry two signals at once.

Keep it honest: format the value, never a hand-typed copy of it. And use Manage Rules (same menu) to see, reorder or delete rules — that panel is where messy sheets get tidied, and where you'll look first when reviewing a workbook someone else built.

Try it for real

Apply both rules on the Conditional format tab, then stress-test them: type 0.19 into a cell that was green and watch it flip red on the threshold. Open Manage Rules and confirm your two rules are listed and apply to B2:B11. That panel is your audit trail.
