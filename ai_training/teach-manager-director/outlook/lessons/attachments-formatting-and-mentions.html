Outlook to a High Standard · Lesson 0007

Attachments, formatting & @mentions: send it right first time

Tied to your mission: what you say in a send is the communication course's job — tone, tightening, the right words. This lesson stops one level below that, at the mechanics: is the file actually there, can the receiver do anything with the numbers you sent, and does an @mention land on someone who can see it. A perfectly worded email with a missing attachment is still a failed send.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario F — The naked send — a drafted reply to Sam Lee that is one click from going out with three mechanical problems still in it.

The one idea

A send is an artefact, not just a message, and it has mechanics that either work or don't — independent of how well it's written. Three mechanics, three failure modes, each one costing the other side a full round-trip back to you: the attachment must be present, and named for the receiver's filing, not Book1 (3).xlsx; the numbers must travel as data — a real table or pasted text the receiver can copy out of, never a screenshot of your workbook; and an @mention is an internal instrument — inside your org it notifies and assigns, in an external mail it's decoration the reader cannot act on, because they don't have an account on your tenant for it to land in.

None of this is about wording. A supplier who gets "confirming as attached" with nothing attached doesn't care how polite the sentence was — they reply asking where the file is, and the round-trip that should have taken ten seconds to prevent now takes a day.

Why it matters to a manager

You don't build most of what you send, but you sign your name to it, and a chunk of your day is submissions and delegations going out to GDM and senior audiences, or down to the person who now owns a task. A number that arrives as a screenshot in one of those threads can't be copied into anyone's tracker — someone has to email you back and ask for the real file, and the "sent it Thursday" you told your own director quietly becomes "chased again Friday". An @mention aimed at a colleague who isn't actually on the send reads, later, as an instruction you gave that nobody received — which is a worse look in a submission than in a quick internal note. Three checks before you click send cost you ten seconds each; skipping them costs a round-trip you have to explain upward, not just sideways.

The bad habit this undoes Writing "as attached" (or "the sheet you need is below") and clicking send without checking the paperclip icon actually holds a file. Pasting a screenshot of the workbook where a real table should sit, because it was faster than selecting and copying the range. And @mentioning a colleague inside an external email — "@Alex can you make sure the DC slots line up" — when Alex isn't on To or CC at all, so the mention does nothing except sit there looking like an instruction that was never sent anywhere. All three fail silently: the mail leaves your outbox looking finished, and you don't find out otherwise until the reply asking what happened lands in your inbox.

How to do it

Open Scenario F in the practice pack and read the drafted reply to Sam Lee. It has exactly three mechanical problems, and the pack's own annotation names them: no attachment on the draft, the week-by-week split is a screenshot of a spreadsheet, and Alex is mentioned but absent from the recipient lines.

Fix all three, in order:

1. Attach and name the sheet. The volume confirmation Sam is waiting on has to actually be on the mail as a file, and its filename has to say what it is to someone filing it later — RiceCrackers_VolumeConfirmation.xlsx, not whatever Excel called it when you saved it. Say what's attached in the body only once you can see the paperclip.

2. Convert the screenshot to a pasted table. The week-by-week split currently exists as a picture of a spreadsheet — Sam cannot select a number out of an image. Select the actual range in your workbook, copy it, and paste it into the body as a real table (or as plain text rows) so the numbers are data he can lift straight into his own sheet.

3. Move Alex from a dead @mention to CC. "@Alex can you make sure the DC slots line up" does nothing in an external mail — Alex isn't on the send at all, so the mention notifies no one. He owns the DC condition this confirmation depends on, so he belongs on CC, addressed by name in the body instead of an @mention: "Alex — can you confirm the DC slots line up with this split." This is the same owes/owns routing test from Threading hygiene: Alex owns a stake without owing Sam anything, so CC, not To, and never a mention that only works inside your own tenant.

Check your rebuilt send against the pack's "A correct send" box under Scenario F — that's the reconciliation check for this lesson.

Check yourself

Answer these before moving on — immediate feedback below each.

1. A draft says "confirming as attached" but the paperclip icon shows no file. What's the actual failure here?

2. Why does a screenshot of a spreadsheet fail where a pasted table succeeds, even though both "show" the same numbers?

3. An email to an external supplier includes "@Alex can you check the DC slots" but Alex is not on To or CC. What should happen to Alex?

Try it for real

Find your own last real send that included, or was supposed to include, an attachment. Run the three checks against it: was the attachment actually present, and would its filename mean anything to the person filing it a month from now — not Book1 (3).xlsx? Did any numbers travel as a screenshot anywhere in that thread, rather than as a table or text the receiver could copy? And if the send mentioned anyone with @, were they actually on To or CC — or was the mention decoration in an external mail? You should be able to say all three passed, or name the one that failed and what it actually cost — a chased reply, a retyped table, an instruction nobody received.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — catch the three mechanical failures before you send — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: attachment present and sensibly named, data travelling as data, and @mentions used only where the mentioned person is actually on the send.

Rung 2 · Aldi:GPT — it can reason about your draft's text, not its attachments

Aldi:GPT cannot see what is or isn't attached to your draft — that's the whole teaching point of this rung. Paste the draft text and ask it to list every claim the text makes about the send's mechanics, then you go and verify each one yourself.

Type this prompt into Aldi:GPT

Check: Aldi:GPT can extract the claims — "as attached", "pasted below", "@Alex" — but it cannot verify a single one, because it never sees your attachment pane or your recipient list. The checklist it hands back is only as good as the walk-through you do against the real draft afterwards. A learner who lets GPT "confirm" the attachment is there has missed the rung entirely: nothing it said makes the file exist.

Rung 3 · M365 Copilot / Outlook built-ins — know which is doing the catching

Be precise about which tool does what here. Outlook's own attachment reminder — a built-in, not Copilot — scans for trigger words like "attached" and nudges you if it doesn't find a file. Copilot's actual move in Outlook is different: it can summarise an attachment you've received before you forward it on, not audit one you're about to send.

Type this prompt into Copilot (in Outlook, with a received attachment open)

Check: the built-in attachment reminder only fires on trigger words. "The sheet you need is below" or "find this useful" sails straight through with no file attached and no warning — the reminder never saw a promise it recognised. Neither the reminder nor Copilot checks your @mentions against the recipient list, and neither converts a screenshot into a table for you. The three mechanics checks from this lesson stay entirely yours to run, every time.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — it builds a pre-send linter from scratch

An agent with file access can build you a real tool, but it cannot see your live Outlook draft or attachment pane — it works from a text export of the draft and a manifest you supply yourself.

Type this prompt into GitHub Copilot

Check: run it on Scenario F's draft, its (empty) attachment manifest, and a recipient list of Sam only — it must print exactly three flags: no matching attachment, a screenshot marker, and an @mention for a name absent from To/CC. Then run it again on your corrected version — the real filename in the manifest, the screenshot language removed, Alex added to the CC list and referred to by name instead of @mention — and it must print "clean". A linter that still flags the corrected send, or stays quiet on the original, is broken; a linter that flags clean sends teaches people to stop reading its output, which defeats the point of building it.

The point of the ladder Same deliverable at every rung — a send whose attachment exists, whose numbers are copyable, and whose @mentions land on someone actually on the send. What changes is how much of the checking you hand over; what protects you is always the same three checks, run against the real draft rather than a description of it. Craft first, so you know what "clean" is supposed to look like before you trust a tool to say so.
