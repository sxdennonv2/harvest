Outlook to a High Standard · Lesson 0008

Invites that get accepted: the agenda is the meeting

Tied to your mission: your day is a heavy flow of meetings, and your mission's coordination line is "turn meetings into tracked, owned follow-ups" — the assumption underneath that is that the meeting already happened and now needs cleaning up afterwards. This lesson is earlier than that: it's about whether the meeting should have needed cleaning up at all. What gets said once everyone's in the room is a different lesson entirely — this one is only about the invite that gets people there prepared, or doesn't. "Send invites and emails that produce decisions, not another meeting" — that mission line starts here.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario D — The agenda-less invite — a real invite that went out for a meeting that matters, with the correct rebuild given underneath so you can mark your own work.

The one idea

An invite is a contract, not a calendar hold. It earns acceptance — and attendance, and preparation — by carrying five things: the decision named in the subject, a numbered agenda where every item has an owner, the prep the attendees need to bring, a duration that matches what the agenda actually needs, and the outcome stated — "we leave with X". Miss any of the five and you haven't scheduled a meeting, you've scheduled a mystery.

A "Catch up" invite is that mystery: an hour borrowed from three calendars with nothing attached that says what it's for. Attendees can't prepare for a meeting that gave them nothing to prepare, so the meeting spends its first twenty minutes discovering its own agenda out loud — the most expensive way an agenda can get written.

Why it matters to a manager

You don't just attend these meetings, you convene most of them — and every one you send sets the tone for how your reports run theirs. Scenario D's meeting exists because a Friday deadline does: if the invite doesn't say so, the room doesn't treat Thursday's hour as the last chance to hit it, and the mystery-hold habit copies itself downward when your own calendar models it. A manager who sends contract-shaped invites gets shorter meetings that end in a decision — which protects the thing you can't get back, other people's calendar time — while "Catch up" produces a full hour that ends in "let's take this offline", meaning a second meeting, chaired by you, to replace the one that just happened.

The bad habit this undoes The mystery hold: subject "Catch up", sixty minutes because that's the calendar app's default, body reading "a few things to cover ahead of the promo". Nobody named is asked to bring anything, nobody owns a line item, and there's no sentence anywhere describing what the room leaves with. The meeting doesn't fail loudly — it just quietly spends its first third finding out why it was booked.

How to do it

Rebuild Scenario D's invite against the five-part contract. Start with the subject: "Catch up" names nothing, so replace it with the decision itself — Promo fortnight: confirm volumes & delivery day. Anyone scanning a calendar now knows what this hour is for before they've opened it.

Next, turn "various bits to cover" into a numbered agenda, and read the thread for the facts rather than inventing them: (1) confirm 110 cases/week — the extra 40 on top of the usual 70; (2) Harvest Lane deliveries move Monday → Wednesday for the two promo weeks; (3) is Popcorn 10pk in or out of the promo. Each line gets an owner — you own confirming the volume figure back to Sam, Alex owns the delivery-day condition, whoever holds the promo list owns the Popcorn call. Name the prep each owner needs to bring (Alex needs the DC capacity sheet in front of him), cut the duration from sixty minutes to thirty — a three-line agenda doesn't need an hour — and close with the outcome: "we leave with the Friday confirmation sendable".

One mechanic falls straight out of the agenda: who's Required and who's Optional follows item ownership. Alex owns item 2, so he's required; if the Popcorn owner is a fourth person only relevant to item 3, they can be Optional with a note that they're only needed for that line — the agenda decides the attendee list, not the other way round.

Check yourself

Answer these before moving on — immediate feedback below each.

1. Which of these turns a calendar hold into a contract?

2. Scenario D's meeting runs 60 minutes. Once the agenda is three owned items, what should the duration do?

3. What decides whether an attendee is Required or Optional?

Try it for real

Take your next real recurring meeting and rewrite its invite to the five-part contract: decision in the subject, numbered agenda with an owner per item, prep named, duration matched to the agenda, outcome stated. Self-check when you're done: could someone who can't attend still contribute their item asynchronously, because the agenda told them exactly what was needed from them and by when? If the answer is yes for every item on the agenda, you don't have a meeting that needs everyone in a room — cancel it and collect the answers by email instead. A meeting that survives that test earns the room; one that doesn't was never really a meeting, it was an agenda wearing a calendar hold.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — turn a mystery hold into a contract-shaped invite — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: every agenda line must trace back to a real fact, and every line must have an owner.

Aldi:GPT can't see your mailbox or the thread, so give it the flabby invite and the facts yourself; it drafts the rebuild, and you check nothing snuck in that you didn't give it.

Every agenda line must trace to a fact you actually gave it; if you see a line you didn't supply, cut it. AOB is exactly where mystery meetings regrow.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — it builds the recurring pack, it can't invent an owner

An agent with file access can build the whole recurring-meeting pack from a meeting-definition file — agenda template, minutes template, invite text — but it cannot send anything or read your live mailbox.

Also confirm the minutes template's action table carries both owner and date columns: a minutes template without owners is a transcript, not an instrument.

The point of the ladder From "AI rebuilds the invite you paste in" to "AI builds the whole recurring pack" — the deliverable never changes: a contract-shaped invite where every agenda line has a named owner. What protects you at every rung is that same trace-to-a-fact check, not the tool. Learn to write the contract by hand first, so you can catch the padding an AI adds back in and the owner it's tempted to invent.
