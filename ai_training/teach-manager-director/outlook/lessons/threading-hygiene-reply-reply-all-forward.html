Outlook to a High Standard · Lesson 0006

Threading hygiene: reply, reply-all, forward

Tied to your mission: what you say in a reply is the communication course's job — tone, tightening, the right words. This lesson is the mechanics that come before any of that: who hears it. Get the routing wrong and the best-written reply in the world does damage the moment you click send.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario C — The promo thread — a four-message thread that has reached Wednesday evening, waiting for you to decide its next send.

The one idea

A thread is a room. Reply speaks to whoever asked. Reply-all fills the room — everyone currently in it hears you. Forward moves the walls — it opens the room to someone who wasn't in it before. The one unforgivable routing error is letting an internal conversation cross the external seam: a supplier reading something that was only ever meant for a colleague.

Route every send by asking two questions of each name on the thread: who owes the next action (that person goes on To), and who owns a stake in the outcome without owing the action themselves (that person goes on CC). Everyone else leaves the room — they don't go on the send at all.

Why it matters to a manager

You rarely draft these threads yourself, but you're copied into them, and you inherit threads your team started the moment you're asked to weigh in or sign off. The internal side of a thread like this is where the real position lives — capacity constraints, how much room there actually is to give a supplier, what's still under discussion — and none of that is written to survive a reply-all or a whole-trail forward crossing to the other side of the table. When it's your name reviewing or sending the next message on a mixed thread, the routing call is yours to catch: a seam leak on a thread you touched doesn't stop being your problem because someone else typed the first message.

The bad habit this undoes Reply-all as the default reflex — "better everyone's kept in the loop" — and forwarding an entire trail to an external contact without pruning it first. Both feel efficient in the moment and both fail silently: nothing crashes, the mail just arrives somewhere it shouldn't, and you don't find out until it costs you.

How to do it

Open Scenario C in the practice pack and read the thread to Wednesday evening. Sam Lee (Harvest Lane) needs Friday's volume confirmation. Alex Moreau (Supply Chain, internal) has already told you the DC condition — Wednesday delivery — and separately asked an open question about Popcorn 10pk that isn't on his sheet.

Route the Wednesday-evening send in three separate decisions:

1. The confirmation to Sam is a reply on the supplier thread — To: Sam, CC: Alex (he owns the DC condition your confirmation depends on). Not reply-all: reply-all on this thread would carry Alex's internal capacity discussion straight to Harvest Lane.

2. Alex's capacity discussion — the internal back-and-forth about whether the DC can absorb it — must never travel onto the Sam thread. It stays where it is: internal.

3. The open Popcorn question doesn't belong buried inside the promo thread at all. It gets its own new thread, its own subject, addressed to whoever actually owns the promo list — not tacked onto whatever thread happens to be open.

Mark your routing against the pack's "A correct routing" box under Scenario C — that's the reconciliation check for this lesson.

Try it for real

Find your own most recent mixed internal/external thread — one with at least one colleague and one supplier or outside contact on it. Audit it: does anything internal (a capacity note, a margin figure, "how much room we actually have") appear anywhere below the last message that went to the external party? Check every name currently on To and CC — for each one, can you say in one word whether they're there because they owe the next action or own a stake in it? If you can't answer that for a name, they shouldn't be on the send.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — route a mixed internal/external thread correctly — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: does anything internal cross the external seam, and can every name on To/CC be justified by owes or owns?

The point of the ladder Same deliverable at every rung — a correctly routed send, nothing internal crossing the external seam. What changes is how much of the reasoning you hand over; what protects you is always the same check: justify every name on To/CC by owes or owns, and scan below the last external send for anything that started life internal. Craft first, so you can supervise what the tool hands you.
