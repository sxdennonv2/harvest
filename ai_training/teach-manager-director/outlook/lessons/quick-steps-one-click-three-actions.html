Outlook to a High Standard · Lesson 0011

Quick Steps: one click, three actions

Tied to your mission: your day is trends and risks in data someone else prepared, submissions for GDM and senior audiences, and a heavy flow of meetings — the same ritual repeated across dozens of Fridays a year is real hours you don't have. Quick Steps collapse that into one click, without handing the moment away to a rule that fires when nobody's looking.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and read Scenario E — The Friday ritual. It names the three actions you repeat every Friday — move to the reporting folder, flag for Monday, reply with the acknowledgement template — and states what a correct automation of it must contain.

The one idea

The line that decides which automation tool you reach for isn't effort, it's attention. A rule fires unattended — perfect for mail that needs no human eyes before it's filed. A Quick Step is a one-click macro that only fires when a person decides to click it — the right tool whenever the ritual needs a glance first.

Scenario E's acknowledgement ("Received, will review Monday — flag anything unusual now") can only be honest if a human looked at the report before it went out. That makes it a Quick Step, not a rule. Choosing which side of that line a ritual sits on is the actual skill here; chaining the three actions together once you've chosen is just clicking through a dialog.

Why it matters to a manager

The weekly sell-through report lands every Friday and starts the same six-minute ritual: file it, flag it for Monday's read-through, tell Finance it's been seen. In a normal week that's routine. In a week where you're due to present trends to GDM on Monday, that report might contain the one number that changes what you walk in and say. An acknowledgement that goes out automatically, unread, says "flag anything unusual now" while guaranteeing nobody has — and the first person to notice the gap in front of senior audiences is you, live. A Quick Step keeps the glance in the loop on every one of those Fridays, spike weeks included, while still collapsing the three actions to a single click so it doesn't eat into a day that's already full of meetings.

The bad habit this undoes Two flavours of the same mistake. The first is doing the three actions by hand, fifty-two Fridays a year — dragging the mail to the folder, right-clicking to flag it, then composing the same two-line reply from memory (or worse, retyping it slightly differently each time). The second, once someone discovers automation, is over-automating: building a rule that auto-replies and auto-files the report the moment it arrives, unread. The week the report comes in broken — wrong numbers, a supplier's figures missing — the acknowledgement has already gone out saying "will review Monday, flag anything unusual" to a report nobody has glanced at. The acknowledgement is now a lie the mailbox tells on your behalf.

How to do it

Build Scenario E's Quick Step from scratch. In Outlook, on the Home tab, open the Quick Steps gallery and choose Create New (or "New Quick Step" → "Custom"). This opens the "Edit Quick Step" dialog — walk it exactly:

Name it. Type a name that describes the ritual, not the mechanics — "Friday report received" reads better in the gallery than "Move + flag + reply."

Add the first action. Under "Actions," the first row is already a dropdown — click Choose an Action and pick Move to Folder, then pick your reporting folder from the list.

Add the second action. Click Add Action to add a second row. Choose an Action again — this time Flag Message, and set the flag to Monday (or "next business day," whichever your version offers) so it surfaces in your to-do list for Monday's read-through.

Add the third action. Add Action a third time. Choose Reply, then click the small edit icon that appears to open the template body, and type the acknowledgement text: Received, will review Monday — flag anything unusual now. Save that reply text into the action.

Optionally, assign a shortcut key from the dropdown near the top of the dialog (Ctrl+Shift+1 through 9) so the whole chain fires from the keyboard, not just a click. Click Finish.

Check it against the pack's box: one Quick Step, three actions, fired by one click on the arrived mail — and you can say out loud why this is a Quick Step and not a rule (the reply only goes out because you clicked it after looking at the report, not the instant it landed).

Try it for real

Find one repeated multi-action sequence in your own mailbox — something you do the same way, in the same order, often enough that you could name its cadence (weekly, on every supplier confirmation, whatever it is). Apply the attention test: does any step need your eyes on the content before it happens? If yes, build it as a Quick Step, walking the same dialog as above. If no — every action is safe to fire the instant the mail arrives — it belongs to a rule instead (covered elsewhere in this course). Check: you can name which side of the boundary your ritual fell on, and say the one sentence why — not "because it felt right," but "because step two needs me to have read it first" (or "because nothing here needs a human glance").

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — turn a repeated multi-action ritual into an automation — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: does the chosen tool respect the attention boundary, and does the acknowledgement (if there is one) keep the ask that does the work?

Rung 2 · Aldi:GPT — it designs the automation, you verify what's real

Aldi:GPT can't see your mailbox, so it can only design the automation from what you describe — which tool (rule vs Quick Step), which actions, in what order. You place it and verify every proposed action actually exists in Outlook.

Check: the signature failure at this rung is capability confusion — Aldi:GPT prescribing something Outlook can't chain (Quick Steps can't run scripts or evaluate conditional logic), or recommending a rule with an unattended auto-reply where the ritual actually needs a glance first. Verify every proposed action against the real Quick Step action list in Outlook (or the Microsoft Quick Steps page) before you build anything from it.

Rung 3 · M365 Copilot — it drafts the template text and the arrival summary, not the automation itself

Be honest about the boundary: Copilot in Outlook cannot create Quick Steps or rules — there is no "build this automation" button it can press. Its real rung-3 move is drafting the reusable acknowledgement text in context, and summarising the report once it arrives, so you glance faster before you click the Quick Step you already built.

Type this prompt into Copilot (in Outlook)

Check: the failure at this rung is a courteous acknowledgement that drops the one line doing the actual work. Read the draft back and confirm the "flag anything unusual now" ask survives — a polished "thanks, received!" that loses that line is a worse template than the one you started with, however well it reads.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — it builds the ritual kit, not the live automation

GitHub Copilot is an agent with file access but no connection to your live mailbox — it cannot configure a Quick Step or a rule inside Outlook for you. What it can do, from a plain-text description of the ritual, is build the kit around it: the reply-template file, a setup card with the exact click-path to build the Quick Step yourself, and — only where genuinely scriptable — the folder/rule scaffolding as PowerShell or Graph steps clearly marked for IT or you to run.

Check: the kit must not claim to have installed anything into Outlook itself — it can only produce files and instructions. Anything it genuinely can't do (create the Quick Step, click through the dialog) must appear as an explicit human step on the setup card, never silently vanish. An automation kit that overstates its own reach is exactly how an acknowledgement starts lying — the same failure as the over-automated rule, just moved one rung up the ladder.

The point of the ladder From "AI designs the automation" to "AI builds the ritual kit around it" — the deliverable is the same one-click Quick Step, built by a human hand at every rung, because none of these tools can reach into your mailbox and click Finish for you. What protects you is the same check throughout: does the tool respect the attention boundary, and does the acknowledgement keep the line that does the work? Craft first, so you can supervise.
