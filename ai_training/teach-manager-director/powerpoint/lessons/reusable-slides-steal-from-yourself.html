PowerPoint to a High Standard · Lesson 0008

Reusable slides: steal from yourself

Tied to your mission: the decks that land on your desk for review keep needing the same fixes under the same Thursday deadline — and the fastest way to hand back a deck that looks right is to not let the team rebuild from nothing every time. Somewhere in last quarter's files is a good supplier-scorecard slide. This lesson turns "somewhere, in some deck" into one named place you or your team can actually find it, so the review cycle is spent on the message, not on slides that are almost-but-not-quite right.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario D — The template job: a supplier-day deck needed by Thursday, the corporate template ready to go, and the usual shortcut — copying last quarter's deck and overtyping it — sitting right there as the obvious wrong move.

The one idea

If you've built the slide twice, it's a library asset. The supplier-scorecard slide, the range-summary slide, the timeline slide — you have made a good version of each of these before, and you will need each of them again. The fix isn't remembering harder or searching old decks faster; it's maintaining one personal library deck, each slide already sitting on the corporate template, so "I need a scorecard slide" means open the library, not open March.

Reuse is two decisions, not one: which slide, and how it pastes. Copy the slide from the library, paste it into the target deck, and choose the paste option deliberately. Use destination theme tells the slide to drop its old look and adopt whatever deck it just landed in — almost always correct, because the target deck owns the look and the slide is there for its content and structure, not its formatting. Keep source formatting is the exception, not the reflex: reach for it only when the slide's look is the content — a competitor's slide you're critiquing verbatim, a supplier's own deck page you're quoting. Get the paste choice backwards and the slide's old template rides into your new deck anyway; you've just moved the contamination one click later.

Why it matters to a manager

A supplier-day deck is judged in the room, slide by slide, and a rebuilt-from-memory scorecard slide is never quite the one that went up in March — a column dropped, a colour that doesn't match this quarter's, a layout that's almost-but-not-quite the one the GDM room has seen four times already. Almost-alike slides are what make a deck feel amateur in front of a senior audience, even when every number on it is right — and as the person presenting or signing it off, that's your credibility in the room, not just the analyst's. Reusing from last quarter's deck instead of a maintained library doesn't fix this either — it just imports that deck's masters, colours, and stray fixes along with the one slide you wanted, one paste at a time, until the deck you're about to present is quietly carrying three old templates and nobody can say why the fonts don't match. Catching that before it reaches your audience, or making sure your own library never lets it happen, is the review-fast payoff of this lesson.

The bad habit this undoes The archaeology workflow: "I made a good version of this in some deck in March," followed by twenty minutes of digging through old files, and — because the search rarely turns up the exact slide fast enough — a rebuild-from-memory anyway, which is the very thing the twenty minutes were meant to avoid. Its close cousin is lifting slides from whatever old deck you did find with keep-source-formatting as the automatic choice, so each borrowed slide drags its origin deck's template in with it — three old templates riding into one new deck, one slide at a time, exactly the way Scenario C's franken-deck got built in the first place.

How to do it

Seed the library, then run the reuse drill once each way so you can see the difference the paste choice makes.

Seed the library. Create a new file, library-deck.pptx, built on the corporate template (New → from template — the same move the template lesson covers). From Scenario D's supplier-day build, find your two best reusable slides — a supplier-scorecard slide and a range-summary or timeline slide, whichever two you'd actually want back next quarter — and move them into library-deck.pptx. Rename each slide in the Slides pane (right-click → Rename Slide, or just keep the title placeholder descriptive) so a future you can identify it at a glance without opening it. That file is now the one place "a good scorecard slide" lives.

The reuse drill — paste it right. Open a fresh deck on the corporate template. In library-deck.pptx, right-click your scorecard slide in the Slides pane and Copy. Switch to the fresh deck, right-click where it should land, and under Paste Options choose Use Destination Theme (the icon that looks like a paintbrush matching the target). Watch what happens: the slide's fonts, colours, and background switch to match the new deck immediately, while its layout and content structure survive the switch intact.

The reuse drill — paste it wrong, on purpose. Undo that paste, right-click again, and this time choose Keep Source Formatting. Compare the two results side by side. The keep-source version carries the library deck's fonts and colours into a deck that's supposed to be on the corporate template — exactly the mismatch a director notices without being able to say why. State out loud which paste was right for this slide and why: use-destination-theme was correct, because the target deck owns the look and the scorecard's value is its structure, not its palette. Keep-source-formatting would only be correct if the slide's specific look were the point being made — which a supplier-scorecard slide never is.

Try it for real

Open your own recent decks and find the slide type you've rebuilt the most this quarter — a scorecard, a timeline, a summary grid, whatever keeps recurring. Move your best version of it into a real library-deck.pptx of your own, on your actual corporate template. Then paste it into a fresh deck using Use Destination Theme. Check: the pasted slide's fonts and colours must match the new deck, not the library deck — open Slide Master view on the new deck and confirm the pasted slide claims a layout from the deck's own master, with no second master riding in behind it. If a second master shows up, the paste brought its origin template along; redo it with the destination-theme option.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — get your most-rebuilt slides into one maintained library and reuse them with the right paste choice — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: does the assembled deck end up with one master aboard, the destination's, and did the manifest of what to reuse get honoured exactly, not edited on the way through?

Rung 3 · M365 Copilot — it assembles from named slides, it inherits the paste problem

Copilot in PowerPoint can pull named slides or sections from an existing file into a new deck for you — but be honest about the boundary: it doesn't solve the formatting choice, it just makes one, and you still have to audit which one it made.

Copilot inherits the paste problem, it doesn't solve it; if a second master shows up, you still have to fix it by hand, the same way you would after your own wrong paste choice.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — it assembles by manifest, on the target's layouts, exactly as instructed

An agent with file access can write a python-pptx tool that takes your library deck plus a manifest — which slides, in what order — and assembles a target deck on the target template. It has no judgement about whether your manifest is a good idea; it only executes it.

Type this prompt into GitHub Copilot

Check: open the assembled deck's Slide Master view — every slide must claim a layout from the target's own master, with no second master aboard (the same one-master test from the masters lesson). Then check the manifest was honoured exactly: the slide order in the output must match the manifest's order line for line. A tool that "improves" the order, skips an entry, or adds one you didn't name is editorialising, not assembling — send it back.

The point of the ladder From "AI designs the library's contents" to "AI assembles the deck from a manifest" — the deliverable never changes: slides drawn from a library you actually maintain, landing on the target deck's own theme, in the order you specified. What protects you at every rung is the same audit — one master aboard, manifest honoured exactly — not the tool. Learn to make the paste choice by hand first, so no rung above it can quietly hand you a deck carrying someone else's template.
