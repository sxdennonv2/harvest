PowerPoint to a High Standard · Lesson 0004

Whitespace & hierarchy: less on the slide

Tied to your mission: a tidy slide and a readable slide are not the same thing. Builds on Lesson align-distribute-group: once boxes sit on a common edge with equal gaps, one font, and nothing stray rotated, the slide looks orderly — and is still a wall nobody can read in the three seconds a GDM audience actually gives it. This lesson is the mechanical layer above tidiness: how many things are on the slide, how big the one that matters is, and how much edge you leave around it. What the headline stat or title actually says is not this lesson's business — that's message-first slide craft; here it's purely size, position, and count, and it's exactly what you're checking for when you review a team deck fast and need to spot the slide doing two jobs.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario A — The untidy slide. Assume you've already run its tidy pass from the previous lesson: the three stat boxes share a top edge with equal gaps, one font family is in play, the rotated fourth box is grounded, and the title sits inside its placeholder. The slide is now aligned. It is still four boxes, a title, and a logo all fighting for the same three seconds — that crowd is your material.

The one idea

A slide gets about three seconds before the audience decides whether to keep reading, and every element on it — every box, bullet, chart, logo — is bidding for those three seconds. Visual hierarchy is a ration, not a layout preference: exactly one dominant element per slide (biggest, boldest, first-seen), everything else visibly smaller, and margins wide enough that the dominant element actually reads as dominant. Whitespace isn't empty space you failed to fill — it's the thing that lets the eye land on the one element that matters.

The mechanical test travels with you to every rung below: squint at the slide until you can't read the small print. Exactly one thing should still win. If two things are still competing, or nothing is, the ration hasn't been set yet.

Why it matters to a manager

The monthly category review slide is exactly the shape that breaks this rule: four stat boxes (revenue, margin, volume, waste), maybe a small chart, a couple of bullets, and the team logo — all present because each felt worth keeping, none demoted because deleting from a slide feels like losing analysis someone did. When you're the one presenting it upward, that slide is the one that stalls in front of a GDM audience; when you're reviewing a team's draft before it goes up, that slide is exactly what you should be catching and sending back. Either way, a senior audience skimming eight decks back to back does not read a slide like that; they resolve the tie by reading none of it, and the one number you actually needed them to remember — this month's headline stat — leaves the room with the same weight as the logo.

The bad habit this undoes The everything slide: four stat boxes, a chart, three bullets and a logo, all sized the same because nothing got cut. Tidying it — the previous lesson's align and distribute — makes the crowd neat, not smaller, and a neatly arranged crowd is still a crowd. The habit survives tidiness because the fix isn't alignment, it's subtraction: someone has to decide which element loses, and "shrink everything a little" is not that decision — it's the same crowd at 90%.

How to do it

Take Scenario A's tidied slide — title, three stat boxes on a common edge, the fourth box grounded — and ration it. First, name the dominant element: for a month-in-review slide, that's the headline stat (the one number the director must leave the room remembering), not the title and not the logo. Scale that one box up — larger type, more of the slide's area — until it is unmistakably the biggest thing on the canvas.

Second, demote or move everything that isn't it. The other stat boxes either shrink well below the headline's visual weight or move off this slide entirely onto a following one — a ration that keeps four boxes and just makes one of them bigger has not removed anything, and isn't a ration. The logo shrinks to a corner mark, not a competing shape. Third, widen the margins around the whole arrangement: pull every element in from the slide edge until the empty border is obviously a deliberate frame, not leftover space nobody filled.

Now run the squint test both ways. Squint at the original tidied slide: two or three boxes still compete for attention — the ration was never set. Squint at the rationed version: only the headline stat is still legible. That contrast, not a rule you memorised, is how you'll recognise the everything slide next time you're about to build one — or the next time a team member hands you one to review.

Check yourself

Answer these before moving on — immediate feedback below each.

2. You take an everything slide and shrink all six elements by 20% so they fit with a bit more edge space. Have you rationed it?

3. What is the squint test actually checking?

Try it for real

Pull up a real slide of yours that has three or more elements of similar visual weight — a review slide, a status update, anything with more than one stat box or bullet cluster. Name its one dominant element out loud before you touch anything, then resize and reposition so that element is unmistakably biggest, demote or relocate the rest, and widen the margins. Self-check: squint at the slide (or shrink the window until text blurs) — if you can still tell which element is the headline, and only that one, you've passed. If two survive the squint, the ration isn't finished; if none do, you shrank instead of rationing.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task at every rung — take a crowded, already-tidy slide down to one dominant element with real margins — and the same squint test proves it worked. What the AI is trusted with grows each rung; the element-count decision and the final check never leave your hands.

Rung 2 · Aldi:GPT — it proposes the ration, you judge it

Aldi:GPT can't see your slide, so hand it an inventory — every element and its rough size — and it proposes which to keep large, which to shrink, and which to move off entirely.

Rung 3 · M365 Copilot / Designer — it arranges what's there, it doesn't decide what stays

Designer will propose polished layouts for the crowded slide exactly as you built it — arranging eight elements beautifully is well within its reach, which is precisely the trap: the element-count decision has to happen before you invoke it.

Check: if you run Designer on the original eight-element slide first, expect a genuinely attractive everything-slide back — Designer optimises the arrangement of what you gave it, it will not tell you to remove five of your eight elements. Confirm the cut happened before Designer ran; if Designer's proposal still shows more than your dominant element plus light support, the elements were never removed, only rearranged.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — it audits the slide by script, it can't decide what's dominant

An agent with file access can write a python-pptx script that measures a slide objectively — element count, the largest element's share of slide area, margin violations — but it only reports the numbers; you still choose which element deserves the largest share.

Check: run it against the original eight-element spec first — it should flag both overcrowded (shape count) and no dominant element (largest shape under 25%, or two shapes close in size). Then run it against your rationed version — it should pass both checks. If either run's numbers don't match what your eye sees, the thresholds are wrong for this deck, not the slide — adjust the percentages in the prompt and rerun, so the audit stays auditable rather than an oracle you can't question.

The point of the ladder From "AI proposes the ration" to "AI audits the ration by script", the deliverable never changes: one dominant element, everything else demoted or moved, margins that hold. Every rung above manual craft will happily help you arrange or measure a crowded slide beautifully — none of them will decide, on your behalf, which element gets to win. Learn to ration by hand first, so a script's "25%" and "6 shapes" mean something to you instead of being numbers you take on faith.
