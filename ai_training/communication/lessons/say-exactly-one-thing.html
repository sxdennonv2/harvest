Communication to a High Standard · Lesson 0002

Say exactly one thing: killing ambiguity

Tied to your mission: the survey asked, in these words, for "professional writing for external stakeholders where ambiguity is removed". This is that skill, isolated. It sits next to Tighten in this theme but solves a different problem — a short email can still send the reader guessing, and a long one can be perfectly precise. Length and clarity are independent dials; this lesson is about the second one.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario I — The ambiguous instruction: a quick internal ask, dashed off between meetings, sent to your assistant. The facts box holds what you actually meant; the draft holds what you actually sent. Peek at the five ambiguities only after you've found your own.

The one idea

Every vague term in a draft is a decision you've silently delegated to the reader. "Soon", "the usual", "similar to last time", an unanchored "they" — each one reads fine to you because you're holding the missing fact in your head as you type. The reader holds none of it. They can't ask you what you meant before they act, so they guess, and the draft never tells you which guess they made.

The kill test is mechanical: for every noun-ish or time-ish term in a draft, ask "could two reasonable readers act differently on this?" If yes, the word doesn't survive — replace it with the value: a date, a number, a name. Not a clearer synonym for "soon", but the actual Friday. This is exacter words, not fewer of them; a tightened draft can still fail this test completely.

Why it matters at work

Scenario I's real version went out as five vague terms in four short lines. Two reasonable assistants read it and produce two different orders: one ships 30 units of the wrong sample line at a renegotiated price, chasing a courier that was never theirs to arrange. The email felt fine to send — it took ten seconds. Untangling which guess actually shipped, at $6.40 a unit, takes the rest of the afternoon. Multiply that by every quick internal ask the office sends, and ambiguity isn't a style problem — it's a silent, recurring cost paid by someone who never got to ask the question.

The bad habit this undoes Vagueness as speed: dashing off "sort the samples thing soon" because writing the actual values feels like it costs you time you don't have. It doesn't save time — it borrows it, at interest, from whoever has to guess. The ten seconds you saved writing "soon" instead of "by Friday" routinely costs ten times that untangling the wrong Friday, the wrong quantity, or the wrong price after the fact.

How to do it

Kill Scenario I's five ambiguities one at a time, against its facts box. Each fix is a substitution, not a rewrite — the sentence keeps its shape, one word changes:

1 · Which line. "The samples thing" names nothing. The facts box says the organic line — write "the organic line samples from GreenField".

2 · When. "Soon" is a different date to everyone who reads it. The facts box says by Friday — write "by Friday".

3 · How many. "The usual amount" assumes a usual that doesn't exist here. The facts box says 24 units — write "24 units".

4 · Which price. "Similar to last time" is an open invitation to quote a different number. The facts box says the May quote holds — write "the May quote, $6.40/unit, still applies".

5 · Who does what. "They said they'd sort the delivery" — an unanchored "they" and an unstated "what". The facts box says GreenField arranges the courier, because they offered — write "GreenField will arrange the courier, as they offered".

Reconcile: read the fixed draft against the facts box one more time. Every one of the five terms is now a value a reader could act on the same way twice — that's the check, not how the sentences sound.

Try it for real

Pull up the last real email where you delegated something — asked someone else to do a thing on your behalf. Run the two-readers test on every noun-ish or time-ish term in it, one at a time. The check: zero terms should survive where two reasonable readers could act differently, and every value you substitute in must come from an actual source you can point to — a quote, a calendar, a confirmed name — never from memory. If you can't source a value, that's a finding, not a lesson failure: it means the fact wasn't as settled as the draft implied, which is exactly what the proof pass is built to catch on the other end.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — Scenario I's draft with all five ambiguities killed — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: every substituted value traces to the facts box, and watch this skill's signature machine failure — a tool that finds the ambiguity but then, uninvited, resolves it with a guess of its own.

The point of the ladder From "it lists the gaps" to "it builds the linter" — the deliverable is always the same audit: every vague term named, every question it raises stated, and no value supplied by anything that doesn't hold your facts. What protects you at every rung is that substitution stays yours — craft the resolution by hand first, so you can tell a real fix from a confident guess when the machine offers one.
