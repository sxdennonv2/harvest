Communication to a High Standard · Lesson 0008

Lead with the answer: bottom line up front

Tied to your mission: the survey didn't ask for this by name, but it's underneath every mention of "preparing submissions for GDM or other audiences" — a submission that makes its reader hunt for the ask has already lost half its readers before the ask arrives. Whoever reads first reads in the order you wrote it, and most stop early. This lesson is about which sentence goes first, in anything you write.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario B — The buried escalation: a Friday decision and an October delist, told in the order it happened rather than the order it matters. Its "must survive" box is your marking scheme.

The one idea

Readers process a message in the order you wrote it, and most stop before the end — so the bottom line goes up front. Sentence one is the answer, the decision needed, or the headline fact; the reader should be able to act, or decide whether to keep reading, from your first two lines alone. Everything after that exists to support a conclusion the reader already holds, not to build toward one they don't have yet.

Background-first ordering feels natural because it's the order you lived it — "as you know, back in March…" — but it makes the reader carry every fact uninterpreted until the point finally lands. That's expensive when they finish the email, and fatal when they stop at paragraph two, which the evidence says most of them do. The inversion test is simple: cover everything after your first two lines. Could the reader act, or decide to read on, from what's left? If not, the ordering is backwards, regardless of how good the content further down turns out to be.

Why it matters at work

Scenario B is a Friday decision wearing a March history's clothes: by the time the reader reaches "if we don't respond by Friday… the line delists for Q4", they've already carried a paragraph of negotiation backstory that changes nothing about what they need to do today. A skimming director reads two lines and moves on — and if those two lines are "hope you had a good week… as you might remember…", the Friday deadline may as well not exist. Lead with the answer and the same reader gets the decision, the date and the stakes before they've decided whether to keep reading at all — which is the only point at which most readers decide.

The bad habit this undoes The narrative build-up — writing in the order you lived it: history, then context, then the complication, then finally the point. It feels honest, even thorough, because that's the order the story actually happened in. But the reader wasn't there for the history and doesn't need to relive it to act — they need the order that matches what matters to them, which is point first, support second, history only if they ask for it.

How to do it

Invert Scenario B in four passes:

1 · Find the real sentence one. Buried in paragraph two is the actual news: Coastal Co want a 4.2% increase on Cheddar Cheese 500g, and missing Friday delists the line for Q4. That's not supporting detail — that's the bottom line. Whatever sentence contains the decision-needed-by-date and its consequence is sentence one, no matter where it currently sits.

2 · Promote it, don't rewrite it. Move it, don't invent a new summary of it. The original already says "4.2% increase," "Friday," and "October production slot lapses" — pull those words forward rather than composing a fresh headline that might quietly claim more than the draft does.

3 · Rank what's left by whether it changes Friday's decision. The rationale (milk input costs, partially supported by market data) and the three options (accept / push back / switch supplier) support the decision — they stay, just after the bottom line, not before it. The March review and the account manager's manner don't change what happens Friday — they move to the end, or they die.

4 · Check with the must-survive box, then the two-lines test. Every item on Scenario B's must-survive list — the Friday deadline, the 4.2% figure, the three options, the October consequence, the decision owner — has to appear somewhere. Then cover everything past your first two lines: can the director see the decision, the deadline and the stakes in what's left uncovered? If yes, the inversion held.

Two siblings, not the same idea Writing up is a register for one audience — the stance you take with a senior reader: owned, calibrated, brisk. Bottom-line-up-front is an ordering that applies to every audience — a peer, a supplier, an assistant, a set of minutes — because it's about what comes first, not about who you're being for them. The one-ask email is a different axis again: it decides what an email carries (one ask, not three); BLUF decides what comes first in whatever it carries. You can write a one-ask email that still buries its ask in paragraph two — this lesson is what stops that.

Try it for real

Subject lines are a BLUF exercise in miniature: the reader decides open-now, open-later, or never — from a dozen words, with no second chance at a first line. Write the bottom-line-carrying subject for three scenarios and check each against its must-survive box. Scenario B: the subject should carry the decision and its deadline — something that tells the director "Friday" before they've opened the email (decision-needed-by-Friday). Scenario E: nothing needs deciding, and the subject should say so, not stay silent and let silence be mistaken for urgency (on-track-no-action). Scenario G: three asks live on three different clocks in the original, but the email you actually send carries one — its subject should carry that one ask and its real date (the-one-ask-with-date). Check yourself: read only your three subject lines, nothing else. Can you tell, from the subject alone, which to open now, which to open later, and which needs no action at all? If you can't tell without opening the body, the subject hasn't done its job.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — Scenario B's history-first draft inverted to lead with the Friday decision — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: the must-survive box plus the two-lines test — and this structure's signature machine failure: the model doesn't promote your bottom line, it writes a new one, and the new one quietly claims more than the draft ever did.

The point of the ladder From "it reorders one draft" to "it builds the auditor" — the deliverable is always the same sentence, moved to the front. What protects you at every rung is the provenance check: the first line must trace back to a sentence that already existed, never a new one composed to sound like a summary. A tool that writes you a tidier bottom line than the one you gave it isn't helping — it's making a decision on your behalf and hiding the seam.
