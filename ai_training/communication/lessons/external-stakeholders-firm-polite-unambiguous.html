Communication to a High Standard · Lesson 0005

External stakeholders: firm, polite, unambiguous

Tied to your mission: the survey asked for this by name — "professional writing for external stakeholders where ambiguity is removed". Anything you send outside the building is quotable. That single fact is the whole lesson: write every external email as if it might be read aloud in a dispute, forwarded to their commercial director, or pasted into their order system — because one day, it will be.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario A — The late price list and Scenario C — The over-casual reply — both mail to an external supplier you need to keep, both with a "must survive revision" list that is your safety rail for this lesson.

The one idea

Builds on Reword for tone & audience: reader × stake picks the register. For a reader outside the building, the stake is always the same one, whatever the email is nominally about — quotability. Assume anything you send a supplier can be read aloud in a dispute, forwarded to their commercial director, or pasted into their order system, and the external register falls out of that single assumption: firm — dates and consequences stated plainly, never softened away; polite — zero blame, because blame spends the relationship and reads terribly aloud; unambiguous — words that survive being taken literally, "confirm by Wednesday COB," not "as soon as you can."

This is the same reader-times-stake model specialised to one reader: someone outside the walls. It is not Writing up's register wearing a different hat — that register optimises for a decision in seconds; this one optimises for surviving quotation. A director rereads your email once. A supplier's dispute file keeps it forever.

Why it matters at work

A supplier relationship survives on emails exactly like Scenario A's — you need the price list and you need Marco answering your calls next quarter. Every external email sits outside your organisation's walls the moment you hit send: it can be screenshotted into a group chat, quoted back at you in a renewal negotiation, or read verbatim by someone who was never meant to see it. Firm-polite-unambiguous isn't house style for its own sake — it's the only register that reads the same the tenth time it's read as it did the first.

The bad habit this undoes External mail fails at two opposite poles, and both fail because the writer forgot the email leaves the building. Venting — "I would have thought this was a pretty basic thing to get right" — reads even worse on their side of the wall than it does on yours; blame you'd shrug off internally becomes evidence in a supplier review. Chumminess — "can't 100% promise on the uplift yet lol" — hands the reader a sentence they can quote however suits them: as a broken commitment, or as proof you don't take the account seriously. Firm-polite-unambiguous is the narrow channel between venting and chumminess, not a compromise between them.

How to do it

Rewrite Scenario A by applying one test to every sentence: would it survive being read aloud in a dispute? A sentence survives if it states a fact, a date, or a numbered request — and fails if it states blame, a mood, or a vague promise.

1 · Strip the blame, keep the fact. "I emailed you last week… heard nothing back… becoming a real problem… doesn't look great on your end… pretty basic thing to get right" is five sentences of blame and not one fact a reader could act on. Replace all five with what's true and load-bearing: the Q3 list hasn't arrived, and range review starts Thursday. Read aloud, that's a status update. The original, read aloud, is an accusation.

2 · Make the requests literal and numbered. "Send it as soon as you can" fails the test twice — "as soon as you can" is Marco's clock, not yours, and it can't be quoted as a breach of anything. Number the requests and date them: (1) the Q3 list with updated case costs for the snacks lines, (2) confirmation of whether the Stacked Chips 12pk 300g price is changing, both by Wednesday COB. "Wednesday COB" survives quotation; "as soon as you can" doesn't survive a second reading.

3 · Keep the deadline firm — don't launder it into a mood. Firm doesn't mean cold. "Range review starts Thursday, so this is now on the critical path" states a real consequence without threatening anyone; it survives being read aloud by Marco's manager exactly as well as it survives being read by yours.

4 · Reconcile against the must-survive box. Check your rewrite against Scenario A's list: Q3 price list outstanding, second chase, updated case costs for snacks lines, Stacked Chips 12pk 300g confirmation, Thursday review, Marco as owner of the next step. All present, zero blame, every request literal — that's a passing rewrite.

Try it for real

Now Scenario C — The over-casual reply: the opposite failure pole. The facts are all present and correct — 95 cases/week, the promo fortnight, the Tuesday delivery shift — but they're wrapped in "yeah… tbh… lol… Cheers!!", which reads as either a promise or a shrug depending on who's reading it back to you. Apply the same quotability test, but this time the fix isn't subtraction of blame — it's keeping the warmth and removing everything quotable against you. Polite is not the same as cold: "Hi Kim" and "Thanks" can stay. What has to go is anything that reads as a hedge to you but a commitment (or a joke) to Kim — turn "can't 100% promise on the uplift yet lol" into an explicit split: 95 cases/week is the committed planning number; any uplift is possible but not committed. Check your rewrite two ways: everything on Scenario C's must-survive list is present, and every sentence — including the friendly ones — survives being read aloud back to you.

The point of the ladder From "it rewords one email" to "it flags what shouldn't cross the wall" — the deliverable is always the same test: would every sentence survive being read aloud in a dispute? What protects you at every rung is that test applied literally, not a feeling that the draft "sounds fine." Craft first, so you can supervise.
