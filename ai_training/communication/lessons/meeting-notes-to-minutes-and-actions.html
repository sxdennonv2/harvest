Communication to a High Standard · Lesson 0011

Meeting notes to minutes & actions

Tied to your mission: the survey's coordination demand keeps surfacing as "meeting notes to text" and "summarise meeting notes & populate minutes" — both assume the notes themselves are the deliverable. They aren't; the minutes document is. A boundary worth stating plainly: a sibling Outlook lesson covers the plumbing — where actions have to live once the meeting ends so they actually fire, as flags, tasks and chases. This lesson is the writing that comes before that: the minutes document itself, the thing a colleague who missed the meeting could read and act on. Zero Outlook mechanics below — only the words on the page.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario K — The minutes (from the promo meeting): raw notes from a real promo meeting, and a "must survive" box that is your marking scheme for everything below.

The one idea

Minutes are a species-sorting exercise. A meeting produces exactly three kinds of content: decisions — made, past tense, nobody owes anything further; actions — owed: what, owner, date, every field required; and parked items — neither agreed nor dropped, the species most summaries lose. The minutes document's whole job is keeping the three apart, because each is consumed differently once the meeting ends: decisions get quoted back at whoever's sheet they change, actions get chased, parked items get re-raised at the next meeting.

Builds on summarise a thread — the ask-table discipline, extract first and write the prose from the table, now with a third species added. Prose written from a sorted table can't blur decisions, actions and parked items; prose written from the meeting's chronological narrative blurs them constantly, because in a story all three wear the same grammar.

Why it matters at work

The minutes are what the room agreed to be true, for everyone who wasn't in the room. A colleague covering your desk reads them and acts; a director scans them and trusts that "decided" means decided. The chronological transcript — "then we discussed deliveries, and Dana mentioned the proof…" — hands a decision, an action and a parked item the identical sentence shape, so three weeks later nobody can say with confidence whether Popcorn was decided out of the promo or merely mentioned, and the organic-samples item, raised once and never labelled, dies quietly with no one aware it was ever alive. The survey's appetite for AI-drafted minutes makes this sharper: a fluent narrative recap reads well and sorts nothing.

The bad habit this undoes The chronological transcript: "then we discussed deliveries, and Dana mentioned the proof, and someone raised the samples question again…" A decision, an action and a parked item all narrated in the same past-tense, comma-spliced sentence, with nothing in the grammar to tell a reader which is which. The reader has to re-derive the species themselves — and the parked item, never an "event" with an owner or a date, is the one species a narrative has no slot for, so it vanishes without a trace.

How to do it

Sort Scenario K's raw notes into the three species, then write the minutes from the sorted table — never straight from the notes.

1 · Read for species markers. Sweep the notes marking: past-tense, settled facts ("confirmed", "agreed", "is OUT") are decisions; verbs with an owner attached ("I send", "I need to tell", "Dana getting us", "Alex to book") are actions; anything explicitly deferred ("parked", "next month's meeting") is parked. Ignore the order things were said in — it tells you nothing about the species.

2 · Build three short tables. Decisions: one column, what was settled. Actions: four columns — what · owner · date · (blank) — every action needs all three fields filled, and a field you can't fill is a finding about the meeting, not a gap in your table. Parked: what, and where it's carried to. Scenario K's must-survive box gives you the count to sort against: 3 decisions, 4 actions each with an owner and a date, 1 parked item.

3 · Write the minutes from the tables, under three headers. DECIDED, ACTIONS, PARKED — as literal section headers, not as connective prose. The marker discipline is what lets the species survive skimming: a reader scanning for "what do I owe" goes straight to ACTIONS and never has to parse a paragraph to find it. Include attendees. If nothing is parked, the header stays and says so — "Parked: none" — because its absence is information a reader needs, not an omission to save space.

4 · Reconcile, then peek. Count your three tables against the must-survive box — 3/4/1 — and check every action has an owner and a date before you write a word of prose. Then open the practice pack's worked minutes for Scenario K and compare structurally: same three headers, same counts, nothing promoted or demoted between species.

Check yourself

Answer these before moving on — immediate feedback below each.

1. What are the three species minutes must keep apart?

2. Why does the chronological transcript lose parked items specifically?

3. An action in your table has a "what" and a "when" but no named owner. What should you do?

Try it for real

Take the notes from your own last real meeting — whatever scruffy form they're currently in — and run the same sort: three tables, DECIDED / ACTIONS / PARKED, then minutes written from the tables. Self-check two things. First, every single action carries all three fields — what, owner, date — and where one is missing, your minutes say so rather than inventing it; a field you can't fill is a finding about the meeting, not about your minutes. Second, the parked section exists in your minutes even if it's empty — write "Parked: none" rather than dropping the header — because a reader needs to know parked was considered and found empty, not left unchecked.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — Scenario K sorted into decisions, actions and parked items, then minutes written from the sort — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: count the output against the must-survive box, 3 decisions / 4 actions with owner and date / 1 parked, and watch for the machine's signature failure — species drift, where a parked item gets drafted as a decision or an action's missing field gets helpfully invented.

Check: feed it Scenario K first — it must produce exactly 3 decisions, 4 actions (all with owner and date), 1 parked, and zero unclassified. Then feed it a second, deliberately altered set of notes with one action missing its owner: the generator must flag that field as missing, not fill it. A generator that guesses an owner to complete the table is assigning work nobody in the room accepted — that failure is worse than an admitted gap, because it looks finished.

The point of the ladder From "it sorts the notes you paste in" to "it builds the whole generator" — the deliverable never changes: minutes with the three species kept apart, every action complete or flagged, every parked item still parked. What protects you at every rung is the same count — 3 decisions, 4 actions, 1 parked, zero unclassified — because a minutes document that reads fluently proves nothing; one that reconciles against the must-survive box proves itself.
