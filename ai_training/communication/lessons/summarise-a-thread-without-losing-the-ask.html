Communication to a High Standard · Lesson 0005

Summarise a thread without losing the ask

Tied to your mission: after rewording, summarising is the survey's next-biggest ask — summarise this message, this thread, these meeting notes. And it carries the worst failure mode in the building: a summary that reads beautifully and has quietly dropped the one thing someone owed somebody by Friday. The craft here is knowing what a summary must carry, so you can spot the machine dropping it.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario D — The promo thread: four messages, three people, two deadlines, one question nobody has answered. Its "must survive any summary" list is your marking scheme for everything below.

The one idea

A thread summary is an extraction, not a compression. Threads carry two kinds of content: obligations — who owes what, to whom, by when, plus the questions still open — and narrative, the story of who said what in which order. Obligations are load-bearing; narrative is packaging. A summary succeeds when every obligation and every open question survives, whatever happens to the story.

So the method is mechanical: extract the ask-table first — what / who owes it / by when / confirmed-or-open — then write the prose from the table. Prose written from a table can't lose an ask; prose written from memory of the story loses them constantly.

Why it matters at work

The summary is what travels: to the colleague covering your desk, to the manager who reads four lines and decides, into the Monday meeting notes. Nobody re-reads the thread — the summary replaces it. Drop the open Popcorn question and no one on earth is now carrying it; it resurfaces as a Friday-afternoon surprise with your name in the From line. The survey's appetite for AI summarising makes this sharper, not softer: the machine summarises the narrative brilliantly and owes nothing to anyone.

The bad habit this undoes The chronological retelling: "Sam proposed the uplift, then I checked with Alex, then Alex said…" — a story, not a state. The reader must re-derive every obligation themselves, and the thread's open question — the thing nobody answered — isn't an event in the story at all, so it vanishes without a trace.

How to do it

Summarise Scenario D for the colleague covering your desk, in four passes:

1 · Read for obligation verbs. Sweep the thread marking only: confirm, need, by, can you, owes, deadline — and every question mark. Ignore everything conversational.

2 · Build the ask-table. Four columns: what · who owes it · by when · confirmed or open. Scenario D yields four rows — two dated obligations, one open question, one standing fact (the September pallet change). If a row has no owner, that's not a gap in your table, it's a finding about the thread.

3 · Write the prose from the table. One line per row, owners and dates kept, open questions labelled open ("OPEN: is Popcorn 10pk in the promo?"). Lead with whatever expires first — Friday beats September.

4 · Reconcile. Check your lines against the scenario's must-survive list: every obligation, every owner, every date, every open question present, and nothing in your summary that the thread doesn't say. That last clause matters as much as the first.

Try it for real

Same thread, harder compression: summarise Scenario D for your manager in two lines — only what needs their awareness or decision this week. That forces triage: the Friday confirmation and the open Popcorn question make the cut; the delivery-day mechanics and the September pallet note become a trailing half-sentence or drop to your own follow-up list. Check yourself the same way: both dated obligations and the open question must survive even at two lines — if compression cost you an ask, it wasn't compression, it was loss.

The point of the ladder From "it summarises one thread" to "it builds the brief generator" — the deliverable is always the ask-table wearing prose. What protects you at every rung is the count: obligations, owners, dates and open questions in, the same number out. A summary that reads well proves nothing; a summary that reconciles proves itself.
