Communication to a High Standard · Lesson 0006

The one-ask email

Tied to your mission: the survey asked for "clarity and conciseness" and clearer messaging — and the most common structural reason an email fails isn't wording at all. It's cargo: three asks in one message, of which exactly one gets answered. This lesson is about deciding what an email carries before deciding how it sounds.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario G — The three-ask email: a perfectly friendly note to a supplier carrying three genuine asks on three different clocks. Its "must survive" list is unusual — all three asks must survive, but not necessarily in one email.

The one idea

An email gets one ask, because readers answer emails, they don't process lists: they reply to the ask that's easiest or first, hit send, archive, and the thread is done in their head — your second ask dies inside a message marked answered. So the whole structure serves the one ask: the subject line carries it ("Eggs promo pricing — needed by Wednesday COB"), the first line states it with owner and date, and everything below only supports it.

One ask is not one sentence. Scenario A's two numbered requests are one ask — same owner, same deadline, same purpose: one decision package. The test for splitting: different clock, different thread. If two asks have different owners, deadlines or purposes, they are different emails — each with its own subject line, which is to say its own memory and its own chase.

Why it matters at work

Every buyer knows the pattern in Scenario G: Kim answers the pricing question, everyone feels concluded, and the May samples enter their third month of limbo — findable only by re-reading a thread whose subject says nothing about samples. Split into three subjects, each ask can be tracked, forwarded, chased and closed on its own clock. One-ask emails aren't shorter conversations; they're conversations that can be audited.

The bad habit this undoes "While I have you…" — the batching instinct: saving up asks and unloading them in one friendly omnibus because sending three emails feels like three impositions. It's the reverse: the omnibus imposes a to-do list wearing an email's clothing, and the polite reader who answers one item has, in good faith, buried the other two.

How to do it

Restructure Scenario G in four passes:

1 · Count the asks. Sweep for requests the way the summarising lesson sweeps for obligations: pricing before Thursday, samples since May, September availability. Three asks, three owners of your chasing energy — and critically, three different clocks.

2 · Rank by clock and pick the passenger. The catalogue locks Thursday — pricing is the ask that travels today. The others aren't dropped; they're scheduled: samples chase tomorrow, availability next week.

3 · Build the email around its one ask. Subject carries the ask and the date. First line states it: "could you send the confirmed promo pricing for Free-range Eggs 12pk by Wednesday COB?" One line of stake ("catalogue locks Thursday"), sign-off. Nothing about samples, nothing about September.

4 · Reconcile against the plan, not the email. The must-survive list has three items and your email has one — the other two must appear in your written plan (tomorrow's send, next week's send), each with its own subject line. An ask that lives only in your head didn't survive.

Try it for real

Now calibrate the boundary across the whole pack — for each scenario, how many asks? Scenario A: one (two components, one decision package — they stay together). Scenario E: zero — and the writing-up lesson taught you that zero must be said out loud ("nothing needs your decision"). Scenario G: three, on three clocks — split. Check yourself: for each call, name the test you applied (owner · deadline · purpose). If you can't name which of the three differed, you guessed.

The point of the ladder From "it splits one omnibus" to "it builds the splitter" — the deliverable is always asks that each own a subject line and a clock. What protects you at every rung is the clock audit: real deadlines travel, absent deadlines stay absent. A tool that invents urgency to be helpful is training your suppliers to ignore your Thursdays.
