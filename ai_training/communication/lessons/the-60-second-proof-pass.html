Communication to a High Standard · Lesson 0009

The 60-second proof pass

Tied to your mission: "spell check" and "proofreading" appear in the survey verbatim — people already hand drafts to AI for exactly this. Which makes the craft question sharp: what do the tools actually catch, and what still gets through? The answer decides where your sixty seconds go.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario H — The order confirmation. It works differently from the other scenarios: the facts box is the truth and the draft contains planted errors — proof the draft against the facts, then peek at the plant list to mark yourself.

The one idea

You cannot proof by rereading, because you read what you meant, not what you typed — the brain autocorrects its own work. A proof pass that works is a set of separate sweeps, each hunting one class of error: sweep one for the money (every name, number, date and reference checked against the source), sweep two for real-word errors (out/our, form/from, except/accept — correctly spelled, wrong word, invisible to spellcheck), sweep three for the shop window (subject line and first sentence, where errors are seen by the most people). Sixty seconds total, because each sweep is scoped to one thing.

Why it matters at work

Scenario H is a routine confirmation email carrying eight errors, and five of them are the expensive kind: wrong quantity, wrong price, wrong day, wrong PO, wrong name. Any one of them survives politely into a supplier's order system and comes back weeks later as 120 cases arriving on the wrong day at the wrong price. Typos embarrass; fact errors compound. That's why the money sweep comes first — it's the only sweep where an error has a unit cost.

The bad habit this undoes One more anxious full reread before hitting send — which finds nothing, because rereading is the one method guaranteed to see intent instead of text — plus quiet faith in the red squiggle, which cannot flag a single one of Scenario H's eight errors: every wrong word in it is a correctly spelled real word, and spellcheck has no idea what you agreed with Marco.

How to do it

Proof Scenario H in three scoped sweeps — resist reading it "normally" first:

1 · The money sweep (30 seconds). Facts box in one eye, draft in the other. Check only: name, quantity, price, day-and-date, PO. Tick each fact off against the source, never from memory — memory is where the transposition came from. This sweep should catch five plants.

2 · The real-word sweep (20 seconds). Read the draft once at walking pace for meaning at the word level — aloud or subvocalised, which breaks the autocorrect. You're hunting words that are spelled right and wrong: "confirm out order", "please except", "form our side".

3 · The shop-window sweep (10 seconds). Subject line and first sentence, fresh. Most-read, first-judged, and — because you wrote them first — oldest and least recently checked.

Then peek at the plant list and count. Eight out of eight and you've earned the sixty-second habit; anything the sweeps missed tells you exactly which sweep you rushed.

Try it for real

Two runs. First, proof something clean: open Scenario E's worked revision and run all three sweeps against E's must-survive list. Finding nothing is the point — a pass that comes back empty is a certificate, and knowing what "done" feels like stops the anxious fourth reread. Second, proof the last email you actually wrote today, using whatever source its facts came from. If you can't lay hands on the source, that's the finding: unproofable facts are the ones that go wrong.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — Scenario H proofed against its facts — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: count the findings against the plant list, and know each tool's blind spot, because proofreading is the rung where the tools' marketing is furthest from their competence.

The signature miss is the transposed digit ($14.05 for $14.50, PO-88213 for PO-88231): language models read numbers the way you do, by intent, and will cheerfully confirm a transposition as "matching". Whatever it scores, the money sweep stays yours — the machine assists the sweep, it doesn't own it.

That's the honest division of labour on this rung: mechanics to the machine, money to you.

The point of the ladder From "it proofs one draft" to "it builds the facts-checker" — the deliverable is always the same audit: facts verified against source, wrong-words caught, and a clean bill stated explicitly. What protects you at every rung is knowing each tool's blind spot: the model reads digits by intent, the editor has no source of truth, and only the sweep you actually run protects the money.
