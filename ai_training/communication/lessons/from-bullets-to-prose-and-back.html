Communication to a High Standard · Lesson 0007

From bullets to prose (and back)

Tied to your mission: the survey asked for help to "write emails" and "formalise" raw notes — and for an AI tool that can "write an introduction email from data". Both are the same underlying move: your notes are bullets, the reader needs prose, and something has to carry the relationships between the facts that the bullets never had to state. This lesson is about what that "something" actually is, so you can do it yourself before you ask a machine to do it for you.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario J — The bullet dump (both directions): five raw notes that need to become a reply to a supplier contact, and — the reverse direction — Scenario E — The padded update, which needs the opposite treatment. Both "must survive" lists are your marking scheme.

The one idea

Bullets and prose are different machines. Bullets store facts: parallel, scannable, no logic between one line and the next — you can shuffle them and lose nothing. Prose carries relationships: because, but, only-if, and the distinction that matters most in a workplace message — what's committed versus what's merely possible. Converting between them is not reformatting; it's adding or removing connective logic. Bullets→prose means making the relationships explicit: "95 is the number we're planning on, but there may be upside, and it is not promised." Prose→bullets means extracting the facts and dropping the connective padding that was carrying nothing.

The test that tells you which direction — or which machine — a message needs: what did each line's neighbour have to do with it? If the answer is "nothing, it's just the next fact," you want bullets. If the answer is "everything — this line only makes sense because of what came before it," you want prose. One more rule travels with the bullets themselves: a note-to-self buried inside them ("check w/ Alex before promising anything") is process, not content — it documents how you reached the number, and it never travels to the reader.

Why it matters at work

Send Scenario J's five bullets to Kim with a bare "see below" and she has to assemble the relationships herself — and she'll assemble them wrong in the direction that costs you: "95/wk" and "maybe more" read as two facts of equal weight, so she plans a fortnight's orders on the maybe. The prose isn't decoration; it's the only thing standing between her and a number you never actually promised. Run the conversion the other way with Scenario E and the failure is the mirror image: three plain facts wrapped in enough hedging and warm-up that a director has to dig them back out — the connective tissue is doing nothing there except costing fifteen seconds of a reader who allocates attention like budget.

The bad habit this undoes Sending the bullets raw — "see below" — and letting the reader assemble the relationships themselves, so a possible number and a committed number sit on the page with equal visual weight and Kim plans on the maybe. Its mirror-image habit is just as common: prose so padded with connective filler that three real facts drown in it, which is exactly what happened to the update in Scenario E.

How to do it

Direction one: convert Scenario J's five bullets into the reply to Kim.

1 · Sort committed from possible. "95/wk eggs for the promo fortnight — plan on this" is committed. "Maybe more if catalogue lands well, NOT committed" is possible. These are not two facts of equal rank; the bullets already mark the difference with their own wording — your job is to carry that difference into prose, not flatten it.

2 · Drop the note-to-self. "(check w/ Alex before promising anything on volumes beyond 95)" is an instruction you left for yourself. It never reaches Kim in any form — not as a caveat, not as a parenthetical, not softened. If it survives into the draft, direction one has failed regardless of how good the rest of the prose reads.

3 · Surface the ask as an ask. "need her to confirm courier for the organic samples" is a bullet fact; in prose it has to read as an actual question aimed at Kim, not a buried clause. A bullet can hide a request inside its phrasing. A sentence can't — say it as a question or it isn't one.

4 · Write it, then check against the worked prose. Draft your reply, then open the practice pack's solution for Scenario J and compare: does your version keep 95 as the committed number, the upside as explicitly uncommitted, the Tuesday-only delivery change, the courier question, and nothing from the parenthetical? Every item on the must-survive list is a line the connectives had to do real work to carry.

Try it for real

Now direction two, the reverse conversion: take Scenario E — The padded update and reduce it to four bullets — the three facts (range reset complete and on schedule; Riverside onboarding slipped to the 18th, you own the recovery with a weekly call in place; promo calendar gone to print) plus a fourth line: no decision needed. Then compare what the connective tissue was doing in each scenario. In Scenario E, the connectives ("just wanted to", "so as you know", "it's hard to say exactly") were doing nothing — no relationship, no logic, pure padding — which is exactly why bullets win there: strip the padding and the four facts stand on their own. In Scenario J's reply, the connectives ("but", "not committed", "for that fortnight only") were carrying the committed-vs-possible line itself — remove them and Kim can't tell the plan from the maybe. Same move, opposite direction, opposite reason. Check: for each scenario, can you say which machine it needed and name what the connectives were doing (carrying a relationship, or padding a fact) that made that the right call? If you can't say why, you converted by instinct, not by the test.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — Scenario J's bullets into the reply to Kim — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: every committed-vs-possible line stated explicitly, nothing promised the bullets don't promise, the note-to-self excluded — this conversion's signature machine failure is commitment flattening: a possible number quietly becoming a committed one on the way from bullets to prose.

A converter that loses the word "NOT" on the round trip isn't a productivity tool, it's a liability generator: it will hand a possible number back as a committed one exactly once, and that once is the one that gets promised to a supplier.

The point of the ladder From "it writes one reply" to "it builds the converter" — the deliverable is always the same: every fact preserved, and every relationship between facts — especially committed versus possible — preserved with it. What protects you at every rung is the modal-verb audit and the notes-to-self test. A tool that flattens "maybe" into "will" isn't saving you a redraft; it's spending a promise you never made.
