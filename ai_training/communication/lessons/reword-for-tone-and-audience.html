Communication to a High Standard · Lesson 0002

Reword for tone & audience

Tied to your mission: this is the single most-requested AI help in the office — reword it, rephrase it, make it professional, fix it for senior leadership. Before you hand that job to a machine, you need the craft yourself: not to compete with the machine, but to judge its rewrite in ten seconds instead of rereading your own draft five times.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario A — The late price list: a chase to a supplier that's honest about the problem and wrong about everything else. Each scenario carries a "must survive revision" list — that list is your safety rail for this whole lesson.

The one idea

Tone is a decision you make before writing, not a property you sand off afterwards. The decision has two inputs: the reader (who they are, what they can do for you) and the stake (what happens if this goes well or badly). Reader × stake picks your register — how warm, how formal, how direct.

And tone doesn't live where people look for it. It barely lives in adjectives at all. It lives in three places: the opening line (fact or accusation?), the ask (clear and dated, or a mood?), and the sign-off (a door left open, or a door closed). Reword those three and the whole message changes register.

Why it matters at work

A supplier relationship survives on emails exactly like Scenario A — you need the price list and you need Marco answering your calls next quarter. A director decides in the first two lines whether your email is a decision or a burden. An external stakeholder reads ambiguity as an exit. The survey said it plainly: what this office wants most from AI is rewording — which means the judgement to check a reword is about to become the core writing skill of the building.

The bad habit this undoes Tone by anxiety: rereading the draft five times, swapping words, bolting on softeners — "just", "sorry to chase", "I would have thought" — until the message is longer, vaguer and somehow still blunt where it counts. Tone chosen once, up front, replaces all of it.

How to do it

Rewrite Scenario A in four passes — in the pack, or paste the draft into a blank email and work there:

1 · Name the reader and the stake. Marco, an external supplier we keep working with; the stake is Thursday's range review. That rules the register: firm about the deadline, zero blame, door open.

2 · Rewrite the opening. The draft opens with an accusation ("heard nothing back… becoming a real problem"). Replace it with the fact and the stake: the list hasn't arrived, and range review starts Thursday.

3 · Rewrite the ask. The draft's ask is a mood ("send it as soon as you can"). Make it enumerable and dated: the Q3 list with updated snacks case costs, plus the Stacked Chips 12pk 300g confirmation, by Wednesday COB.

4 · Strip the sludge, then reconcile. Delete "I would have thought this was a pretty basic thing" and "it doesn't look great on your end" — blame spends relationship and buys nothing. Then check your version against the scenario's must-survive list: every fact, every ask, every owner still there. That reconciliation is this course's version of "the parts must sum to the whole".

Try it for real

Now Scenario B — The buried escalation: same four passes, harder audience. The reader is your director; the stake is a Friday deadline with a Q4 delist behind it. Your register: lead with the decision, compress the history, options with risks, one dated ask. Check yourself two ways: everything on the must-survive list is present — and the director could act from your first two lines alone. If the deadline is in paragraph three, it isn't rewritten yet.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — Scenario A, reworded firm-but-polite — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: compare the output against the must-survive list, and watch for the machine's signature failure — invented commitments it added to sound nice.

The point of the ladder From "it rewords one email" to "it builds the register library" — the deliverable is always the same message in a chosen register. What protects you at every rung is the must-survive list: facts, asks and owners are load-bearing; everything else is clothing. Craft first, so you can supervise.
