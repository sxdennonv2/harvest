Communication to a High Standard · Lesson 0003

Writing up: the senior-leadership register

Tied to your mission: the survey named this audience specifically — "tone in responses where the audience might be senior leadership". Writing up is the register people get most wrong in the most expensive direction: the more senior the reader, the longer and more padded the email becomes. It should be the opposite — and once you know why, you can catch a machine making the same mistake.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario E — The padded update: a monthly update to a director in which three clean facts and one owned risk are drowning in apology and waffle. Its "must survive revision" list is your marking scheme.

The one idea

Builds on Reword for tone & audience: reader × stake picks the register. For senior leadership the reader is someone who audits stances rather than reads prose — in seconds, they want to know: what's your position, how sure are you, and what do you need from me? The upward register answers exactly those three, and nothing else.

Three stance moves make it: own the position ("I recommend…", "I own the recovery" — not "it could be argued"); calibrate, don't hedge — calibration names what's known, what isn't, and the risk ("partially supported by market data"), while hedging protects the writer ("probably partly on their side, hard to say exactly"); and keep only what changes their decision — process narrative is you showing work, and they aren't marking it. Seniority compresses: if there's no decision to make, the email's job is to say so.

Why it matters at work

Directors allocate attention the way you allocate budget. An update they can clear in fifteen seconds buys you credibility for the day you need fifteen minutes; a padded one teaches them your emails can wait. And the stance moves are how judgement travels upward — "slipped a week, I own the recovery, flag if the 18th moves" reads as someone running their patch. The same facts wrapped in "hopefully more or less sorted" read as someone the patch is running.

The bad habit this undoes Padding up: formality inflating with seniority — apology openers, process narrative to show effort, hedge-stacking ("probably… hard to say… hopefully") to pre-spread the blame, and "sorry for the long email!" doing the confessing. The draft gets longer as the reader gets more senior, which is exactly backwards.

How to do it

Rewrite Scenario E in four passes:

1 · Rule the register. Reader: director. Stake: their attention and your credibility; nothing needs deciding. So: brisk, owned, calibrated — and the subject line does the triage for them ("on track, nothing needs your decision").

2 · Extract the facts and lead with the shape. Three facts and one risk are in there: reset complete, Riverside slipped to the 18th, calendar at print. Number them. The one that carries risk gets the fullest line.

3 · Convert hedges to calibration. "Probably partly on their side, hard to say exactly, hopefully more or less sorted" becomes "slipped; revised completion the 18th. I own the recovery: weekly call in place." Same uncertainty, but now it has a date, an owner and a tripwire — that's calibration.

4 · Close the loop explicitly, then reconcile. "Nothing needs your decision. I'll flag Riverside immediately if the 18th moves." Then check the must-survive list: all three facts, the 18th, your ownership, the weekly call — present; nothing invented. Cutting the apologies is free; cutting a fact is loss.

Try it for real

Now the decision case: Scenario B — The buried escalation. You reworded it in the reword lesson; this time apply the stance moves on top. Own the recommendation outright ("Recommendation: push back, accept as fallback by Thursday noon"), calibrate the evidence ("partially supported by market data" — keep it, that's calibration, not weakness), and cut the March-review history unless it changes Friday's decision. Check: everything on B's must-survive list, plus a recommendation a director could approve with one word — and no false certainty the thread doesn't support.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — Scenario E in the upward register — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: facts, dates and ownership against the must-survive list — and this register's signature machine failure: stripping calibration along with the hedges, so honest uncertainty comes back as false confidence.

The point of the ladder From "it compresses one update" to "it builds the brief template" — the deliverable is always the same three answers: position, confidence, ask. What protects you at every rung is the calibration test: uncertainty in, uncertainty out — with a date, an owner and a tripwire. A machine that makes you sound more certain than you are is spending your credibility, not saving your time.
