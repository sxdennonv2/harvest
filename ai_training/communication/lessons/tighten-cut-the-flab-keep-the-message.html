Communication to a High Standard · Lesson 0001

Tighten: cut the flab, keep the message

Tied to your mission: "tighten emails" and "clarity and conciseness" came straight from the survey, in those words. Tightening is the foundation skill of this whole course — every other lesson assumes you can tell the words that carry the message from the words that carry nothing, because that's also exactly the judgement you need when a machine offers to shorten your draft for you.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario F — The process email: one process change wrapped in four times its own weight of filler. The tone is fine — this scenario is pure flab — and its "must survive revision" list is deliberately short: four items. Everything else is cuttable.

The one idea

Every phrase in a draft is either load or flab. Load changes what the reader knows or does: facts, dates, instructions, owners. Flab is everything else — and the test is ruthless and simple: cut it, and ask whether the reader would now know less or do differently. If not, it was flab, however professional it sounded.

Flab clusters in knowable places, which is why tightening is a craft and not a talent: throat-clearing ("I just wanted to reach out to provide a quick update in relation to…"), phrase-for-word swaps ("at this point in time" for now, "by means of" for through), buried verbs ("a decision has been made to make a change" for we're changing), and empty qualifiers ("basically", "a certain amount of", "as you may or may not be aware"). You're not writing less; you're deleting what was never writing.

Why it matters at work

Scenario F goes to the whole category team. At ~160 words, thirty readers skim it and two of them miss the portal instruction — which is precisely the "certain amount of confusion" the email itself complains about. At ~45 words, the deadline, the date and the instruction are unmissable because they're most of what's left. Multiply by every process email the office sends and tightening stops being style — it's error rate.

The bad habit this undoes Padding as politeness: the belief that "I just wanted to reach out" softens an email and "it should also be noted that" makes it formal. Neither reaches the reader as courtesy — filler reads as noise, and the reader pays for it with the attention they had budgeted for your actual message.

How to do it

Tighten Scenario F in four passes:

1 · Mark the load. Underline only what the must-survive list names: 1 August, Monday 10am, was-Wednesday, QA turnaround, portal-not-email, questions to you. Six fragments in ~160 words — that ratio is the diagnosis.

2 · Delete the throat-clearing. The whole first sentence and every "it should also be noted" go without replacement. A subject line does their job: "Sample submissions — new deadline from 1 August".

3 · Unbury the verbs and swap phrases for words. "A decision has been made to make a change to the submission deadline" → "the deadline is changing". "By means of the portal rather than being sent through via email" → "through the portal, not by email".

4 · Reconcile and count. Every must-survive item present, nothing new invented, and the word count at half or better — Scenario F tightens from ~160 to under 50 without losing a single fact. If you had to cut a date to get there, you didn't tighten, you amputated.

Try it for real

Now do it where flab and substance are tangled: Scenario A — The late price list. It isn't just wordy — it has blame mixed into the flab ("I would have thought…", "doesn't look great on your end"). Tighten it with the same four passes and notice something useful: the blame goes out with the flab, because blame never passes the load test either. Check against A's must-survive list: the two requests, the Thursday review and the Wednesday deadline all present, at half the words or better.

The point of the ladder From "it cuts one email" to "it builds the linter" — the deliverable is always the same message at half the weight. What protects you at every rung is the load count: the same facts, dates, instructions and owners in the short version as the long one. Shorter with less information isn't tighter — anyone can do that, including, enthusiastically, the machine.
