The before/after pack

A growing set of deliberately flabby drafts. Every lesson in this course revises one of them — by hand first, then up the GenAI ladder. The check never changes: the revision keeps every fact, every ask, and every owner from the original. Suppliers are fictional (shared with the Excel course).

Scenario A — The late price list

To: Marco Bianchi, Acme Foods (external supplier) · From: you · Situation: the Q3 price list was due last Monday; range review starts Thursday; second chase.

Must survive revision: Q3 price list is outstanding · second chase · updated case costs for snacks lines · confirm whether Stacked Chips 12pk 300g price changes · range review starts Thursday · owner of the next step: Marco.
Worked revision (peek after your own attempt)

Hi Marco, Following up on the Q3 price list — we haven't received it yet and range review starts Thursday, so it's now on the critical path. Could you send by Wednesday COB: 1. The Q3 list with updated case costs for the snacks lines 2. Confirmation of whether the Stacked Chips 12pk 300g price is changing — we're seeing conflicting figures If anything is blocking the list, tell me today and we'll work around it. Thanks, [Name]

Must survive revision: decision needed by Friday · 4.2% increase, Cheddar Cheese 500g, Coastal Co · rationale: milk input costs, partially supported by market data · three options (accept / push back / switch, each with a risk) · missing Friday = October slot lapses = Q4 delist · decision owner: the director.

Subject: Decision needed by Friday — Coastal Co +4.2% on Cheddar 500g Coastal Co have tabled a 4.2% increase on Cheddar Cheese 500g, citing milk input costs (partially supported by market data). Happy to talk it through before then.

Scenario H — The order confirmation (proofing specimen)

To: Marco Bianchi, Acme Foods (external supplier) · From: you · Situation: an order confirmation, seconds from being sent. This scenario works differently: the facts box below is the truth, and the draft contains planted errors. Proof the draft against the facts; the full plant list is behind the peek.

The facts (this is the truth): recipient Marco Bianchi · 210 cases of Stacked Chips 12pk 300g · delivery Friday 17 July · price $14.50 per case, as agreed · PO number PO-88231.
The plant list (peek after your proof pass)

Seven plants, in three classes: Money sweep (facts vs draft): "Mario" → Marco · 120 → 210 cases · $14.05 → $14.50 · "Thursday 17 July" → Friday 17 July · PO-88213 → PO-88231. Real-word errors (spellcheck-proof — every word is a real word): "confirm out order" → our · "please except this email" → accept · "form our side" → from. Note the count: that's 8 findings across 7 sentences of a routine email — and not one of them draws a red squiggle.

Scenario I — The ambiguous instruction

To: your assistant · From: you · Situation: a quick internal ask, dashed off between meetings. Every vague term in it is a decision you've silently delegated. The facts box holds what you actually meant.

The facts (what you actually meant): the organic line samples from GreenField · 24 units · needed by Friday · pricing: the May quote, $6.40/unit, is fine · GreenField arranges the courier (they offered).
The five ambiguities (peek after your own attempt)

"the samples thing" — which line? (organic line) "soon" — when? (by Friday) "the usual amount" — how many? (24 units; there is no established "usual") "pricing similar to last time" — which number? (the May quote, $6.40/unit — "similar" invites a different one) "They said they'd sort the delivery" — who is "they", and sort what? (GreenField arranges the courier) Each vague term reads fine to you because you hold the missing fact. The assistant holds none of them — five guesses, five chances to be wrong at $6.40 a unit.
