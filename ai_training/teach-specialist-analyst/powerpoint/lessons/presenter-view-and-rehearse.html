PowerPoint to a High Standard · Lesson 0009

Presenter view & rehearse

Tied to your mission: you pull the sustainability metrics, category and compliance figures out of SAP extracts and shared workbooks, interpret them, and turn them into the summaries and charts your team and leadership act on — but the deck landing badly in the room undoes that work just as surely as a wrong formula would. Rehearsing with the instrument you'll actually perform with is the last mile: a deck rehearsed once in presenter view, with timings checked against the slot, before it ever meets a room.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario E — The 10am presentation — your range review, tomorrow, 15 minutes, then the deck goes to attendees and two people who weren't in the room.

The one idea

Rehearse with the instrument you'll perform with. Presenter view is a different machine from editing view — your notes appear as cues, not a scrollable document; a next-slide preview sits beside the current one; a timer runs whether you watch it or not. None of that exists when you're clicking through slides in the editor. The first time you see presenter view must not be 10am in the GDM room.

One rehearsal pass in presenter view does three things at once: it converts notes into cues (short lines you glance at, never paragraphs you read aloud), it surfaces the one slide you always overrun on, and it fits the deck to its slot — Rehearse Timings does that arithmetic for you, slide by slide and as a total.

Why it matters to a specialist analyst

The range review is a recurring 15-minute slot, and 15 minutes is not much room for surprise when the deck is carrying sustainability or compliance figures leadership will act on. A director who watches you read slide text aloud has stopped listening to the numbers and started wondering why you needed the deck to remember your own analysis. Two people on the invite won't be in the room at all — they get the deck afterwards, notes and all — so what you actually typed into the notes pane isn't backstage material, it's a document with an audience of its own, sitting alongside the figures you pulled from SAP or a shared workbook. Rehearsing in presenter view once, the day before, is cheaper than finding out live that slide 6 eats four of your fifteen minutes.

The bad habit this undoes Rehearsing by scrolling the deck in editing view — clicking through slides at a desk, no timer running, no next-slide preview, notes sitting in a pane you can read in full rather than glance at. That's a different instrument entirely. The first time the real presenter view shows up is live, at 10am, and the notes written for editing view turn out to be essays — so the fallback is reading the slide text out loud to the room, which is the exact tell that no real rehearsal happened.

How to do it

Open Scenario E's deck (reconstruct it from the pack's spec if you're not rehearsing a live file) and run the rehearsal solo, in presenter view, exactly as you will tomorrow.

Start the show in presenter view only — Slide Show > Present in Presenter View, or hold Alt+F5 to force presenter view onto your one screen even with nothing projected. Do the full pass end to end, without stopping to fix anything mid-run: watch the current slide, the next-slide preview beside it, and your notes rendered as the presenter-view pane actually shows them.

Wherever the notes pane shows a paragraph instead of a line you can take in at a glance, that slide has a note-essay problem. Pick the two worst offenders and rewrite each down to at most three short cue lines — a phrase per beat, not a sentence you'd read verbatim. A cue reminds you what to say; it does not say it for you.

Watch the timer as you go and notice where you talk longest — that's your overrun slide. Decide its fix on the spot: cut content that doesn't earn its time, split it into two slides so the pacing resets, or simply practise that one slide again until it fits. Then run Slide Show > Rehearse Timings for a second, timed pass — PowerPoint records a duration per slide and totals them when you end the show. Confirm the total lands inside the 15-minute slot with at least two minutes of margin; if it doesn't, go back to the overrun slide and cut, don't just talk faster next time.

Check yourself

Answer these before moving on — immediate feedback below each.

1. You've been rehearsing by scrolling through the deck in editing view. What does that rehearsal actually miss?

2. A note reads as three sentences of full prose in the presenter-view pane. What's the fix?

3. Rehearse Timings reports a total of 18 minutes against a 15-minute slot. What does the check require?

Try it for real

Take a deck of your own with a real slot attached and run the same pass: presenter view on, one full run-through, notes converted to cues wherever they read as essays, the overrun slide found and fixed. Self-check when you're done: run Rehearse Timings once more and confirm the recorded total is at or under your slot's length minus two minutes — if it isn't, you haven't found the real overrun slide yet.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — rehearse in presenter view, convert notes into cues, fit the deck to its slot — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: cues stay glanceable and never add content your notes didn't have, and the timing arithmetic must sum to slot-minus-margin.

Rung 2 · Aldi:GPT — it compresses your notes, it doesn't rehearse for you

Aldi:GPT can't see your deck or hear your rehearsal, so it can only work on notes you paste it; converting an essay into a cue is compression, not summarising to a different meaning, and you judge whether it kept your wording's substance.

Rung 3 · M365 Copilot — it summarises the deck, it doesn't rehearse it

Copilot in PowerPoint can read the deck that's open and draft a summary for the follow-up email — its honest present-share move — but it has no access to a room, a timer, or your delivery; it cannot rehearse anything for you.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — it builds the rehearsal pack, it can't rehearse for you

An agent with file access can extract your existing notes into a printable cue card and compute a timing sheet from the slide count — but it works from what's already in the deck; it cannot invent better notes or watch you present.

Check: the cue card must preserve your own wording exactly — compressing notes into glanceable cues is rung 2's job, done with your approval, not something this script gets to do unsupervised. Then check the timing sheet's arithmetic like any parts-to-whole total: the per-slide budgets must sum to exactly slot-minus-margin (13 minutes on a 15-minute slot with a 2-minute margin); if the running total lands anywhere else, the script's arithmetic is wrong, not your slot.

The point of the ladder From "compress my notes into cues" to "build the whole rehearsal pack" — the deliverable never changes: a rehearsal run in the instrument you'll actually perform with, cues you can glance at, a total that fits the slot with margin. What protects you at every rung is that same check, not the tool. Learn to rehearse in presenter view by hand first, so you can tell a real cue from a shorter essay and a correct timing sum from a plausible-looking one.
