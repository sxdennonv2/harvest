Excel to a High Standard · Lesson 0016

Charts: a clean column chart

Tied to your mission: a four-row summary of revenue by category is fine in a table, but a column chart lets a director see the shape of it in a heartbeat — who's biggest, who's lagging. The skill isn't clicking "Insert Chart"; it's making a chart that says one thing, cleanly.

Practice workbook · open this first

Open the course workbook and click the Chart tab — a tidy Category / Revenue (AUD) summary is ready. Your job is to turn it into a clean chart.

The one idea

A chart maps numbers to a picture. For comparing amounts across a handful of categories, a clustered column chart is the honest default: bars from a common zero baseline, read left to right. Select the summary, insert the column chart, then strip it back — a clear title, no clutter, light gridlines. A chart that shows one comparison well beats a busy one that shows five badly.

Why it matters to a specialist analyst

Your commentary lives next to a picture — a sustainability metric, a category total, a compliance figure pulled from SAP or a dashboard. If the picture is clean, the eye lands on the point you want made — Snacks dwarfs the rest — and your words simply confirm it, rather than re-explaining a cluttered mess. A default chart, or the wrong type (a pie for six near-equal slices), makes leadership work to find the point, and quietly erodes trust in the number behind it — especially when that number has to hold up across refresh cycles.

The bad habit this undoes Accepting Excel's default chart untouched — legend for a single series, a generic "Chart Title", heavy gridlines, maybe a 3-D pie — and shipping it. Or worse, screenshotting numbers instead of charting them. A minute of tidying turns a default into something a director reads at a glance.

How to do it

On the Chart tab, select A1:B5, then Insert → Column Chart → 2-D Clustered Column. Now clean it: click the title and type Revenue by category; with one data series you don't need a legend, so select it and press Delete; keep gridlines faint. Drag the chart clear of the data so it reads on its own.

Choose the right type: columns compare amounts, a line shows change over time, and a pie only earns its place with two or three parts of a whole. When in doubt, use columns.
