Word to a High Standard · Lesson 0010

Building blocks: the reusable clauses

Tied to your mission: new documents are supposed to be born from the template, not from last month's copy — that's the previous lesson. But a template only fixes the document's shape. The standing clauses that live inside that shape — the samples caveat, the delivery-terms clause, the doc-control block — still get typed from memory into every new submission unless you fix that separately. This lesson is that fix: Building Blocks, Word's version of an instrument you've already met.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and reread Scenario E — The new-doc job. It names the fix directly: standing clauses inserted as building blocks, "the same instrument Outlook taught" — this lesson is where you build them.

The one idea

If you've typed it twice, it's a building block. A clause retyped from memory is legally different every time it's typed — not because you're careless, but because memory doesn't preserve exact wording, and the differences are invisible until they matter: the day a supplier quotes their copy of your samples caveat against yours, and the two don't match. A Building Block (Word's umbrella term; the reusable-text flavour lives in the Quick Part gallery) is the standing clause saved once and inserted by name, identical every time, because it's the same saved block, not a memory of it.

This is the deliberate cross-app sibling of Outlook's Quick Parts lesson — the same one idea, in a different host. Outlook's Quick Parts save a paragraph you paste into mail; Word's Building Blocks save a clause you insert into a document, and they carry one extra power Outlook's don't: a Building Block can bind to the template itself, which is how a whole team shares one true version of a clause instead of thirty people's private recollections of it.

Why it matters to a specialist analyst

Your reports carry clauses and notes that are load-bearing, not decorative, in a different way to a contract but no less strict: a methodology note explaining how a sustainability metric is calculated, a data-source caveat on a POST/NBC figure, a doc-control footer stating which draft leadership is looking at. These have to be identical wherever they appear, because "identical" is what lets a reader — your director, a category team, an auditor months later — treat the note as a standing definition rather than wonder whether this month's phrasing quietly means something different from last month's. Typed "more or less the same" across a year of reporting cycles, none of the copies actually match — and the mismatch surfaces at the worst possible moment, when someone lines up two of your reports side by side and the methodology note doesn't say the same thing twice.

The bad habit this undoes Clause-from-memory: the data-source caveat or methodology note retyped "more or less the same" in report after report over a year, none of them word-for-word identical, and nobody notices until a reader compares two versions and the wording doesn't match. The other flavour of the same failure is the personal-stash variant — everyone on the team keeps their own slightly-different version of the same note, pasted from whichever old report they last had open, so "the caveat" quietly means three or four different things depending on who assembled the report.

How to do it

Build one Building Block from Scenario E's specimen, then prove it inserts identically into a fresh document.

Select the standing clause. In a submission built from the template, type or locate the samples caveat exactly as your organisation states it, for example: "Sample pricing shown above is indicative only and does not constitute a committed order price. Confirmed pricing will be issued at purchase order stage." Select the whole clause, paragraph mark included if it needs to carry its own formatting.

Save as a Building Block. Go to Insert → Quick Parts → Save Selection to Quick Part Gallery. Word opens a dialog asking three things, and each answer is a real decision, not a formality: a name you'll recognise at a glance ("Samples caveat", not "Block1"); a gallery (Quick Parts, for reusable text like this); and a location — save it to Building Blocks.dotx and it's yours alone on this machine, save it to the organisation's template and everyone who opens a document from that template gets the same block in their gallery. The location field is the sharing decision, not a technical detail — pick the template if the clause is meant to be the team's standing wording, not just yours.

Insert it by name. Open a fresh document from the template (never a Save-As on a predecessor — that's the lesson before this one). Click where the caveat belongs and either start typing its name and press F3 to expand it, or go to Insert → Quick Parts and pick it from the gallery. Repeat for the delivery-terms clause and the doc-control footer block, each saved and named the same way.

Run the drift drill. In a second, throwaway paragraph, retype the samples caveat from memory, without looking at the block. Now select both versions — the from-memory retype and the block-inserted one — and read them side by side, word for word. Count the differences. There will be at least one: a dropped clause, a swapped word, a missing sentence. That gap is the entire lesson. Delete the from-memory paragraph; only the block-inserted version belongs in a real document.

Check yourself

Answer these before moving on — immediate feedback below each.

1. What actually makes a retyped-from-memory clause a legal risk, rather than just an untidy habit?

2. When saving a clause to the Quick Part gallery, why does the "location" choice (Building Blocks.dotx vs. the organisation's template) matter?

3. You retype the samples caveat from memory next to the block-inserted version and compare them word for word. What does finding zero differences tell you, and what does finding even one tell you?

Try it for real

Pick one clause you know you retype across your own documents — a caveat, a standard scope line, a doc-control footer. Select it in a document where the wording is correct, save it as a Building Block with a name you'll actually remember, and choose its location deliberately: your personal Building Blocks.dotx if it's just yours, the organisation's template if it should be the team's standing wording. Then run the drift drill: retype the same clause from memory in a throwaway paragraph, compare it word-for-word against the block, and count the differences. Check: the block-inserted version must match your correct source exactly; if the drift drill turns up zero differences, retry it on a longer clause — a short one is too easy to get right from memory, and this lesson is about the clauses that aren't.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — turn a retyped-from-memory clause into a saved, named Building Block — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: does the clause's wording, once frozen as a block, stay exactly what it was meant to say, and can you name where the block lives and who else can use it?

Rung 2 · Aldi:GPT — it drafts the block library list, you supply the wording and save the blocks yourself

Aldi:GPT can't see your documents or your Quick Part gallery, so it can only work from clauses you describe or paste in. Its job is organising them into a library list with names and a maintenance rule; your job is supplying the real wording verbatim and doing the saving in Word yourself.

Type this prompt into Aldi:GPT

Check: the signature failure at this rung is aspirational libraries and reworded clauses — a list of blocks you'll never actually maintain, or the model quietly polishing your caveat's wording into something that reads better but isn't the wording you gave it. Changing the words is changing the one thing a block exists to keep identical. Read its output against what you pasted in; if a single clause has been reworded, reject that part and reuse your original text.

Rung 3 · M365 Copilot in Word — honest boundary: it drafts a clause, it cannot create the Building Block

Be honest about the boundary: Copilot in Word cannot save a Building Block or a Quick Part — there is no button it can press for Insert → Quick Parts → Save Selection. Its real move here is drafting or tightening a clause's text before you freeze it as a block; after that, it's a different job entirely, one it also can't do invisibly.

Type this prompt into Copilot (in Word)

Check: the working line must survive the polish — this caveat exists to say sample pricing isn't a committed order price, and a smoother version that drops or softens that line is a worse clause however tidy the prose. And once you've frozen the wording and saved it as a Building Block, any later Copilot rewrite of block-inserted text is an edit to this document only — it does not reach back and change the saved block. Know which one you just changed before you move on.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — it builds the clause library kit, it cannot install anything into Word

An agent with file access can build the whole clause-library kit around your standing text: the canonical wording as files, a manifest mapping each clause to its gallery and template location, and a checker that scans real documents for near-miss variants — but it cannot open Word and save a Building Block for you; every save still happens by hand, from the frozen text the kit ships.

Type this prompt into GitHub Copilot

Check: run the checker against the thirty-documents scenario — real from-memory retypes with small variations — and confirm it actually finds the drifted ones. Then run it against documents where the clauses were inserted as blocks: it should go quiet, flagging nothing, because an exact block insert has zero edit distance from the canonical text. A checker that flags legitimate, unrelated contextual sentences as "drifted clauses" cries wolf — tighten its distance threshold before you trust its output on a real submission.

The point of the ladder From "AI organises the library list" to "AI builds the drift-checking kit" — the deliverable never changes: one canonical wording per clause, saved once, inserted by name, identical every time. What protects you at every rung is the same check — does the frozen wording still say what it's meant to say, and can you name where it lives and who else can reach it. Learn to save and insert a block by hand first, so a generated library or checker can be judged against a standard you already hold yourself.
