The mailbox pack

Scenarios for practising Outlook mechanics. Some run in your own mailbox (search, rules); others supply the material here (an inbox to triage, a thread to route, an invite to fix). Suppliers are fictional and shared with the course family. Each scenario states what a correct result must contain — that's the check at every rung.

Scenario B — The lost attachment (search)

Practised in your own mailbox — the targets below are described, not supplied. Build the operator query before you look.

Three retrieval jobs, in rising difficulty:

1 · A price list attachment from a named supplier contact, received roughly three months ago — you remember the sender, not the subject.
2 · The message where a colleague asked you a question you never answered — you remember one distinctive word from it.
3 · Everything still flagged from before last month — the follow-ups that quietly aged out.

A correct result: each job solved by a written operator query (e.g. from: hasattachments:yes received: ranges, flagged) — not by scrolling; each query returns a candidate set small enough to scan in one screen (<20 results); you can say which operator did the narrowing.

Scenario C — The promo thread (threading & routing)

The four-message thread below has reached Wednesday evening. Decide the mechanics of your next send: reply, reply-all, or forward — and who belongs on To vs CC.

A correct routing: the confirmation to Sam is a reply on the supplier thread (To: Sam; CC: Alex — he owns the DC condition) — never reply-all across the internal/external seam, because Alex's internal capacity discussion must not travel to Harvest Lane · the Popcorn question goes to whoever owns the promo list on a separate thread (its own subject), not buried here · nothing internal is forwarded to Sam without pruning the trail.

Scenario D — The agenda-less invite (scheduling)

This invite went out for a meeting that matters. Predict its fate, then rebuild it.

A correct rebuild: subject names the decision ("Promo fortnight: confirm volumes & delivery day") · body carries a numbered agenda (confirm 110/wk; Monday→Wednesday deliveries; Popcorn in/out) · each item names its owner · prep stated ("bring DC capacity sheet") · duration matches the agenda (30 min, not 60) · outcome named ("we leave with the Friday confirmation sendable").

Scenario E — The Friday ritual (automation)

Every Friday, the same six minutes: the weekly sell-through report arrives from Finance; you file it to the reporting folder, flag it for Monday's read-through, and send the same two-line acknowledgement ("Received, will review Monday — flag anything unusual now"). Fifty-two times a year.

A correct automation: one Quick Step chaining all three actions (move to folder + flag for Monday + reply from template), triggered by one click on the arrived mail · it is a Quick Step and not a rule, and the learner can say why (the acknowledgement should go only after a human glanced at the report — rules fire unattended) · the reply template keeps the "flag anything unusual now" ask.

Scenario F — The naked send (attachments & mechanics)

You're about to send the volume confirmation to Sam Lee (external). Alex Moreau (internal) should see it land. The draft below has three mechanical problems; sending it as-is causes three follow-up emails.

A correct send: the sheet actually attached, with a filename the receiver can file (not "Book1 (3).xlsx") · the split pasted as a real table or text, never a screenshot (Sam can't copy numbers out of a picture) · @mentions are an internal instrument — Alex goes on CC (he owns the DC condition); an @mention in an external mail is just decoration the external reader can't act on · attachment named in the body matches the attachment on the mail.

Scenario G — The meeting output (recap → tracked follow-ups)

Raw notes from Thursday's promo meeting. Present: you, Alex Moreau (Supply Chain), Dana Whitfield (Merch). The meeting is over; the question is where its outputs now live in Outlook.

A correct capture: every action lives where it will fire, not in the notes — yours (Sam confirmation Fri AM; tell Alex today) as flagged items/tasks with dates · others' (Dana's Tuesday proof, Alex's Monday booking) tracked by a follow-up you can chase, not by memory · the parked item lands on next month's meeting invite, not in a note · decisions (110/wk, Wednesday, Popcorn out) travel to the people whose sheets they change. Writing the minutes document is the communication course's job — this is about where actions live.

Scenario H — The absence brief (out-of-office)

You're out Monday 20 – Friday 24 July, back Monday 27. Coverage agreed: Alex Moreau for DC/logistics questions, Priya Shah for supplier commercials; genuinely urgent items escalate to your director. Build the out-of-office from these facts.

A correct out-of-office: dates exact, including the back-on date · who covers what, by name, so the sender self-routes · no "checking emails occasionally" (a promise that makes coverage pointless and you half-on-leave) · two versions: internal (names, direct routes) and external (no org chart — one contact, no internal detail) · set before you leave, expiry set so it turns itself off.
Make it yours. These are fictional. Describe your real Monday inbox or your real Friday ritual and the lessons retarget to your actual folders, suppliers and rituals.
