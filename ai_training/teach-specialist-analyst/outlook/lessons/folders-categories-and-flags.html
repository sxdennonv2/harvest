Outlook to a High Standard · Lesson 0001

Folders, categories & flags: one home per mail

Tied to your mission: you want Outlook mastered properly — not the patchy, self-taught version you inherited — and that starts with a triaged inbox that protects your analysis time. Nowhere does that demand live more than in an inbox nobody has triaged in months. This lesson is the floor everything else in this course stands on: before you can search fast, route data-conversation threads cleanly, or automate the repetitive, every mail that lands needs a decided fate. (The words you'd put in a reply are a different course — see the communication pipeline; this one is only about where the mail lives.)

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario A — The Monday inbox — twelve messages waiting for a decision, with the correct triage given underneath so you can mark your own work.

The one idea

On first touch, every mail gets exactly one of three fates. A folder is its home — where it lives once you're done with it. A category is a label that can cross homes — "Q3 Promo" might tag mail sitting in three different supplier folders. A flag is neither: it's a promise, and it only belongs on a mail if you personally owe a dated follow-up because of it.

Say that last part twice, because it's the one people skip: flags are promises, not bookmarks. An inbox is a loading dock — mail arrives, gets sorted to its fate, and moves on. It is not a to-do list, and the moment you start using it as one, the flag column stops meaning anything.

Why it matters to a specialist analyst

Your week runs on figures pulled from systems and reports — SAP extracts, published dashboards, shared workbooks — that you interpret and turn into the summaries, statistics and charts your team and leadership act on. That work needs blocks of uninterrupted attention, and a Monday inbox full of unsorted supplier price lists, data-correction chasers and compliance notices is the single biggest threat to getting one. Without a home per topic, a genuine data discrepancy you need to chase looks identical to a newsletter you'll never read. The analysts who protect their time aren't triaging faster; they've made the sorting decision automatic, so the only thing left in the inbox by Friday afternoon is what still needs a decision — and every figure that ends up in a report can be traced back to a mail that's still findable.

The bad habit this undoes Leaving hundreds of read mails sitting in the inbox "so I don't forget" — which means nothing is forgettable, so nothing stands out. Flagging everything that feels even slightly important, until the flag column is as long as the inbox and tells you nothing. And one giant "Suppliers" folder that swallows every vendor into a single unsearchable pile instead of one folder per supplier.

How to do it

Work Scenario A's twelve mails one at a time and give each a fate. First ask: does it need a home? Mail #1 (Harvest Lane's Q3 price list) and #2 (Acme's delivery confirmation) each go to that supplier's own folder — not a shared "Suppliers" folder, a named one per supplier, because you will search by sender later and a shared dump defeats that. Mail #3 (the IT outage notice) and #6 (the newsletter) are read-once, dated or disposable — file them or delete them, but they don't sit in the inbox once read.

Next ask: does a category help you find this later across homes? A "Promo" category on #10 (Sam Lee chasing promo volumes) means that when the promo fortnight is live, you can pull every related mail regardless of which supplier folder it's filed in.

Last, and strictest: does this mail leave you owing a dated follow-up? Not "is this important" — owing, with a date, by you. Mail #4 (Kim Tan's samples update — you owe QA a heads-up) gets a flag due before Thursday. Mail #7 (Alex Moreau's capacity question) gets a flag due Tuesday. Mail #10 (Sam Lee's chase) gets a flag due Friday. Mail #12 (Finance's month-end ask) is really a calendar task, so a flag is acceptable there too. That's four flags out of twelve mails — everything else, including #11's speculative pitch with no date, gets a home and nothing more.

Check yourself

Answer these before moving on — immediate feedback below each.

1. A supplier confirms Friday's delivery in a reply — no question back to you, nothing you owe. What's its fate?

2. What turns a folder into "the bad habit this undoes" rather than good triage?

3. A speculative pitch from a supplier with no date and nothing you owe. Correct fate?

Try it for real

Take your own inbox's top twelve mails and run the same three-fates pass: home, category, flag-only-if-owed. Self-check when you're done: open your flagged list and for every single flag, say out loud what you owe and by what date. If you can't name a date, it isn't a flag — take it off. If nothing is left sitting unfiled in the inbox that already has an obvious home, you've passed.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — decide folder, category, or owed-flag for real mail — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: every flag left standing must be a promise you can name with a date.

Rung 3 · Microsoft 365 Copilot — it reads your mailbox, it can't triage it

Copilot in Outlook can read and summarise what's actually in your mailbox today, but be honest about the boundary: it cannot create folders, rules, or flags for you — you still do the filing by hand.

Type this prompt into Copilot (in Outlook)

Check: Copilot can surface asks, but it doesn't know which ones you personally owe — it can't tell an FYI from a promise. Run its list back through the three-fates test yourself: for each item it flags as needing action, confirm there's a real date and that you're the one who owes it, before you turn any of them into an actual Outlook flag.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — it builds the triage kit, it can't touch your mailbox

An agent with file access can build you a ready-to-use triage kit from a description of your mail — but it cannot configure your mailbox; you still import or apply the rules yourself in Outlook.

Type this prompt into GitHub Copilot

Check: read the kit against your own description before you apply anything — it must not invent a folder for a mail type you never mentioned, and every folder it proposes must ship with a matching rule in the setup card. A folder with no rule beside it means the kit is incomplete; send it back.

The point of the ladder From "AI suggests the scheme" to "AI drafts the whole kit" — the deliverable never changes: one decided fate per mail, and every surviving flag a promise you can name. What protects you at every rung is that same check, not the tool. Learn to triage by hand first, so you can tell a good scheme from a 40-folder mess and a real owed follow-up from an AI's best guess.
