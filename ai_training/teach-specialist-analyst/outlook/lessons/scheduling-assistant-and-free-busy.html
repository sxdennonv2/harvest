Outlook to a High Standard · Lesson 0009

Scheduling Assistant & free/busy: find the slot without the email volley

Tied to your mission: the survey's coordination demand keeps showing up as "just find a time that works" — and the instinct it triggers is an email. This lesson replaces that instinct. Outlook already holds the answer to "when suits everyone?" as data, not as a question to ask five people one at a time. Builds on Invites that get accepted — that lesson got the invite's contract right; this one is about placing that contract on a time that actually holds.

Practice pack · open this first

Open the practice pack and find Scenario D — The agenda-less invite again — the rebuilt 30-minute invite from that scenario is the meeting you're now scheduling.

The one idea

"When suits everyone?" is not a question for email — free/busy is shared data, and the Scheduling Assistant reads it. Open the invite, switch to the Scheduling Assistant tab, and read the attendee rows: each internal attendee's calendar renders as a strip of free, busy, tentative and out-of-office blocks, already respecting their working hours and time zone. You place the meeting where the rows are clear — you don't ask and wait to find out.

When no common slot exists, the Assistant doesn't just fail quietly — it shows you why, block by block. The escalation from there follows a ladder in order: shorten the meeting first (a 30-minute ask clears rows a 60-minute one can't), then demote non-owners to optional (their busy block stops blocking a slot they didn't need to attend anyway), and only once both of those are exhausted do you propose alternative times to the room. Reach for "propose alternatives" first and you've skipped the two moves that usually make it unnecessary.

Why it matters to a specialist analyst

Supplier meetings like this one carry a reporting deadline behind them — Scenario D's meeting exists because Friday is the last chance to confirm a volume figure before it has to go into a cut you're building for the director. Every day spent collecting "does Thursday work?" replies one at a time is a day taken straight out of the block you'd otherwise spend pulling and checking the figures themselves, not chasing a room. A specialist analyst who reads the Scheduling Assistant places the meeting in minutes and gets the saved time back for the analysis; one who emails around for availability is still waiting on Alex's reply while the reporting deadline gets closer and the actual numbers work hasn't started.

The bad habit this undoes The availability volley: five emails of "does Thursday work? how about Tuesday morning?", collecting stale, one-at-a-time answers for data Outlook already holds and shows you in one screen. Its quieter sibling is the default-60-minutes hold — squatting on three calendars for an hour because that's what the new-invite dialog opens with, when the agenda underneath only needs 30. Neither failure is loud: the volley just costs a day, and the over-length hold just costs everyone the last half-hour of a meeting that finished its agenda early.

How to do it

Take Scenario D's rebuilt invite — the 30-minute "Promo fortnight: confirm volumes & delivery day" meeting with you, Alex Moreau and Sam Lee (Harvest Lane, an external contact) — and schedule it without a single "are you free" email.

Open the invite and switch to the Scheduling Assistant tab. Your row and Alex's row are both internal, so both render as real free/busy strips — read them directly rather than asking either of you by email. Sam's row is different: external free/busy is usually invisible to you, because it lives on a calendar Outlook doesn't have permission to read. That invisibility changes the move, it doesn't stop it — you still read the internal rows off the Assistant to find where you and Alex are both clear, but for Sam you offer two concrete options drawn from those clear internal slots rather than asking an open-ended "when are you free?".

With the internal rows in view, use AutoPick to jump to the next slot where every required attendee is free — this is where required-versus-optional earns its keep again: it follows item ownership, exactly as it did when you built the invite. Alex owns the delivery-day condition, so he's required and his row blocks the search; if anyone on the invite isn't tied to an owned agenda item, mark them optional so their busy blocks stop narrowing the search for a slot they don't need to attend. Once AutoPick lands on a 30-minute window where the required rows are clear, that's your first option for Sam; pick a second clear window on a different day as the alternative, and send those two — not an open question.

If AutoPick can't find a clear 30-minute window at all, the Assistant tells you why — the conflict blocks are visible in the grid, not hidden behind a "no slot found" message. Work the ladder in order: first confirm the meeting really only needs 30 minutes (it does — the agenda is three owned items), then check whether everyone marked required actually owns an item (demote anyone who doesn't), and only after both of those are true do you widen the search and propose alternative days to the room.

Try it for real

Take your next real multi-person meeting and schedule it entirely through the Scheduling Assistant — zero "are you free" emails sent to any internal attendee. Self-check when you're done: can you name, out loud, what the Assistant showed you that an email volley would have taken a day to collect (whose calendar was actually clear, and when)? If you can name it, you used the tool correctly. If you sent even one availability email to an internal attendee to confirm what the Assistant already showed, that email was the volley creeping back in.

The AI moves — climbing the ladder

Same task — place the meeting where the free/busy data says it fits — with more handed to AI at each rung. The check never changes: every slot offered has to trace back to a real free/busy fact, never an invented one.

Rung 2 · Aldi:GPT — it drafts the proposal text, you supply every fact it can't see

Aldi:GPT can't see any calendar, internal or external, so you read the Scheduling Assistant yourself first and hand it the full constraint set; it drafts the two-option proposal text for Sam, and you check nothing in the draft goes beyond what you gave it.

Check: every slot the draft offers Sam must exist in the constraint set you gave it — Tuesday 10:00-10:30 or Wednesday 14:00-14:30, nothing else. The signature failure at this rung is invented availability: a draft that adds "or Friday afternoon, if that's easier?" when nobody's calendar was checked for Friday. If you see a slot you didn't supply, it's a guess wearing the shape of a fact — cut it before sending.

Rung 3 · Microsoft 365 Copilot — it schedules from context, you check the terms

Copilot in Outlook can read the invite and the named attendees and set up the meeting directly — but it doesn't know the agenda only needs 30 minutes or which attendee owns which line; those terms are yours to check, not to trust to its defaults.

Type this prompt into Copilot (in Outlook)

Check: verify the scheduled duration is actually 30 minutes — Copilot defaults to a 60-minute hold if the prompt doesn't pin the length down hard enough, and the agenda only needs 30. Confirm required-versus-optional still follows item ownership rather than Copilot marking everyone required by default, and confirm the slot it picked respects working hours for every attendee. The calendar data Copilot reads is correct; the contract terms — duration, who's required — are yours to enforce, not its to assume.

Rung 4 · GitHub Copilot — it builds a slot-finder, it can't decide which conflict to drop

An agent with file access can build a slot-finder from exported .ics calendars, but it has no live calendar connection and no authority to resolve a conflict on its own — it can only report what the data shows and flag what it can't reconcile.

Type this prompt into GitHub Copilot

Check: reconcile the script's reported windows against the source .ics files by hand for one day — open that day's calendars directly and confirm the windows it found are genuinely free for everyone. A finder that "resolves" a conflict by quietly dropping an attendee's busy block has decided something a tool must not decide; it should flag the conflict and stop, not choose who gets overridden.

The point of the ladder From "AI drafts the proposal text you paste in" to "AI builds a slot-finder from exported calendars" — the deliverable never changes: a meeting placed on a slot that traces back to a real free/busy fact, at the length the agenda actually needs. What protects you at every rung is that same trace-to-a-fact check, not the tool. Learn to read the Scheduling Assistant by hand first, so you can catch the invented slot an AI adds back in and the conflict it's tempted to resolve on your behalf.
