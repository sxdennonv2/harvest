# Harvest

*Why this pattern exists. The glossary is [CONTEXT.md](CONTEXT.md); the estate
register is [DIMENSIONS.md](DIMENSIONS.md); this is the argument they sit on.*

## The problem

Harvest began as a study in how to use AI to increase productivity —
specifically, the break-out move from *agents assisting you* to do the work,
to *you assisting agents* to do it. While the agent assists you, your
attention is the ceiling — and you have to be sitting at the keyboard: every
unit of output passes through your hands. To invert the roles, something new
is required of you first: the work must be articulated up front — the outline
of an application as a list of objects, entities, tasks, and relationships —
so that agents can iterate over that structure and produce the output each
atomistic task calls for, in parallel, without you inside each one.

## What Harvest is

Many tasks — perhaps most — can be abstracted into a few dimensions,
orthogonal or correlated, each having a few unique values. Factorially
combined, these expand into a space of coordinates, each of which defines an
atomistic task for an agent to implement. The space, together with the
machinery that works it, is a **pipeline**: a sequence of composable,
atomistic skills in which judgement is spent once — captured as a frame (the
dimensions and their values) and a contract (what a good unit is) — and
re-spent by agents across every coordinate. The pipeline performs the
meaningful, useful, monetisable task; each coordinate yields one unit of its
produce.

## The boundary

Use Harvest when you want to spawn instances at scale; don't use it when you
only want to do something once or twice. The working rule is the rule of
three: the first time, just do the work; the second time, notice; the third
time, promote — by then the instances have revealed the dimensions, and the
frame is born from real, validated artifacts instead of guesswork. Repetition
is necessary but not sufficient: if "good" can't be written down as a
contract, the judgement won't delegate without decay; and if a unit's
feedback can never find its way back to the coordinate that made it, the
pipeline can't learn. One question decides it — will one written standard
govern every unit? If every piece needs its own spec, run a chain and be
done. If one contract covers them all, build the frame.

## Why the output can be trusted

The beating heart of every unit the pipeline creates is its **contract**: the
written anatomy of what is acceptable and what is not. A unit ships only
through a **gate** that checks it against that contract — deterministic
checks first, judged review second, human sign-off where liability demands it
— and the gate is sized to the stakes: cheap and automated for a daily post,
a human signature on a priced risk assessment. Continuous improvement is
built into the pipeline itself: every unit carries its coordinates into the
world, so whatever comes back — engagement, redlines, a client's override —
finds its way back to the exact frame cell, contract clause, or skill that
produced it, and is folded in. The next unit is born from better machinery
than the last. Nothing improves by memory; everything improves by commit.

## The estate

A Harvest pipeline can be built for any process or project that reduces to a
few dimensions with unique values — and because machinery is itself produce
to the line that makes it, pipelines can be produced as well as producing:
social media posts across many channels; governance documents across many
organisations, in one industry or spanning several; online education across
subjects, topics, and levels of ability. None of this is hypothetical — those
three illustrations are the estate: an AI-training pipeline, **built**, 223
lessons standing; supported-independent-living operations, **building**, meal
plans first of three lines; risk governance, **sketched but already
monetised**, delivered by hand while its containers wait; punditry,
**sketched**, ten channels planned. The lines run at two speeds, and the
speeds trade: fast public lines are the laboratory — cheap feedback, weekly
learning; slow priced lines are the register — human sign-off, real money.
Liquid lines learn; illiquid lines earn; what moves between them is pattern
knowledge, never content.
